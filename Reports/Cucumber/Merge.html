VI. Demand for the Championship

To understand the possible impact any changes to the championship structure may have on demand it is necessary to first understand the current demand for the championship.

A. Current Demand, Division I Championship Wrestling

The current format of the championship utilized for NCAA Division I wrestling competition has been successful financially because of strong attendance. And it is due to this success that there has been a concern about any changes having a negative impact that would diminish the fan experience and result in a decline in attendance at the championships (Byers, 2012).

This may be a valid concern. It's vital that we review and analyze the empirical evidence to best understand if the demand for the wrestling championship is so precarious that any changes would no longer make the championship viable.

    1. NCAA Division I Wrestling Championship Sell Outs

    The NCAA Wrestling Championship experienced ten (10) sequential years of sold-out events, prior to the cancelation of the 2020 championship due to the Covid-19 pandemic and subsequent restriction on fan attendance for the 2021 championship (SportsMinneapolis.org, 2020).

    2. NCAA Division I Wrestling Championships Ticket Allocation

    While the reported attendance figures for the championship event have been strong, simply looking at the attendance numbers doesn't give a lot of information about the event's spectator demand and thus warrants a deeper dive into how the tickets are allocated for the event.

    The Wells Fargo Arena in Des Moines, Iowa, host for the 2013 NCAA Wrestling Championship, had the lowest capacity of all venues to host the event in the 10 years prior to Covid-19. The venue was close to the minimum seating capacity required by the bid standards (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2019a).

    To better understand the different groups that are in attendance, we will use this championship as a model due to the venue's capacity aligning nearly perfectly with the minimum bid standards for venue capacity. According to the NCAA's Bid Documents for the Wrestling Championship, the minimum seat allocation by group shall be as follows:

    Group Minimum Seat Allocation (15,500) Percent of Total
    Teams (Division I Wrestling Programs) 10,000 64.52%
    General Public 2,000 12.9%
    Host/Local Organizing Committee 1,000 6.45%
    National Wrestling Coaches Association 1,000 6.45%
    NCAA 1,000 6.45%
    Participants (Student-Athletes/Support Staff) 500 3.23%

      Source: (NCAA Bid Selection Process)

    This means that the minimum capacity for a session at the championship is 15,500 seats. However, only 2,000 of these seats are allocated for the public at large. The majority of all tickets (roughly 70%) are reserved for Division I wrestling programs for their fans, administration, as well as the participating athletes and their family members. This allocation process is designed to ensure the key stakeholders of individual Division I programs have access to tickets for the championship.

    As a result of this process, most attendees at the championship are former collegiate wrestlers or coaches, who have attended the championship in prior years (Palmer, 2015). This system benefits programs by allowing them to ration access to tickets to key members of their program, including donors (Wyoming Athletics, 2010). But this allocation system also makes it harder for individuals without a connection to a Division I program to obtain a ticket to the championship. This results in many would be fans getting shut out from experiencing the championship in person.

    The seats designated for general admission (13% of tickets) are intended to be a pool of tickets made available to individuals without a connection to any specific program. However, due to the high demand for these tickets, they often go to regular championship attendees, making it difficult for first-time attendees to access them. With tickets sold in six-session packages, requiring a commitment of a minimum of three days, including two workdays, to utilize the tickets, many people interested in experiencing the competition when it comes to their community are reluctant to make such a major investment for their first championship experience.

    While the allocation process has its benefits, it also makes it difficult for individuals without a connection to a Division I program to attend the NCAA Wrestling Championship, even when the event is hosted within their home community. Championship attendance may be more indicative of an attendee's status within the wrestling community than anything else.

    3. Demand for Attendance

    The demand for NCAA Championship tickets has long been a topic of debate and speculation, with questions about whether there is excess demand and whether there are significant numbers of individuals who would buy single session tickets but not commit to buying all six sessions. In 2020, a change in venue for the NCAA Wrestling Championship provided an opportunity for a natural experiment to measure demand. The championship was to be held at U.S. Bank Stadium in Minneapolis, which had a much larger seating capacity than previous venues and also allowed for the sale of individual session tickets. At the time of its cancellation due to the COVID-19 pandemic, 40,000 all-session tickets had been sold (Carlson, 2020; Goodwin, 2020), which was a significant figure considering that the only in-state Division I program and host of the championship, the University of Minnesota, had averaged 2,955 fans per home competition, with a peak attendance of 4,025 in the same season (Diehl, Steen, & Bryant, 2022).

    This natural experiment demonstrates that there is significant demand from individuals to be able to watch Division I wrestling championships far beyond the current championship capacity and that the relative market performance for regular season demand is not indicative of NCAA Championship competition demand.

    Analysis of Demand

    Despite concerns that there may not be enough support to sustain both individual and team championships in NCAA Division I wrestling, There is strong demand for NCAA Division I Championship Wrestling and will be enough excitement and support from consumers to support a separate team and individual championship.

    The number of unique viewers of these events is not limited to a core group of supporters who must attend both the individual and team championships for the events to be viable. Should a vacancy arise at the individual championships due to a regular attendee choosing to attend the team championship instead, it is likely that a new spectator will fill the vacancy, increasing the number of unique individuals who get to experience championship competition within a season.

    2. Consumption Comparisons: Regular Season to Postseason

    There is concern that an increase in the frequency of rematches in sports with fragmented consumption, such as wrestling, could result in diminished consumer demand. To examine how a championship rematch in a sport with comparable consumer demand to wrestling was affected by rematches, we can look at the 2022 NCAA Women's Volleyball Championship when Nebraska and Wisconsin met in the Championship game after playing twice during the regular season.

    Despite taking place at a neutral site out of state for both programs, the third meeting between these two programs at the NCAA Championship had higher attendance and television viewership than the first two meetings. It's possible that the demand for the championship event as a celebration of collegiate volleyball to conclude the season leads to strong attendance, regardless of the matchups. However, coaches and athletic administrators have also suggested that demand for team championship events increases because the audience expands beyond the sport's loyal followers to include alumni, people with ties to the university, and those in the community where the championship is held who want to attend a niche event.

    Due to wrestling's change in competition format from the regular season to the postseason, it's not possible to compare the differences between regular season and postseason for team dual competitions. However, we can compare the television viewership metrics of the individual championships for the Big Ten, Big 12, and the NCAA to the team competitions during the regular season.

    We have observed an increase in viewership for NCAA competitions compared to regular season viewership. However, the Big Ten and Big 12 Championships did not experience a rise in viewership compared to regular season dual team competitions, despite having more teams represented and presumably featuring a better product with more highly rated athletes competing in the conference finals than in the regular season team dual competitions. If viewers were motivated to watch individual athlete competitions, the ratings for the conference championships would be expected to be higher than regular season team dual competitions. However, this is not the case. One-on-one team dual competitions outperformed all non-NCAA intercollegiate wrestling individual championship television ratings. Even the increase in viewership for the NCAA Championships from the regular season competitions is not as significant as expected, given the comparisons to women's volleyball.

    Increases in meetings between programs, especially over an extended period, help to foster intense rivalries which leads to a greater interest in viewership (Tyler & Cobbs, 2017) that will further attract spectators to the sport (Havard et al., 2016). As a result, having rematches of regular season contests at the championship post season will not diminish the interest in the contests but will increase the interest in the postseason meetings.

C. Impact of Changes to Consumer Demand for Division I Wrestling

Determining the effect of sport restructuring on wrestling consumer demand is a crucial inquiry that requires careful analysis. Given the significance of this question, we must thoroughly examine the implications of the changes to determine the expected impact on consumer demand.

    1. Terminal Championships

    Terminal championships are the championships that conclude the season for a sport and define the success of the season. Unlike regular season event championships or conference championships, terminal championships are the final championships of the season.

    The extensive research conducted by Daniel Dayan and Elihu Katz (1992) referred to the phenomenon in which people tune in to watch these terminal sports championships as "television of occasion." These are "events which are final and definitive." Though they may resemble other more common competitions in every other way they are much more significant in the eyes of the viewers as historic moments for they convey titles of significance that magnify the importance of the competition that is understood by a much wider audience.

    For this reason, it is nearly impossible to derive anticipated demand for a championship from other competitions, such as regular season competitions or even conference championships. Just as observed within the context of women's volleyball where the regular season meetings between Wisconsin and Nebraska were not indicative of the demand to watch their championship match, we cannot look at current regular season competition demand and conclude that the demand will remain static when placed in the context of the NCAA's terminal championship.

    The nature of terminal championships dictates that there is a reasonable expectation for consumer interest and demand to increase whenever the contest is a terminal championship event. This is especially true when the terminal event is a recognizable brand and honor such as the NCAA championship.

    2. Nation Branding

    In the realm of sports, researchers have discovered that spectators are drawn to contests where athletes share their national identity (Hu & Tang, 2010). In fact, Allison (2000) contends that national identity is the most marketable product in sports, a fact that is underscored by the overwhelming popularity of events like the World Cup and the Olympics.

    While national identity is often thought about within the structure of a nation-state, a nation does not need to exist within a political or legal structure. Anderson's (1991) theory of an "imagined community" proposes that a nation is forged through shared experiences and is a socially constructed unit based on shared cultural or historical criteria that exist outside of legal or political structures. Flint's (2016) definition of a nation as a cohesive and coherent unit based on shared cultural or historical criteria similarly emphasizes the socially constructed nature of nations.

    Colleges and universities endeavor to cultivate their brands and cultivate a sense of identity among their institutional communities. Sports are an effective tool used to brand institutions and develop their community (Shulman & Bowen, 2001). According to Kevin Knowlton, the Athletic Director at the University of California, Berkeley, integrating athletics into the campus is pivotal in "building affinity and really building community" (Kane, 2018). Similarly, Scott Barnes, Athletic Director at Oregon State University, asserts that athletics function as the university's front porch, being the most visible aspect of the institution (Longman, 2009).

    By encouraging individuals to identify primarily within the context of their school's community, colleges and universities foster a lifelong attachment to the institution which creates a nation, where returning to campus is viewed as a pilgrimage and homecoming (Piccini & De Lisle, 2019).

    Many institutions now brand their alumni and supporters as members of their nation with their campus being their geographic homeland.

    Sampling of images on official .edu websites of schools calling their alumni and/or fans a nation.

    With it's individual-centric postseason, intercollegiate wrestling is missing out on a powerful consumer base composed of these institutional nations and the communities they create. When schools participate in team competition, especially postseason competition, it draws in individuals connected to the participating schools, making them consumers of the sport as they support their specific schools in the context of championships (Trail et al., 2003). These communities of supporters and rivals increase demand for championship team competitions by allowing the consumers to feel vested in the competition without requiring the consumer to know the individual competitors (Kim et al., 2008), even when they are not fans of the sport (Branscombe & Wann, 1991). Team identity is the single greatest predictor of a consumer's intent to attend an event (Kim & Trail, 2010).

    While fandom is dynamic and can change at any time (Tamir, 2020), team identity remains relatively constant within intercollegiate sports, regardless of team success (Wann & Schrader, 1996). This is because an individual's identity within an institutional community extends beyond the context of a specific sport or one team's performance. The more individuals share, such as being an alumna of a school and enjoy watching students from the school compete in other sports, the stronger the community bond becomes (Fischer, 1982; McPherson et al., 2001). This allows members of the nation to view the successes of the teams representing the nation as their own successes which increases affinity for the nation (Bernache-Assollant et al., 2016).

    Adopting a team-centric championship model creates greater accessibility to institution loyalists to share in the experience of team successes and identity (Dietz-Uhler & Murrell, 1999). By utilizing the institutional affinity of these nations, we can enhance support for constituent wrestling programs (Adler & Adler, 1988). This, in turn, will reduce the dependence on transient wrestling loyalists as the sole consumer base, who may have little allegiance to individual programs.

    Traditionally, alumni associations have been hesitant to promote events that require a three-day commitment to watch only a handful of competitors from the institution. However, with a team competition featuring the institution, alumni associations are much more likely to market to their base, increasing support and affinity for their institution and by extension the sport of wrestling. This is especially true when the event is available to be viewed on television which provides for more passive engagement of the sport making the sport more accessible to consumers without requiring a major investment or commitment (Sedky, Kortam, & AbouAish, 2022).

    Institutional alumni associations can now market championship events to their alumni base, not just wrestling supporters, as the new format makes it more accessible and attractive to tune in and support their institutions (Bernthal & Graham, 2003).

    Understanding the power of the institutional brands and their nations of supporters, many events are created specifically for the purpose of leveraging these connections to drive interest in programs. "The Big Chill at the Big House", the name given to the Michigan vs Michigan State men's ice hockey game played at Michigan Stadium, the home of the football program, is one such example (Arnold, 2010). Though the Michigan men's ice hockey program averaged 6,649 in attendance per home contest the season prior (Michigan Athletics, n.d. -b), the "Big Chill" attracted a crowd of 113,411. Drawing on the academic and athletic rivalries developed between the two institutions through other sports (Wenger & Brown, 2014). This crowd, as noted by Steve Shoup (2010), was not primarily Michigan ice hockey fans, or even ice hockey fans in general, but rather a predominant mix of Michigan football fans and people simply wanting to experience being a part of a major event.

    With large groups of alumni and supporters spread out around the country, support for institutions and their teams is not confined to the institutional home (Desarbo et al., 2017). For instance, a quarter of Cornell University's recent graduates end up settling in the New York City metro area. Similarly, the University of North Carolina sends as many alumni to New York City and Washington, DC, as it does to Raleigh, North Carolina, while the University of Iowa sends one in six graduates to the Chicago metro area, double the rate at which they move to Des Moines, Iowa (Dougherty, et al., 2018). This distribution creates a consumer base for institutions should a team championship be held in these cities and these programs be represented (Clark, 2010).

    Furthermore, fans are an integral part of the cultural experience of attending a sporting event (Green, 2001). Being surrounded by others who strongly identify with and are passionate about the event being attended can greatly enhance the overall quality of the event (Cordina, Gannon, & Croall, 2018). Alumni may attend for the novelty of the event but stay for the passion and excitement of the sport and atmosphere created by the sport's fans.

    Wrestling programs can take advantage by tapping into the inherent fanbase within their nation, a group 81% more likely than the general public to have an income greater than $100,000 a year, one of the most sought-after demographics for marketers (Learfield, 2021). And in doing so, the wrestling program will raise its profile with the institution's supporters and key stakeholders through the novel engagement of a championship event (Cialdini et al., 1976).

    3. The Missing Majority

    Though wrestling has made great strides to engage women and girls, as evident by the rapid growth in sponsorship and participation within girls' wrestling programs (Bennett, 2022; Eaton, 2022), the sport continues to be hindered by a significant spectator gender imbalance (James & Ross, 2004).

    Women are the majority within the United States, comprising 51% of the population (U.S. Census Bureau, 2021) as well as the majority within the Universities (Goldin, Katz, & Kuziemko, 2006) which largely fund wrestling beyond the high school level. Women also watch sports at similar rates as men (Dietz-Uhler et al., 2000) and account for 47% of all highly engaged fans across the thirteen most popular sports in the world (Gemba, 2020).

    The underrepresentation of women as wrestling consumers can be attributed to the misalignment with wrestling's competition and championship structure and the unique motivations of women for consuming sporting events (Sutton and Knoester, 2022).

    Women enjoy attending sporting events that maximizes the crowd experience for fans (Zembura & Żyśko, 2015), placing a significantly higher degree of importance on the amenities available to attendees of an event than men (Hall & O'Mahony, 2006), and often view sporting events as social functions providing the opportunity to engage with others within a community (Dietz-Uhler et al., 2000).

    Women demonstrate a greater propensity for team identification and will attendand sporting events for their preferred team at a higher rate than men (James & Ridinger, 2002), driven by their belief in the importance of fan loyalty (Chen, 2010). This contrasts with men, who tend to display less allegiance to a particular team, rendering men a more challenging consumer for sports marketers to capture.

    The unwavering loyalty exhibited by women towards their favored team makes them a highly significant consumer group for sports. Regardless of athletic performance, they will consistently support and relish the experience of watching their team compete (Hall & O'Mahony, 2006).

    The main factor that motivates attendance at sporting events is the level of interest in the team (Kim & Trail, 2010). Women are more likely to identify with teams and become heavily invested in rivalries (Havard, Achen, & Ryan, 2019). This finding underscores the significance of women as a critical consumer base in the sports industry, as their strong allegiance to teams renders them a valuable target for sports marketers.

    However, the omission of women as consumers of wrestling could have significant implications beyond their immediate involvement. For instance, women play a crucial role in bridging networks of disparate groups and generations (Cornwell, 2011). This point is further supported by research from Grundy & Read (2012), who found that having a daughter significantly improves the likelihood of interaction between elderly parents, their children, and other relatives. According to Mark Waller, former chief marketing officer for the NFL, women are the driver of decisions within households and were responsible for creating the conditions to make football a family tradition (Kitroeff, 2013).

    Research suggests individuals who are married or have children tend to exhibit a stronger attachment to their preferred team compared to those who are single (Bickle & Burnsed, 2018). This information suggests that university students, who are often single and not yet parents, may be an ideal target consumer for intercollegiate wrestling, as they are already on campus where contests are held, and have a low cost of acquisition compared to prospects who are away from campus. At this stage of their lives, university students are more likely to establish an attachment to their school's wrestling program, and this connection is more likely to endure in the long term.

    Interestingly, gender plays a significant role in how family obligations affect an individual's sports consumption. Men often experience a decrease in their sports consumption as their family responsibilities increase. On the other hand, women tend to experience an increase in their sports consumption, particularly in terms of attending live sporting events, after becoming parents (Sutton and Knoester, 2022). This is especially true for women who were already consumers of sports prior to having children (Newland & Hayduk, n.d.). While sports fandom is passed down from parents to their children, mothers can have an even greater impact on fandom by their nature to cultivate attendance and loyalty (Tamir, 2020).

    As a result, women act as a multiplier for generational support for a team driving attendance in both the short term and the long term.

    To attract and engage women consumers, the sport must place an emphasis on teams and create competition environments that are social and engaging (Fridley, Dittmore, & Stokowski, 2022), while developing marketing strategies that are not focused solely on wins and losses (Chang & Chiu, 2016). It is important to note this tailoring to be more inclusive of women will not exclude the dependable core of male fans (Hall & O'Mahony, 2006) as these changes do not negatively impact their motivations for attendance or engagement. However, the tailoring for women does better align with the process of cultivating a culture of financial donors for collegiate sports programs (O'Neil, & Schenke, 2007).

    4. Progressive Marketing

    The progressive format of the team advancement tournament in the NCAA Wrestling Championship creates a snowball effect, attracting increasing interest with each passing round.

    Each individual team dual competition in the NCAA Wrestling Championship provides an opportunity to engage with the community, drawing on the institutional brand to drive attendance (Laverie & Arnett, 2012). The early round on-campus events serve as marketing opportunities for the sport and subsequent championship events. As fans are drawn to the event, they become familiar with the sport, individual athletes, and programs through the team context.

    The NCAA Division I Men's Basketball Tournament was not always the cultural phenomenon it is today. In fact, many people were once unaware of its existence. Prior to 1963, the championship game was only broadcast on some regional television stations, often on tape delay during non-peak hours (Walker, n.d.).

    In 1979, the NCAA Wrestling tournament drew a total attendance of 62,809, with a daily average of 20,936 (National Collegiate Athletic Association, n.d. - j). While the exact number of sessions is unknown, assuming six sessions, the attendance per session would have been 10,468, comparable to the Men's Basketball Championship average attendance of 11,914 across 22 sessions in the same year (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2022q).

    It wasn't until the launch of ESPN which picked up the early round Men's Basketball Championship games in 1980 that the tournament experienced significant growth in popularity (Walker, n.d.). ESPN enhanced media coverage for the early rounds of the tournament, leading to greater visibility for teams. Additionally, they introduced the "Selection Show" in 1982, a widely appreciated segment that acknowledged teams for their achievements. This enabled fans to observe and support the regional teams they already knew and enjoyed, while also developing an interest in other programs and their athletes (Hoegele, Schmidt, & Torgler, 2014b). As the fans became vested in the tournament, they maintained their interest in the championships even after the regional teams of interest were eliminated (Tournament History, n.d.).

    The nature of dual meet team competitions, which provides programs the opportunity to utilize various line-ups of athletes throughout the team-advancement tournament, increases spectators' exposure to more athletes within the program and in doing so increases fan affinity to the program (Hoegele, Schmidt, & Torgler, 2014a).

    In addition to their favorite team, fans' exposure to athletes and teams during the tournament can leave a lasting impact. Notably, fans often continue to follow the success and advancements of standout athletes from rival programs in future competitions, particularly if they made a memorable impression in their previous performances (Hoegele, Schmidt & Torgler, 2014b). In most NCAA team-advancement championships, fans of the losing team typically become interested in tracking the progress of the team and athletes that defeated them throughout the championship. However, wrestling stands apart by introducing a separate individual championship. This unique format allows the losing program to generate increased interest in their own student-athletes who have left a lasting impression on event spectators. As these athletes participate in the individual championship, they become more recognizable to the viewers, whether they attended the event in person or watched it on television. This is due to the athletes being previously introduced to fans through team competitions held on dates and at times that did not clash with high school competitions or NCAA men's basketball tournaments.

    The progressive nature of the tournament format utilizing breaks between rounds of competition maximizes the benefits of basking in reflected glory, the psychological phenomenon in which fans and alumni identify with success of programs and claim the success as their own (Cialdini et al., 1976). This is why supporters are likely to say, "we won" following a team's win but distance themselves in a loss to say, "they lost." It's also why fans are more likely to wear team apparel following a win. This effect is magnified for fans attending the contest at the time of the win which makes a contest location vital for cultivating fan support for a program (Madrigal, 1995). This phenomenon is not simply limited to the psychological benefits for supporters of winning teams but extends to the physiological benefits as well. In addition to improving their mood and self-esteem, supporters of winning teams experience an increase in their testosterone levels when they watch a team they support do well (Bernhardt et al., 1998).

    Attendance is an important signal of the value to consumers and media partners. When consumers of televised sports see fans in the stands, they respond positively to the event and the significance of the event is magnified which increases interest in future events (Behrens & Uhrich, 2021). Holding events in venues that lack a quality crowd atmosphere not only deprives the student-athletes of a quality atmosphere but leads to a lack of interest by would be viewers of the event. To build a community of supporters, it is necessary to put a focus on spectators and cultivate a quality atmosphere for the event (Walsh & Beatty, 2007). By driving demand for attendance, programs will be able to benefit from transactional based fundraising efforts (i.e., season tickets or access to limited NCAA Championship tickets) which will improve efficiency in fundraising efforts (Hanson, 2019).

    The team competition structure with on-campus team dual meets, creates an atmosphere that increases the opportunity for fan engagement. Fans can connect with the wrestlers and the teams in a more intimate setting, allowing for a deeper emotional investment in the championship event. The break between rounds allows fans to reflect on the successes of the teams and individual wrestlers in those events. This reflection increases fan excitement and investment in the tournament as fans experience the highs of success for a longer period of time. While a subsequent round loss may come, the hope and excitement that fans feel in the time off between rounds continues for a greater period leading to greater satisfaction with the team and a greater vested interest in the success of the program.

    5. Induced Demand

    William Stanley Jevons' (1865) pioneering research marked the beginning of the exploration into induced demand. He discovered that the more efficient steam engine, which required less coal for production, resulted in an unexpected increase in total coal consumption. This economic phenomenon, whereby enhancements in the efficiency of a technology or resource usage led to a surge in the overall consumption or usage of that resource, is now commonly known as the Jevons Paradox.

    Induced demand refers to the phenomenon where increasing the supply of a good or service leads to an increase in demand for that good or service. In other words, when the availability or accessibility of a resource or service increases, people tend to use it more frequently, leading to an overall increase in demand. This can be the result of unidentified latent demand whereby demand is unable to be met due to constraints (Clifton & Moura, 2017).

    The interesting thing about induced demand is how it can impact markets and industries whereby demand would be expected to be static. This can lead to unexpected results.

    Some examples:

    • Physician Induced Demand - When the physician-to-patient ratio increases in markets with fixed medical costs, the expected result would be fewer work hours for physicians due to an oversupply of physicians, resulting in lower demand for their services and, consequently, lower physician incomes. However, research has shown that physicians do not necessarily work fewer hours or earn less money in response to lower demand. Instead, patients will often schedule more appointments with their doctors as the wait times are likely to be lower allowing patients to see their doctors to discuss items that wouldn't have warranted taking the time to try to get an appointment in the past (Dzampe & Takahashi, 2022) or patients may be provided with alternative services that yield higher reimbursement rates as doctors have more time to provide these services to their patients (Das, 2002; Cromwell & Mitchell, 1986).

    • Traffic Induced Demand - Expanding highway capacity to alleviate traffic congestion may appear to be a logical solution; however, this practice will often actually increase overall highway travel volume, subsequently leading to further congestion (Lee, Klein, & Camus, 1999). This phenomenon is not limited to the United States, as it is replicated worldwide (Bucsky & Juhasz, 2022). When highway capacity increases, it becomes more convenient for individuals to make more trips or to have commutes that are greater distances requiring them to be on the highways for longer periods of time. This leads to an increase in overall highway usage, resulting in the highway capacity again reaching a state of congestion equilibrium. The Katy Highway in Texas serves as a prime example, the highway was expanded to 26 lanes to resolve traffic congestion, making it the world's widest highway. However, five years after the expansion the highway was experiencing longer commute times than before the expansion (Cortright, 2015).

    Intercollegiate wrestling has been the subject of a growing and contentious debate regarding alternative competition structures, specifically team and individual formats, and which yields the most interest from consumers. While it is widely acknowledged within the wrestling community that the NCAA Championships are "college wrestling's crown jewel" (Hamilton, 2022) and "the most fun wrestling event in the world every year" (Hook, 2022), there has been a fear that adding another NCAA Championship event would dilute interest in wrestling by offering too many events.

    This debate between team and individual formats has likely misunderstood the core motivation for consumer interest in the NCAA Wrestling Championship. The event's success is not predicated on the individual competition format, nor is it due to calculating team standings based on results from athletes competing at the event. These attributes abound at competitions throughout the season without anywhere near the level of consumer interest experienced by the NCAA championships.

    The NCAA championship is successful because it is the NCAA Division I Championship, the event for which all athletes have spent their year training, the championship that all member schools strive to win. Fans, as well as the public, know the significance of the competition thanks to the strength of the NCAA brand.

    The best wrestling takes place at the event specifically because it is the NCAA Division I Wrestling Championships and athletes are committed to succeeding at this event. The spike in interest for the championship, compared to other events, is due to the event being an NCAA Championship and is experienced by all NCAA sports. The NCAA branding is universally known and accepted as a significant competition that determines historic accomplishments for teams and athletes which will live on in the record books.

    The decoupling of the NCAA Wrestling Team Championship, to be held on a separate date and at a different location than the NCAA Individual Wrestling Championships, will not change the significance of the honors the NCAA bestows. The same team crown will be awarded to a team and ten individuals will continue to be recognized for winning their weight division at the NCAA wrestling championships.

    This expansion of the current event into another event will expand viewership and attendance based on the same principles we observe within these other examples of induced demand. This theory suggests that by creating an additional event with the same qualities that consumers desire and reducing the burden of attending this event, there will be an increase in total demand to attend NCAA Wrestling Championship events.

    The motivation for consumers is specifically due to wrestling taking place within the context of an NCAA Championship. As a result, expanding the NCAA Championships will not dilute the consumer base for NCAA Championship wrestling but will induce demand. Just as expanding a highway lowers the cost (in time) to travel a distance and induces more traffic, the expansion of the NCAA Championship will lower the cost for consumers to be able to attend an NCAA Wrestling Championship event by having more events in more diverse locations. Bringing the NCAA championships to more prospective consumers, which will increase overall consumer interest in championship wrestling by lowering the cost to the consumer (in time, money, and hassle) to attend NCAA Wrestling Championship competition in person.

    The introduction to NCAA Championship wrestling serves as the gateway to interest in college wrestling as a whole. It is through these events that consumers become educated on the sport, its individuals, the pageantry, and witness the historic storylines that have engrossed wrestling loyalists and made them vested in the results of competitions throughout the season.

    However, the current form of the NCAA Wrestling Championship cannot accommodate the consumer demand for in-person attendance while maintaining the high level of service for attendees that makes the event so successful (Hall & O'Mahony, 2006). The only way to grow the event and increase personal engagement is through horizontal scaling of the Championship to serve more consumers through a distributive championship model by breaking out the individual components of the current championship event (Wilder, 2012).

    Instead of the significance of the NCAA championships being limited to three consecutive days of competition, mostly during the workday, NCAA Championship competition will take place over the course of a month. This increase in NCAA Championship competition, aided by expanded media coverage and a competition schedule that reduces conflicts with other NCAA Championship events, will provide greater engagement and education while maintaining the significance of the NCAA Championship.

    The NCAA Championships will remain the crown jewel of wrestling with consumers remaining eager to attend. We have witnessed the excessive demand for major events where traditional indoor arenas are unable to accommodate the crowds, such as the cancelled 2020 NCAA Championships in Minnesota, which experienced "soaring" demand (Carlson, 2019) and the record-breaking outdoor college dual meet in Iowa attended by 42,287 people (Meredith, 2015; Moore, 2015). Expanding the NCAA Championship will induce more demand, as some people will attend both championship events, but many more people will attend their first NCAA Wrestling event.

    In regard to the NCAA Championships, the popular quote from the movie Field of Dreams (Robinson, 1989) may best capture the significance of the event: "If you build it, they will come."

    6. Student-Athlete Demand

    It should be noted that this change in competition structure has a significant impact on the demand for the championship from student-athletes themselves.

    The changes to the new structure opens postseason competition opportunities to more athletes by decoupling the current four championships moving away from the one athlete model within Division I Wrestling.

    Under the current competition model, only one athlete per program, per weight classification, may compete within postseason competition. As a result, one athlete is given the opportunity to compete for an individual conference championship, team conference championship, individual national championship, and team national championship.

    The new structure not only allows different athletes to compete at different postseason competitions by decoupling these events but also provides an opportunity for multiple athletes to compete within the same championship segments.

    For instance, a program could choose to have a different athlete compete in the 157 lbs. weight classification in each round of the NCAA Wrestling (Team) Championship on their way to a National Championship with each athlete contributing to the team's national championship performance. Or a conference could choose to permit multiple athletes to compete for each school within the same weight classification at the conference championships.

    This pathway to additional opportunities for student-athletes, which is more inclusive of the program's roster improves the student-athlete experience. This can have a significant impact for programs over the longterm as this can aid in retention of athletes but also a positive undergraduate experience improves the likellihood of financial donations to programs in the future (Monks, 2003).

D. Championship Properties

To best serve the sport as well as the member schools, it is important to maximize the value of all championship properties. This will provide an avenue for continued investment within the sport.

    1. Conference Championship Properties

    The new competition and championship structure ensures that conferences retain complete control over their conferences and their championship properties. Conferences will continue to have the option to use the conference championship exemption (17.28.5.3a) to conduct a conference championship for wrestling without needing to count a date of competition on their schedule.

    The only significant change to the conference would be the timing of the event. If a conference desires the matches to count towards qualification for individual athletes, the championship must now be held at any point in the season prior to the conclusion of the regular season. Under the current qualification system, conferences are mandated to hold their conference championships, if used for qualification, after the close of the regular season, post pre-allocation bid announcements and before the scheduled announcement of at-large bids. This new structure offers more scheduling flexibility for conferences. Though in practice, many conferences may opt to schedule the championship as the final event for the final weekend of the regular season, as is common in other individual sports, there is no requirement for them to do so.

    With the change in individual athlete qualification processes, the conference championships may become more valuable for conferences as the events will no longer be limited based on the NCAA's dictated qualification requirements. This means that the conference membership will no longer be restricted to one athlete per weight class. With conferences having the option to increase representation at their conference championships.

    Conferences that elect to permit multiple entries into their conference championship may enhance the value of the championship while offering increased opportunities for athletes within member programs to compete in championship environments and stay engaged throughout the season, ultimately enhancing the student-athlete experience.

    2. NCAA Championship Properties

    The NCAA, with over 100 years of experience in overseeing collegiate sports, is renowned for its ability to run championship events that are both competitive and enjoyable for athletes and fans. This reputation has made the NCAA wrestling championship the premier domestic wrestling event, with the association's significant investments in the championships contributing to this success.

    The prestige of the NCAA championships has created excitement among fans, raising the profile of the association's championships. The changes to the NCAA championship properties will result in a significant increase in the quantity and value of the wrestling championship. These changes will be a net addition to wrestling and will not require the elimination of any events from the season's schedule.

    The increased value of the wrestling championship as a stand-alone property will be beneficial for media partners, as it will allow for a better return on investment when marketing the championships. It will also provide media partners with an alternative sport for consumers not interested in basketball competitions.

    According to Desser Sports Media, Inc, in the Media & Sponsorship Addendum of the NCAA External Gender Equity Review (Kaplan Hecker & Fink LLP, 2021c), the practice of bundling of media rights for NCAA championship has resulted in only one media partner being capable of serving all championships, leading to a lack of competitive bids, and resulting in the media partner underinvesting in the assets.

    When the current media rights deal for the "Other Championships," a package of 28 NCAA Championships, concludes in 2024, it is expected that at least some championships will be split into other media packages. Women's basketball, as the most valuable property within the current "Other Championships" package, is the most likely asset to have a different distribution partner in the future (Kaplan Hecker & Fink LLP, 2021c). This presents a great opportunity for wrestling, as ESPN is likely to remain the best-positioned distribution partner for the NCAA Wrestling Championship properties and many of the remaining properties in the "Other Championships" package. Wrestling could become the flagship championship on ESPN for winter collegiate sports.

    However, the current structure of the NCAA Wrestling Championship, with three consecutive days of competition, does not lend itself to investment from a distribution partner, as the programming is limited. As a result, the event does not warrant an investment from ESPN to advertise the event to its network's viewers. To take advantage of this opportunity, it is imperative that the NCAA's Wrestling Championship property assets and programing be expanded to better serve the network for an extended period on the calendar and be conducted in a manner that will attract consumers to the programming.

      Niche sports such as wrestling have a dedicated and loyal fan base, providing advertisers with a unique opportunity to increase brand awareness by penetrating deeply into wrestling's nationally distributed consumer base (Greenhalgh & Greenwell, 2013). By partnering with an expanded Wrestling Championship, corporate partners can significantly enhance their national brand exposure, benefiting from extensive impressions and engagement at a competitive price point, ultimately leading to more potential partnerships for the championships.

      c. Media Distribution

      Despite the growing popularity of streaming video services, linear television continues to dominate as a mass medium, reaching 71% of US households in the United States and remaining a driver of media consumption and social cultural contagion (Hutchins & Sanderson, 2017; Munson, 2021; Madaris, 2022). Even streaming giant Netflix, with its substantial market share in the United States and Canada, has yet to match the widespread appeal of linear TV, reaching only around 53% of households (Netflix, 2022).

      As one of the few mediums left where an advertiser can reach a large audience (Dimmick, 2003), linear television has experienced a significant increase in advertising revenue (Nielson, 2021). Major media companies have taken note of this trend and have made long-term investments in the future of linear TV (Weprin, 2022).

      There has been a lot of talk about "cord cutting" due to the increased adoption of video streaming services. However, the reality is that those who don't often use video streaming services are more likely to view streaming services as a threat to linear television than regular viewers of streaming (Cha & Chan-Olmsted, 2012). Rather than replacing linear television, video streaming services have largely complemented linear television distribution, in part due to cable providers being a main source of internet access and bundling their services to keep their customers (Baccarne, Evens, & Schurrman, 2013), as well as linear television holding the distribution rights to the most in-demand live events (Gantz & Lewis, 2014; Harvey, 2022). In fact, the number of people watching linear TV on a weekly basis increased in 2021 compared to 2020 when many people were at home due to the pandemic (Bentley, 2022). This could be attributed to the lack of sports programing available during the pandemic in 2020 and the return of sporting events in 2021 which brought viewers back to linear television.

      Streaming services are an effective way to distribute niche programming that lacks mass appeal (Hindman & Wiegand, 2008), as well as the perfect distribution channel for continuing to watch beloved programming that was once the staple of television syndication as "reruns" (Albiniak, 2021). However, not all content performs well on streaming platforms (Wildman, 2008). Some programming, particularly programming that is only likely to be consumed in the moment it is happening, such as sports and the news, performs better on linear television (Wildman, 2008; Fischer, 2022).

      Some of this preference for consuming live sports through linear distribution is due to the technical limitations of streaming. Despite marketing campaigns touting the convenience of being able to watch programing from any device while you're on the go, consumers still prefer to watch streaming video via a traditional television set (Baccarne, Evens, & Schurrman, 2013; Bury & Li, 2015). Even younger consumers prefer watching video on television sets, electing to use alternate viewing devices as a last resort (Greer & Ferguson, 2015). With linear television having higher signal fidelity than streaming services, the viewing experience for consumers is better through linear television (Gantz & Lewis, 2014).

      The infrastructure needed to effectively deliver quality streaming video to a distributed consumer base, while being able to scale based on fluctuating demand, requires the use of content delivery networks, which creates a delay in the delivery of the content to the consumer (Munson, 2018; Byrne, 2018).

      This poses a problem for consumers due to the way they increasingly engage with live sport. A significant motivator for watching live sports is due to the social experiences that come from sharing in the sport experience, such as being able to talk with others about the event and share experiences (Hino, 2015). Even the motivation to seek out live sports on a streaming platform is based largely on social influences, more than the quality of the video or the cost of the programming, particularly with younger viewers (Indrawati & Haryoto, 2015; Whittle, 2018). Unlike other forms of video entertainment, viewers of sports programming tend to be more deliberate and engaged in their viewing, actively participating in their viewership, and seeking to share the experience with others (Gantz et al., 2016). This often involves real-time communications such as text messages, tweets, forums, chatrooms, and other forms of online interaction (Smith, Pegoraro, & Cruikshank, 2019).

      Communicating with different individuals during live events, combined with the latency in video delivery from streaming platforms, can result in "spoilers" and a frustrating social experience.

      Linear television, especially when not on a premium subscription tier, remains the best media distribution method for live sports. For instance, when the NCAA Division I Colonial Athletic Association (CAA) signed a media distribution deal with FloSports to stream all their conference games not on linear television, the conference used the money from the streaming deal to purchase linear TV time to get more of their games on television (Madia, 2019). Similarly, the Tennis Channel fought a seven-year legal battle with Comcast to get its channel included on a lower tier of cable, despite offering all its programming, including the prestigious French Open, through its direct-to-consumer streaming platform Tennis Channel Plus and tennischanneleverywhere.com (Boswell & Dell, 2009; Dittmore & Li, 2017).

      While linear television distribution is desired, it is not always possible. Sometimes programing simply won't have the demand to justify distributing to a mass audience and streaming distribution will be the best option to reach consumers.

      Streaming offers the advantage of utilizing a multi-channel distribution model to create customized spectator experiences that cater to the diverse motivations of consumers, satisfying their desire for a more personalized and immersive sports viewing experience that cannot be replicated on linear television (Hu, Zhang, & Wang, 2017).

      The most widely known example of such programming may be the popular ManningCast in the NFL. This alternative programming, which is often simply the same video feed of the game available on the linear broadcast, is made available on the ESPN+ streaming service. However, rather than the expert commentary and analysis provided on the linear feed, viewers are able to listen to the Manning brothers casually viewing and discussing the game, providing a more social and intimately connected viewing experience (Honey, 2021).

      The NCAA Wrestling Championships have already demonstrated the success of complementary programming through the "Every Mat, Every Match" production, which offers viewers at home the flexibility to choose which matches they want to watch, rather than being limited to the matches covered on the linear feed (Dachman, 2015). This approach enables the linear feed to be tailored for a wider audience, using announcing geared toward viewers who are less familiar with the sport and its competitors, while still keeping the audience engaged. At the same time, the match feeds can cater to more knowledgeable fans who want to follow specific matches.

      To further develop this concept, the multi-channel format can be extended to the NCAA Wrestling Team Championship, offering team-centric experiences through individual feeds featuring each team's radio announcer. This approach has the potential to foster stronger emotional connections to teams, particularly among alumni of the schools, without increasing overhead costs.

      By delivering high-quality programming, this approach not only enhances the viewing experience for a diverse set of consumers, but also fosters engagement and discussion around the event, ultimately adding value to the championship property and media partners distributing the content.

      Having quality distribution partners available for both linear and streaming distribution is essential for a successful championship property, as increased media distribution leads to increased attendance (Falls & Natke, 2014). Although bifurcation of distribution is possible, the best distribution strategy involves one exclusive distributor with the ability to self-serve both linear and streaming channels (Stennek, 2014). With increasing levels of vertical integration within media companies, there is an incentive to maximize the value of owned properties at the expense of non-owned properties (Waterman, & Weiss, 1996; Law, Harvey, & Kemp, 2002). This increases investment into the championship programming by the distributors, ultimately improving the experience for consumers and maximizing the value of the property (Stennek, 2014).

      For the NCAA Wrestling Championships, ESPN is the ideal distribution partner, as they not only have the infrastructure and a track record of producing quality content for wrestling consumers during the NCAA Championships but also hold the media rights for the majority of Division I wrestling programs, giving them an incentive to continue investing in the sport to maximize the value of the properties they already own (Mid-American Conference, 2014; Volner, 2019; Hofheimer, 2020; Ufnowski, 2022).

      For Divsion I Wrestling to be well positioned for a continued relationship with ESPN, the Championship property must be further developed to continue to cultivate the consumption of intercollegiate wrestling. The increased consumption of linear programing is necessary to increase the overall media value of the Championship property by increasing the subsequent consumption on the streaming platforms for ESPN and the member schools (Tang and Cooper, 2013).
