III. Modernizing the Competition Structure

The framework proposed in this report, when fully implemented, will modernize the sport in a way that will enhance the experience for its key stakeholders. It is important to note that this report will only address the structural framework of collegiate wrestling and will not discuss the specific rules of individual matches (as outlined in Wrestling Rule 2.2.7). This means that there will be no mention of the competition rules for individual matches. The actual wrestling that occurs between two athletes is beyond the scope of this report.

The forthcoming framework for NCAA Division I Wrestling seeks to uphold the sport's inherent character while seamlessly integrating features from other collegiate sports. This endeavor aims to establish a structure that adheres closely to NCAA regulations. By taking cues from analogous individual sports, wrestling can employ these models as a testing ground to validate proposed alterations. Whenever a change is derived or modified from another NCAA sport, it will be duly noted to indicate its source.

Why is change warranted?

The Division I wrestling structure presents a unique blend of individual and team competition, with occasional conflicts between the two. This duality has historically been a strength of the sport, but the current structure fails to effectively balance the two, resulting in conflicting incentives that adversely affect both individual and team interests. By revising the structure, it is possible to better align these interests and serve Division I wrestling stakeholders more effectively. Moreover, streamlining the administration of the sport through greater consistency with other NCAA sports will help modernize wrestling and address long-standing issues within the sport.

A. Scheduling

Scheduling of team competitions will have a new framework.

    1. Competition Declarations

    The wrestling season presents a unique challenge in terms of continuity that is not present in other NCAA sports. While some sports like tennis may have both individual and team competitions, they are not conducted simultaneously during the same part of the season. Wrestling, on the other hand, regularly divides its programs between team dual competitions and individual competitions, leading to a lack of continuity and diminished team aspect. This can discourage fans from investing in team competitions, whether in person or through other media.

    This lack of continuity in wrestling can also result in confusing scenarios, such as when a conference awarded Conference Wrestler of the Week honors to an athlete competing unattached and redshirting while the varsity program was competing in two dual team competitions against other Division I programs (FloWrestling Staff, 2014; Clarion Athletics, n.d.). Such awards may inadvertently draw attention away from team competitions and towards individual performances, creating an imbalance in the sport.

    To address this issue, a clear distinction between the two competition structures, individual and team, will be established to better balance the two. This will ensure that each competition structure is given equal weight and recognition and that the team aspect of wrestling is not overshadowed by individual performances.

    Competition Date Types

    Each date in which an athlete from the program competes must be one of two possible competition date types:

      Team Date

      Team dates are dates in which the team conducts team competition with another program utilizing dual meet scoring. Under this format, the result is intended to be recorded on the team's season record.

      Adopting the policy used within Tennis, all team dates are to be counted as a date of competition, no matter the number of athletes participating, or the total number of matches conducted.

        Tennis - "Dual Tennis Match. A dual tennis match in which head-to-head competition occurs between two collegiate institutions or between an intercollegiate team and an outside team shall count as one date of competition." - NCAA Division I Manual, 17.23.5.1.1 Dual Tennis Match.

      Note: Though a team date will count against the maximum number of allowable dates of competition, the date is not to be counted for the purposes of meeting NCAA rule 20.10.6.3, Minimum Contests and Participants Requirements for Sports Sponsorship, if the minimum number of athletes do not compete on the date.

      Participation in any of the following competition types requires the use of a team date of competition:

      • Dual-Meet Competition (Wrestling Rule 3.5.a)
      • Extra Match (Wrestling Rule 3.5.c)
      • Dual Meet Team-Advancement Tournament (Wrestling Rule 3.5.g)

      When utilizing a team competition date, athletes may only compete against rostered athletes attached to the specific school the program is engaging with in a team competition on the given date.

      Individual Date

      An individual date is to be used when any rostered athletes compete in any competition where there is not a team competition that will be reflected on the team's season record.

      Individual dates may or may not require the use of a countable date of competition.

      Participation in any of the following competition types constitutes an individual date:

      • Exhibition Match (Wrestling Rule 3.5.b)
          • Note: Not to be included on an athlete's Season Record Form
      • Individual Advancement Tournament (Wrestling Rule 3.5.d)
      • Open Event (Wrestling Rule 3.5.e)
      • Pool/Round Robin Event (Wrestling Rule 3.5.f)
    Process

    Prior to the start of the season, when a program declares their season of competition, they will also need to declare their team competition dates by publishing their season schedule with opponents and/or events.

    Any date that is declared as a team date is reserved exclusively for team competition. All other dates may be used for individual competition.

    If an institution encounters extenuating circumstances (e.g., natural disaster) that require a change of an already scheduled team competition to a different date, an individual date may become a team competition date. However, the previously scheduled team competition date may not be repurposed as an individual competition date. (Example: A team may not reschedule a team competition to allow their athletes to compete in an individual competition that was scheduled for the same date.)

    A date may only be declared as one type. A date declaration applies to all athletes within the program, even in the event of multi-site or split roster competitions.

    Penalty/Enforcement

    If a program has engaged in conflicting competition types on the same date, the date will automatically become a team date.

    Competitions that are not permitted based on the program's selected date declaration type are to be updated on the athlete's season record form, with any wins recorded by athletes becoming a loss by forfeit. Opponents will record a win by forfeit because of the ineligible athlete competing. Losses recorded by ineligible athletes are not to be altered.

    Benefits, Competition Date Types

    By reserving team competition dates, athletes and fans can better plan for competitions and ensure that all athletes are available for team competition. This structure allows for individual competition, split team individual competition, and open competition, but also provides a framework for unifying teams and being more inclusive. At the start of the season, programs have complete control over the number and type of competition dates they use and when they choose to declare each type of competition date within the season. This flexibility in the quantity and type of competition dates allows each program to meet its specific scheduling needs. This structure will improve athlete well-being and help athletic departments plan more effectively, as there will be less strain on team medical staff due to split competitions.

    2. Daily Team Limit - Division I Opponent

    The current common practice of Division I wrestling teams competing in only one team competition on the date of competition will be codified for competition against Division I opponents.

    Team competitions against Division I opponents are to be limited to once per day. There is no daily limit of team competitions for team contests where the opponent is not a Division I member.

    Exemptions

    There will be an annual exemption for Multiple-Team Events to continue to provide opportunities for multiple Division I team competitions on a date as a mean to incentivize bringing teams from different conference and different regions of the country together to compete as has been successfully adopted and implemented within college basketball.

      Regular Season Multiple-Team Event to be applied to NCAA Rule 17.28.5.3 Annual Exemptions, Number of Dates of Competition (17.4.5.1.1, Men's Basketball. adapted)

      A team may compete against more than one Division I opponent in a day at one event per season, provided the following:

      1. The event is sponsored by the NCAA, an active member institution, a member conference of the Association, the National Wrestling Coaches Association, or the Peninsula Wrestling Association (Virginia Duals only);
      2. Participation is limited to one team per conference and, by institution, to not more than once in any four-year period for any event sponsored by the sponsoring entity;
      3. Each participating institution must participate in the same number of team competitions in the event, not to exceed five team competitions per team;
      4. The event must be a single-day or two-day event for all participating members;
      5. Tickets sold for the event must only grant admission to competitions for the specific event and not include admission for any other event;
      6. The event may include a non-Division I institution as a participant only if the non-Division I institution serves as the host of the event.
        • An isolated institution that serves as the sponsor of a qualifying regular season multiple-team event is not required to count its participation in the event as its one qualifying regular season multiple-team event, provided the event is conducted at the institution's regular competition venue and the total number of team contests the program competes in at multiple-team events during the season does not exceed (6) six. Such an event qualifies as a qualifying regular-season multiple-team event for the other participating institutions.
        • Isolated institutions are not limited to sponsoring a multiple-team event once every four years.

      Isolated Institution - Any institution where there are no other Division I institutions sponsoring wrestling located within 400 miles of the institution. (Softball, Cross Country, Soccer, Field Hockey adapted).

      Note: Multiple-Team Events should not be confused with Multi-Team Events. Both are declared in the NCAA Manual and have different definitions.

    Benefits, Daily Team Limit

    By limiting team competitions between Division I members, the events that do occur have greater significance. These events enhance the fan experience and allow media partners to better package team competitions. The use of the Multiple-Team Event and the Isolated Member Exemption serves the Association's membership by providing unique opportunities to compete against member programs outside of their region without being too burdensome on individual program budgets. This rule also preserves longstanding events that are a staple of the collegiate season, while ensuring that the schools sponsoring the sport have some control over the limited exempted events. This change provides a more inclusive experience for the athletes within a program by incentivizing the scheduling of non-Division I programs or scheduling an individual competition when more than one match is desired on a given date, allowing more athletes to participate.

    As a result of each program being limited to sponsoring a MTE once every four years, the opportunity for different programs to be able to market, promote, and benefit from a unique event will be extended to each program. This allows programs to work with outside partners to help market the events as novel events which will allow them to reach deeper into their local and regional communities which benefits the programs and those wrestling communities in their region (DiPietro, 2001).

    3. Elimination of the NWCA Competition Exemptions.

    The following exemptions under NCAA Rule 17.28.5.3 Annual Exemptions, Number of Dates of Competition shall be removed:

      (i) NWCA All-Star Meet.

      (j) NWCA National Duals.

    The National Wrestling Coaches Association (NWCA) All-Star Classic, according to NWCA Executive Director Mike Moyer, is intended to "add significant value to amateur wrestling because it serves as a 'kick off' event for college wrestling as it showcases amateur wrestling’s heroes and champions all across the country" (Cooper, 2009b). However, the presence of this event diminishes the significance of competition during the season. The matches contested during the event are not official and do not hold any significance for the athletes or their teams in the season. The event also distracts athletes (and coaches) from focusing on their season events, as it takes place during the season. While the event may receive significant interest from fans, it may also diminish the significance of subsequent matches that take place during the season for these athletes and/or teams. Support for the event has been inconsistent, with sporadic participation by invited athletes and programs, and the event was completely cancelled for the 2019 season (Naasz, 2019). Despite its long history, the event should be discontinued, as it is outdated in the modern era where wrestling is readily available to fans through technology.

    Benefits, Elimination of NWCA Competition Exemptions.

    All programs will be given an equal ability to schedule events and utilize all the permissible scheduling exemptions. The proposed Regular Season Multiple-Team Event, which is more inclusive for the sport as it is not restricted to one organizer and a select number of programs, will replace the exemption for the current NWCA events within the NCAA Manual.

B. Team Competition - Administration

Team competitions, more commonly known within wrestling as dual meets, will have an updated framework and process for how they are to be administered.

    1. Visiting Team - Single Team Competition Weight Allowance

    For any team competition where the visiting team is only competing in one team competition on the date, the team will be given a 1-pound (one pound) weight allowance for all their athletes.

    This weight allowance is not to be given to the home team for competitions held at neutral sites or when the visiting team will compete in a second team contest on the date.

    Athletes may only use the visiting team weight allowance if they meet the following criteria:

    • Athlete has weighed-in and competed at the weight class during the same season.
    • Athlete is permitted to compete in the weight class, based on their weight loss plan in the Optimal Performance Calculator (OPC), without needing the weight allowance.

    Any athlete not meeting the above requirements will have to meet standard weigh-in requirements.

    For determining the application of this policy, as it pertains to site location, the NCAA Statistics Policies and Guidelines document should be consulted.

    This provision is to be appended to Wrestling Rule 9.3.2, 1-Pound Weight Allowance.

    Benefits

    The NCAA weight management program has been a success in reducing unhealthy weight loss practices within the sport (Oppliger et al., 2006). This change will not alter the program; athletes will still need to follow the program to compete.

    The visiting team weight allowance works to balance the home venue advantage (Jamieson, 2010) in which the home team can benefit in the closest of matches (Moskowitz & Wertheim, 2011; Unkelbach & Memmert, 2010) while improving student-athlete welfare by reducing the burden to an athlete when traveling for a single team dual competition.

    2. Extra Countable Matches

    Extra matches are any matches provided to athletes outside of the context of structured event.

    Though extra matches may be held at any point on a team competition date, provided the two opponents are attached athletes from teams scheduled to compete in a team competition on the date, it is recommended that any additional matches precede the team scored portion of the contest.

    Note: If an extra match take place between athletes and their respective teams are not competing in a team dual competition on the date of the extra match, those matches are to be designated as exhibition matches and are not to be recorded on an athlete's season record form.

    Benefits, Extra Matches

    The use of extra matches is particularly useful for wrestling competitions that are being broadcast on linear television networks. This is because wrestling has a unique problem where, unlike other sports, if you miss the first 10 minutes of a dual, you miss an entire match. This can be an issue when the previous programming, such as a basketball game, goes into overtime and runs long, causing fans who were planning to watch the first wrestling match to miss it. This problem even persists at wrestling's marquee event, the NCAA Wrestling Finals, where viewers at home often complain about missing portions of the first match. This issue may have even been the catalyst for the change in the selection of the starting and ending weights for the championship finals (Elder, 2013).

    Including extra matches before the scored team competition allows for more flexibility in programming and creates a more inclusive competition for the participating teams by giving more athletes the opportunity to compete. It also ensures that spectators at the venue are not delayed in watching wrestling, should the competition have to wait for television availability. By including additional matches, fans can become familiar with a greater number of athletes within the program, which helps to foster a sense of team identity (Fisher & Wakefield, 1998). Maximizing the quality and duration of wrestling's programing is important to be well positioned to be attractive for linear television distribution as television is the best medium for attracting spectators to less popular sports (Sedky, Kortam, & AbouAish, 2022).

    Athletes don't want to be regulated to non-scored open competitions while their team is competing in front of supporting crowds. Extra matches will be possible only in concert with team competitions to extend the experience to additional athletes within the program. This inclusion and cohesion with the program will lead to a greater sense of satisfaction for student-athletes (Kimball, 2007).

    3. Strategic Match Ordering

    Strategic match ordering is the process in which weight class order is not sequential and is determined based on input from the participating teams' head coaches. This process will replace Rule 3, Section 8 in the Wrestling Rules.

    Process

    The selection of the match order will take place at the scorer's table prior to the captains' meeting with the referee.

    The visiting team will have first selection with the home team selecting second and alternating between the two teams until all matches are assigned.

    Any not yet selected weight class may be selected and placed into any position (1 through 10) in the order for matches to be wrestled.

    Simulation of Coaches Selecting Match Order

    Benefits, Strategic Match Ordering

    This process gives coaches the ability to make strategic decisions about the order of matches and the available athletes for competition. Competing in matches outside of the traditional progression from lighter to heavier weight classes may prevent some programs from being able to use certain athletes later in the match and could result in certain matchups being locked in. It may also allow coaches to hide injured athletes in late match bouts if the outcome of the team competition is not in doubt.

    While this process does not change the wrestling itself, it adds an additional element to the team competition and allows coaching staffs to use more strategy to benefit their programs. This structure has been introduced into the Division I men's golf championship and has added to the experience of athletes and fans (Herrington, 2012).

    4. Team Competition Lineup

    Prior to any dual meet team competition, coaches will be responsible for submitting their lineups to the event official as well as the opposing team.

    This process of formalizing lineups is already established practice within the NCAA:

      Baseball - "Ten minutes before the scheduled starting time, the umpire-in-chief shall conduct the pregame meeting with the representatives of the opposing teams. The head coach, during the first game of a weekend series or any nonconference game, must attend the pregame plate conference. During this meeting, the umpire-in-chief shall: a. Receive from the home team and visiting team their respective batting orders, in duplicate. The umpire will determine that the copies are identical, keep one copy and give the other copy to the opposing team’s representative. The umpire now is officially in charge of the game, and the lineups are official." - 2021 and 2022 Baseball Rules and Interpretations, Section 4.

      Softball- "Before the scheduled starting time, the plate umpire shall conduct the pregame meeting with a member of each coaching staff. During this meeting, warmups on the field of play shall be suspended and the plate umpire shall: Review lineup cards with each team’s coach and provide one last opportunity for changes without the changes being charged as substitutions. Once returned to the umpire, each team’s lineup is official. In the event of a subsequent change, the plate umpire shall report the substitution(s) to the opposing team, official scorer and public-address announcer" - 2022 and 2023 NCAA Softball Rules and Interpretations, Rule 5.8.2

    Process

    Athletes who are eligible to compete on the date must be declared for each team contest. To be declared for a team competition, an athlete must be included on the line-up card presented to the match official and opposing coaching staff.

    For dates where more than one team competition will be conducted, the submission of lineups shall take place 15 minutes prior to the scheduled start time of the contest, or immediately following the previous team contest should the contest be scheduled to start following a preceding contest.

    When only one team competition is scheduled to take place on a date, the establishment of lineups is to take place in concert with the weigh-in.

    Athletes are to be declared as only one of three types of athletes for the team contest:

      Committed Athletes (Team Competition) - Limited to a maximum of ten athletes, one per weight class. Committed athletes are declared as the team's primary competitor at a weight class for the scored team competition. Committed athletes are locked in to compete in their committed weight class, should they compete in the event. Committed athletes ensure there is representation for the team at the weight class in the competition. If the team declines to compete at this weight class, the committed athlete will be charged with a loss by forfeit. Committed Athletes are not eligible to compete in extra countable matches against the opposing team in which they have been declared a committed athlete, nor are they permitted compete in another weight class.

      Rovers - Limited to a maximum of three athletes for each team, per team competition, and only available when a team has filled each of the ten weight classes with a committed athlete. Rovers are not bound to any specific weight class. They may compete in the team competition at any weight class where they are eligible to compete (Wrestling Rule 3.7, Shifts in Weight Class - Dual Meets). Rovers are not eligible to compete in extra countable matches against the opposing team in which they have been declared.

      Extra Athletes - Athletes weighed in for the date and are available to compete in extra countable matches against extra athletes for the opposing team. Extra athletes may not compete in the countable team contest. There is no limit in the number of extra athletes a team may weigh-in. A team is not required to fill each weight class in the contested portion of the team competition to have extra athletes compete in extra countable matches.

    Benefits, Team Competition Lineup

    Establishing lineups before a contest ensures representation within weight classes. When an athlete is placed in the lineup as a committed athlete, they are locked into competing in the team competition. This means that the decision on whether the athlete will compete must be made before the team contest starts.

    The rover provides flexibility to the program to change the lineup during the contest, if there is representation within each weight class in the team contest.

    The number of rovers is determined based on historic travel limits imposed by conferences and a reasonable number of options based on the roster size.

    While the use of committed athletes or rovers does not prevent other athletes from competing in matches against the opposing team, these additional athletes are designated as extra athletes and may only compete in non-scored extra countable matches. They are not permitted to compete in the scored portion of the team competition.

C. Team Advancement Tournaments

    1. Terminal Scoring

    Terminal Scoring is a process, to be used in all season-ending events, where a team competition is concluded when one team is no longer able to win the team competition.

    Process

    When terminal scoring is used, a team competition will end when the point differential is greater than the available points remaining for a team to win. The points available should be determined by taking the number of matches remaining and multiplying by 6.

    When the team competition concludes, the result should identify that not all matches were completed by annotating with the number of matches completed in parenthesis preceded by the letter F to signify the result is final.

      Example: Team A is down by seven points with one match remaining in the dual. The team competition will end after only nine matches with the annotation of "(F9)" appended to the result to signify that the contest was final after nine matches.

    Terminal scoring is to be used for the NCAA Wrestling Championship. Team Advancement-Tournaments may elect to implement terminal scoring only if announced prior to the event and applied to all contests held for the event.

    All team contests, outside of team-advancement tournaments, are to contest all ten matches in a team competition, even if the team result is determined. However, the score should be recorded as final once the team score has been decided.

    This system has already been adopted for team contests at NCAA Championships:

      Tennis - "During the championships, all matches will be stopped after the doubles point is decided. All remaining individual matches will be stopped once a team winner (four points won) has been determined. The score will only reflect completed matches." - 2022 Division I Men's and Women's Tennis Championships, Pre-Championship 2021-22 Manual.

      Golf - "In team match-play competition, a total of five points will be available with one point being awarded for each individual match. Winning teams will advance to the semifinals and subsequently, the finals. The first team to win three points within the team match will advance, or in the case of the final match, be declared the national champion. Once a team has won three individual matches, any remaining individual matches will be halted at that point with the individual match recorded as it currently stood." - 2022 Division I Men's Golf Championships, Pre-Championship 2021-22 Manual.

    Benefit, Terminal Scoring

    During team championship events, the focus is on the team competition and any matches that are not necessary for the competition will not be contested. This eliminates the anti-climactic nature of team competitions that continue despite the result already being decided. Under this change, team competitions held within the context of a team championship event will always end with a match determining the team result. This process is beneficial for mismatches, particularly when combined with strategic match ordering, as it creates a power struggle between programs that want to "end the contest early" to rest certain athletes and those that want to extend the competition. It also creates an incentive for outmatched teams to try to extend the team competition (Moore, as cited by Palmer 2012). This system allows for the efficient use of limited national media exposure and provides opportunities to redistribute that exposure to other programs, maximizing the benefits for wrestling. This is possible due to the nature of scheduling championship events and the benefits of linear television distribution (to be discussed later).

    2. Tie-Breaker, Team Advancement

    The following process will be adopted to replace Rule 3, Section 21. Breaking Ties in Dual Meets and Team-Advancement Tournaments in the NCAA Wrestling Rulebook.

    When a team competition remains tied at the conclusion of 10 matches the result for the team competition is to be recorded as a tie. When a tie-breaker is required, the following process is to be used for determining team advancement.

    Process

    Following the conclusion of the team competition, the official timekeeper is to put five minutes on the clock. The first tie-breaker round will start when the clock reaches 0:00.

    Each tie-Breaker round is a single individual match of wrestling two minutes in length. (i.e. Identical to the first period of any match other than the duration.)

    However, the points accumulate from match to match, as does riding time.

    Though tie breaker rounds are held for a weight class they are not a re-match of the match wrestled at the weight classification. Any athlete who was eligible to compete in the weight class within the team dual meet is able to compete in a tie-breaker round for a given weight.

    A minimum of three rounds is necessary for the tie-breaker. However, a tie-breaker may conclude before the three rounds have been completed, when a match terminating event, such as technical fall, fall, forfeit, or injury default, has taken place.

    Match points are accumulative within tie-breaker matches. As such, a technical fall may take place when the accumulation of points reaches a margin of 15 points. For this reason, the team-advancement tie-breaker can be thought of as a super match with three two-minute periods whereby each period is contested at a different weight classification with athletes starting in the neutral position.

    Should the tie-breaker finish the third period with a tied accumulative score, the tie-breaker will continue with an additional period until the tie-breaker has a result of either a terminating event or ends a period with an accumulative point advantage for one team.

    Riding advantage time, injury time, cautions, and stalling will carry over from period to period and be accumulative with the same applications as within a single match.

    Riding advantage time will carry over from period to period and will award an additional point when any team has an advantage of one minute at the end of the third tie-breaker period or any subsequent period. In the event that one minute of riding advantage time is awarded and results in a tie in the accumulative team points, riding time will be reset to zero, no matter the previous time advantage. An additional point may be awarded at any additional tie-breaker period when accumulative riding advantage reaches one minute.

    Tie-breaker match order is to be selected by the following:

    • All the weights contested in the dual will be ordered based on the margin of victory.
      • Margin of victory is calculated by the difference in points in the individual match. (Example: A match ending in a 3-2 decision would be a one-point margin.)
      • Matches that end by Fall will be given a margin of 16 points.
      • Weights that ended by Injury Default, Disqualification, or Forfeit will not be included in a tie-breaker.
    • When two weights have the same margin of victory the weight first contested will be the first tie-breaker held.
    • Should all (available) weights be contested in the tie-breaker, the tie-breaker will restart the cycle with the weight that started the first tie-breaker. Tie-breakers continue until a winner is determined.

    Notes:

    • Tie-breakers do not meet the criteria for Wrestling Rule 3.1.5(c) Extra Match and thus should not be considered individual matches. The result of a team tie-breaker is NOT to be included on an athlete's season record form. Tie-breakers are for determining the team result only.
    • Athlete's are not limited to competing in only one tie-breaker but if an athlete competes in more than one tie-breaker for the same team competition, then Wrestling Rule 3.22.17 Rest Between Matches would apply and the athlete would be restricted from competing in another tie-breaker until rest time has passed.
    • This method must be used for breaking ties in all team-advancement tournaments, including the NCAA Wrestling Championship.
    Benefits, Tie-Breaker (Team Advancement)

    A tie in team sports may be the most accurate reflection of the team's performance, and it should be acknowledged as such. However, it is not a satisfactory result for programs, and this often leads to frustration, which has been cited as a reason to eliminate ties. Nevertheless, this frustration can be valuable in creating exciting rivalries through spoilers which help to foster an attachment and investment into the identity of programs (Reimer, 2011).

    It is worth noting that ties are commonplace in team contests across various sports, including NCAA soccer and ice hockey (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2020a; 2022n) and even in the NFL on occasion (Molski, 2021). These ties have not prevented the assessment of team performances or the determination of conference or national standings. In fact, the NCAA recently changed its soccer rules for the 2022-23 school year to eliminate overtime for regular-season contests, a move that may result in even more ties (Saint Louis University Athletics, 2022).

    However, there are instances where a tie-breaker becomes necessary, such as determining which team advances in team advancement tournaments. In such situations, it is best to use actual competition between athletes specifically designed to decide the team contest. This process eliminates the current practice of using statistical analysis to determine the winner of a tied team contest. Rather than relying on the scorer's table to count takedowns, the result of the team contest will be placed back into the hands of the competitors, with team advancement determined based on competition on the mat.

    This approach provides fans with the opportunity to witness more wrestling in a close contest and enables athletes to step up and secure advancement for their program. By prioritizing the weight class with the closest margin of victory, the team result can be determined by re-contesting the closely contested weight classes for the specific purpose of deciding the team result. This creates a tie-breaking match in which multiple athlete are able to contribute and have an impact on the results so that not only one athlete has the responsibility of determining the team result. However, athletes may single handily determine the team result by securing a terminating result (such as a fall or tech fall).

    This format allows for an additional dynamic within the team competition for athletes and fans in which additional wrestling in close team competitions is used to determine tie-breakers.

    As a tie-breaker does not count as a match on an athlete's individual season record form, coaches are incentivized to move athletes into non-traditional weight classes based on specific matchups, leading to more unique and exciting matchups without risking a loss on an athlete's season record. This approach not only enhances the overall quality of the sport but also provides a fair and exciting way to break ties in team contests.

D. Other Administration

    1. Countable Matches, Division I Opponents

    Only competitions against rostered athletes competing attached to Division I institutions shall count as Division I opponents. Any competition against any rostered Division I athlete competing unattached shall not be counted as a Division I opponent, though the athlete may still be counted as an intercollegiate competition, the same as competition against another collegiate athlete (NAIA, Division II, Division III, NJCAA, etc.).

    Benefits, Countable Matches, Division I Opponents

    The process for qualification to the NCAA (Individual) Championship requires wrestlers be compared against the field of Division I competitors. Though unattached athletes compete directly with collegiate athletes and can affect the outcome of matches or tournaments, they are not representing Division I programs when competing unattached. As such, these matches should not factor into the process for qualification. Though they may be collegiate students, when competing as unattached athletes, they are not competing as Division I athletes. This provision ensures that better comparisons are made between Division I eligible athletes based on the field of athletes that competed with the support of their Division I institutions. This means that the results which involve unattached competitors will not be included or considered when calculating matches completed, RPI, or coaches ranking.

    The RPI and Division I Win Percentages will be improved by omitting the unattached competitors which are prone to inconsistent results due to the lack of support. This improves the accuracy and reliability of the comparison metrics used for qualification purposes.

    2. Entering a Competition - Athlete Competition Commitment

    According to Rule 3.9 Weigh-In Sheet Requirements, if an entrant is listed on the NCAA Official Weigh-In Form but does not attempt to make weight, it must be recorded as "Did Not Enter (DNE)" on the form.

    The act of weighing in for a competition signifies an athlete's consent to compete within the framework of the competition. When an athlete passes the medical check and weighs in for any individual competition, such as an individual advancement tournament, open event, pool/round-robin event, or extra matches, they have entered the competition and are bound to the matches defined in the competition format. The matches they are then assigned within the event become their committed opponents. Just as with committed athletes in team competitions, athletes are committed to competitions for which they have entered.

    Updates to Rules Book

    This provision will require some changes to the Wrestling Rules Book with those changes reflected by any additions being underlined in green while items that are removed are struck out in red and any current portion of the rule that is being emphasized will be marked in bold.

    When an athlete is unwilling or unable to compete in a competition they have entered, they may use one of the following methods of withdrawing from their competition provided within the NCAA Wrestling Rulebook under Rule 2, Section 3: Determining the Winner.

        Art. 9. Default — Injury. An injury default is awarded in a match when one of the wrestlers is unable to continue due to an injury or by choice of their coach. An injury default shall be included as a win or loss in each wrestler’s individual season record.

        Art. 11. Forfeit. A forfeit is received by a wrestler when the opponent, for any reason, fails to appear for the match. (Exception: Incidences in which a medical forfeit has been declared criteria has been met.) In order to receive a forfeit, the non-forfeiting wrestler must be dressed in a competition uniform and appear on the mat. A forfeit shall be included as a win in the victor's individual season record and a loss for the individual forfeiting. Wrestlers shall not accept a forfeit in one weight class and compete in another class. In tournament competition, forfeits advance in a bracket until they meet an opponent. (See Table 4.11.1.)

    If an event would require multiple losses in order to exit the event, an athlete may utilize the following method to withdraw from subsequent matches:

        Art. 12. Medical Forfeit. A medical forfeit is an action that allows a competitor who fails medical examinations, is injured, or becomes ill during the course of tournament competition to forfeit their match(es) without having to weigh in (see Rule 9.1.8 for medical forfeit and weigh-ins for national qualifying events) or show up at the mat. In tournament competition, medical forfeits advance in a bracket until they meet an opponent.

          a. In order for a medical forfeit to be official, the medical personnel, or an authorized institutional representative, shall inform the tournament director before the ill or injured competitor is called to the mat provided the following criteria are met: the competitor's previously contested match in the event was recorded as a loss by Default — Injury or Forfeit and the criteria in Rule 9.6.3 was met for the previous match. If the previous match was one defined in Rule 9.6.4*, then the match must not be recorded as a medical forfeit and Rule 2.3.11 Forfeit should be applied.

          b. Medical forfeits count as a win for the opponent and do not count as a loss for the forfeiting wrestler. Even though medical forfeits do not count as a loss, they must be placed on the wrestler's Individual Season Record Form in the Trackwrestling system.

            * Rule 9.6.4 Non-Allowable ISRF Matches. Matches between teammates, against opponents who are not listed on an institutional roster, a club-level wrestler, a prospective student-athlete, or an armed services student-athlete shall not be counted (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2021f).

    The use of the medical forfeit allows athletes to withdraw from competition after they have taken a loss at the event without needing to be charged with further losses.

    The type of previous match will determine which match gets recorded as a loss if an athlete is competing at an open event and the losing match was against a non-collegiate. In this instance, the loss would be recorded against the next scheduled collegiate athlete.

    This provision does not change the result of the open competition as both matches would be recorded as advancements for the other athlete. The only change would be at the administrative level within the collegiate program and how they enter the results within the Trackwrestling system.

    The intended purpose of this provision is to minimize any gaming of bout assignments to include matches against non-collegiate athletes as a mechanism for athletes to withdraw from a competition without impacting their season record form.

Benefits, Athlete Competition Commitment - Entering a Competition

This system of binding athletes to the competition terms of an event better aligns the expectations for competition with the terms of the event. When an athlete enters a competition, they know that failing to complete the event undefeated will result in a loss. If an athlete does not want to enter an event due to the size of the field or the demands of the competition, they are free to enter other events or have their coaching staff schedule extra matches for them alongside their team competitions.

The purpose of this provision is to ensure that when athletes enter competition and have matches scheduled, they are fully committed to the event. While this provision may seem punitive, it helps to communicate results more accurately at events. Athletes who suffer losses by forfeit, as well as those who win by forfeit, will not have their seeding and committee selections impacted any differently than they are in the current medical forfeit provisions. The only difference will be in the reflection on their record, which will be a more accurate representation of their performance against the events on their schedule.

Event organizers may choose to have more limited consolation wrestle-backs if they choose to limit when an event terminates. But the terms of the competition must be universally applied to all athletes and defined prior to competition.

Losses by forfeit will be omitted from an athlete's record when calculating RPIs in order to minimize the impact on comparisons of athletes based on competitions that did not take place.

3. Team Point Deductions

One of the more perplexing provisions in the Wrestling Rules Book is the use of team point deductions as a punitive measure. This concept is quite foreign to sports, as it allows a competition official to unilaterally alter a team's score based on their judgment, and it contradicts the modern sporting emphasis on the accuracy of statistics and historical records.

While this system was adopted due to the challenges in managing team scoring for individual-centric tournaments, this process must be phased out.

In most other NCAA sports, penalties are handled in a manner that does not directly impact the competition's outcome. This is achieved by enforcing rules at the individual level, rather than the team level.

Wrestling will adopt similar policies by utilizing established procedures from NCAA baseball, soccer, and softball to establish a new framework for enforcing infractions that were previously penalized with team point deductions.

The following section will include the changes to the rules as pertaining to the current team point deduction.

Additions to the Rulebook are underlined in green while items that are removed are struck out in red and any current portion of the rule that is being emphasized will be marked in bold.

    Rule 3 - Conduct of Meets and Tournaments

      Section 2. Competitors, Coaches and Medical Personnel Attire

        Art. 1. Competitors' Attire. For all competition, it is required that all clothing worn by wrestlers in the competition area be either unadorned (plain) or of the representative institution’s school colors. If the item contains a logo, it is required that it be of the school that is represented. Coaches and competitors should be aware of NCAA bylaws in regard to manufacturer's and distributor's names and logos. Hats, stocking caps or other inappropriate apparel are not permitted. Violations shall result in the head coach being penalized with a control of mat area violation a Level I control of mat area violation (warning) shall be issued to each violator to correct the condition or leave the team area. If the violator(s) do not immediately comply, the violator(s) shall be issues a Level II control of mat area violation (disqualified and ejected). The head coach is to be notified of any violations. If the coaching staff has vacated the reserved zone, the referee shall inform the tournament committee, which will be responsible for notifying the institution. Any subsequent attire violations by a competitor or member of the coaching staff during the event shall automatically be considered a Level II control of mat area violation resulting in immediate disqualification and ejection for the violator(s). (Softball Rule 3.1.2 adapted)

        Art. 2. Coaches' Attire. The wearing of jeans, T-shirts, sweatshirts/sweatpants and noninstitutional-logoed warm-up suits (tops and/or bottom), headwear and similar apparel are not suitable attire for coaches during competition and, therefore, are prohibited. Institutional-logoed tops/jackets are permissible. Each coach in violation of this rule shall be penalized with a control of mat area violation issued a warning with notice of the warning being provided to the school's Athletic Director. Any subsequent attire violations by any member of the team during the event are to result in immediate disqualification and ejection for the violator(s). For the purpose of the event violation sequence, multiple violations at the time of first warning shall be considered simultaneous violations. (See Rule 2.2.15) Any member of the coaching staff issued two warnings within one season must serve a one contest suspension to be served during the next scheduled event on the program's published schedule. Further, the coach(es) in violation shall not sit in the designated reserved zone for coaching if the prohibited apparel is not replaced with suitable attire. (Softball Rule 3.1.2 adapted)

        Art. 3. Medical Personnel Attire. Medical personnel must be appropriately attired. The wearing of shorts, jeans, T-shirts, sweatshirts/sweatpants and noninstitutional-logoed warm-up suits (top and/or bottom), headwear and similar apparel are not suitable attire for medical personnel during dual and tournament competition and, therefore, are prohibited. Individuals not adhering to this rule shall be penalized with a control of mat area violation issued a warning with notice of the warning provided to the school's Athletic Director. Medical personnel unable to immediaty resolve the attire issue are to be restricted to the designated spectator area for the remainder of the event, including multi-day events. The member of the team’s medical staff shall continue to serve the team’s medical needs.

      Section 11. Intentional Delay During Premeet Period A team intentionally delaying its appearance on the mat beyond five three (3) minutes from the established meet starting time shall have one team point deducted be issued a team caution.

        Art. 1. Team Caution The match official will issue a visual caution, signified by a “C” formed by the forefinger and thumb at the time of issuance. Team cautions are to be handled in the following manner:

        1. First violation
          1. The match official will instruct the score keeper to immediatley begin a two minute count down. Once the two minutes has elapsed, if the team is not present, a second team caution is to be issued;
          2. When a meet is contested following the issuance of a team caution, with each prematch period, a visual caution, signified by a “C” formed by the forefinger and thumb is to be issued for each match. This caution is to be applied as a first violation in the starting position penalty sequence. (Team cautions do not carry over to another meet within a team advancement tournament or muti-day competition.)
        2. Second violation
          1. The offending team has forfeited the meet which should be recorded as a loss 1-0 for the offending team and a win for their opponent (Baseball Rule 5.12.a adopted);
          2. All component matches within the match are to be recorded as a forfeit in adherance with Wrestling Rule 2.3.11 for all entrants based on each team's lineup declaration;

      Section 19 Art. 4. Failure to Prove a Rule Misapplication. Failure to prove a rule misapplication will be treated in the following manner:

      1. First violation — Level I violation warning to the institution;
      2. Second violation — Level I violation warning to the institution;
      3. Third violation and all subsequent violations — deduct one team point Level II violation (disqualification and ejection) is issued to the acting head coach for the event. Failure to prove a rule misapplication violations are cumulative for each institution throughout a dual meet, single- and multi-day tournaments, and both dual team- and individual-advancement events.

      Section 20. Control of Mat Area

        Art. 2. Reserved Zone — Tournaments. A maximum of three team personnel are permitted in the reserved zone. If three team personnel are used, two must hold coaching credentials and one must be properly credentialed medical personnel. Unauthorized team personnel in the reserved zone shall be penalized as a control of mat area violation disqualified and ejected from the event. (Note: The penalty only needs to be enforced against the number of violators necessary to become in compliance with the rule. Any of the individuals in the reserved zone may serve the penalty provided the number of individuals serving the penalry would have resulted in compliance with the rule) .

        Art. 4. Leaving the Reserved Zone. Coaches may leave the reserved zone only:

        1. To request a review of the timing and/or scoring;
        2. To question the referee's application of a rule;
        3. During a video review challenge;
        4. During a charged timeout; and
        5. At the end of the match, however, the coach may never enter the competition circle. Entering the competition circle is an automatic level II control of mat area violation.

        Art. 6. Badgering. Badgering the referee by the coaching staff or any member of the team shall not be permitted. Failure to comply shall be considered a control of mat area violation based on the following applications:

        • Head Coach
          • When the first badgering violation for a team at any event is issued to the head coach a Level I (warning) control of mat area violation is to be issued to the head coach.
          • When a badgering violation has already been issued to a team at an event and a head coach is issued a violation for badgering, the violation is to be a Level II (disqualification and ejection) control of mat area violation.
        • All other team members
          • Any badgering violation is an automativc Level II (disqualification and ejection) control of mat area violation. Note: Within tournament competition, if the head coach is not present for a match then any violation at the match would result in a Level II control of mat area violation. Each program must designate one person to act as the head coach at the event. Failure to designate a head coach will result in all violations resulting in a Level II violation for the team.

        Art. 7. Control of Mat Area Violation — Penalty Sequence. For the first violation and all subsequent violations, one team point shall be deducted. The referee shall indicate the control of mat violation by displaying the control of mat violation signal and verbally informing the offending coach of the violation. (See Rule 2.2.15 for simultaneous violations.) The official will then report to the violation to the scorers table to confirm the appropriete level applied for the violation.

    Rule 5 Infractions

      Section 2. Unsportsmanlike Conduct

        Art. 3. Wrestler — Prematch or Postmatch Period. An unsportsmanlike conduct violation for a wrestler during the prematch or postmatch period shall result in the deduction of one team point issuance of a Level I (warning) violation for the first violation; the penalty for the second violation outside of a match and during the same dual meet or tournament session will be the issuance of a Level II violation (disqualification and ejection) , the deduction of one team point and removal from the premises for the remainder of the event. In addition to using the appropriate signal, the referee shall verbally notify a member of the offending coaching staff immediately after the violation. If the coaching staff has vacated the reserved zone, the referee shall inform the tournament committee, which will be responsible for notifying the institution. Additional unsportsmanlike conduct violations that occur by the same wrestler before the verbal notification are considered simultaneous violations. (See Rule 2.2.15.)

        Art. 5. Noncompeting Wrestler and Personnel - Prematch, Match or Postmatch Period. An unsportsmanlike conduct violation by coaches, athletic trainers, managers, physicians and noncompeting wrestlers shall result in a Level I (warning) violation the deduction of one team point for the first violation; the penalty for the second violation will be the issuance of a Level II violation (disqualification and ejection) , the deduction of one team point and removal from the premises. In addition to using the appropriate signal, the referee shall verbally notify a member of the offending coaching staff immediately after the violation. If the coaching staff has vacated the reserved zone, the referee shall inform the tournament committee, which will be responsible for notifying the institution. Additional unsportsmanlike conduct violations that occur by the same individual before the verbal notification are considered simultaneous violations. (See Rule 2.2.15.)

        Art. 6. Accumulation of Penalties. The penalties for unsportsmanlike conduct are cumulative throughout a dual meet or a tournament session for noncompeting wrestlers, coaches, athletic trainers, managers and physicians. In-match unsportsmanlike conduct calls for competitors are cumulative within that match.

      Section 5. Flagrant Misconduct

        Art. 2. Wrestler — Prematch, Match or Postmatch Period — Dual Meet. A flagrant misconduct violation shall be penalized by a Level II (disqualification and ejection) violation the deduction of one team point, disqualification of the wrestler, removal from the premises for the remainder of the event, declaration of the opponent as the winner and the award of six points to the opponent’s team. (Also see Rule 5.5.9 for mandatory suspension requirements.)

        Art. 3. Wrestler — Prematch, Match or Postmatch Period — Tournament. A flagrant misconduct violation during a tournament shall be penalized by a Level II (disqualification and ejection) violation the deduction of one team point, removal from the premises for the remainder of the event, declaration of the opponent as the winner and two team points awarded for advancement. Additionally, the wrestler charged with the flagrant misconduct will not be credited with points earned before the incident, nor receive placement points or an individual tournament award. Other wrestlers will remain in their respective positions on the bracket. In dual-team advancement tournaments, the flagrant misconduct penalty points are only deducted for the session in which the offense occurred. (Also see Rule 5.5.9 for mandatory suspension requirements.)

        Art. 4. Noncompeting Wrestler. A flagrant misconduct violation shall be penalized the same as if the noncompeting wrestler were a competing wrestler. (Also see Rule 5.5.9 for mandatory suspension requirements.)

        Art. 5. Institutional Representative. If the offender is an institutional representative other than the competing and noncompeting wrestlers and medical staff, the penalty shall be a Level II (disqualification and ejection) violation the deduction of one team point, and the offender shall be removed from the premises for the remainder of the event, including multi-day events. (Also see Rule 5.5.9 for mandatory suspension requirements.)

        Art. 6. Institutional Medical Staff. If the offender is a member of the team’s medical staff, the penalty shall be a Level II (disqualification and ejection) violation the deduction of one team point and the offender shall be restricted to the designated spectator area for the remainder of the event, including multi-day events. The member of the team’s medical staff shall continue to serve the team’s medical needs. (Also see Rule 5.5.9 for mandatory suspension requirements.)

Benefits, Eliminating Team Point Deductions

The elimination of the team point deduction is a significant change within the sport of wrestling. This modification will enable match officials to manage matches more efficiently, as they will no longer need to issue a team point deduction as their first violation. This change allows officials to set their standard and communicate their expectations to teams by issuing a warning.

Moreover, eliminating the team point deduction means that officials will not be making subjective judgments that directly affect the scoring of events. This will result in a more consistent standard of scoring and allow teams to respond better to the expectations of officials. It also ensures that a team's performance is solely based on their skills and tactics rather than the subjective interpretation of a match official.

By implementing these changes, wrestling can create a fairer and more transparent competition environment, which aligns with the modern sporting society's emphasis on accuracy and fairness. These changes will also encourage greater sportsmanship and encourage athletes to compete to the best of their abilities, free from the worry of potential subjective rulings that could significantly impact the match outcome.

While it is true that in regular season dual meets all weight classes will be contested, the selection of order for weight classes may impact on the athletes that a coach decides to use within those weight classes.

This system provides flexibility for programs while giving a strategic competition dynamic to the team competitions that is not currently in place today.
