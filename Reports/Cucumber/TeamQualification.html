V. NCAA Wrestling Championship: Team Championship

The event where a team champion is crowned for NCAA Division-I Wrestling is to be called the NCAA Wrestling Championship to signify that one singular champion will be crowned by virtue of the event's result (31.02.2.1).

A. Founding of Intercollegiate Competition and Team Championships

In 1749, Benjamin Franklin, wrote in his proposal for the founding of the University of Pennsylvania that the students should "be frequently exercised in running, leaping, wrestling and swimming." His proposal even included plans for a wrestling facility to be on the campus (Kantemneni, 2021). And while collegiate wrestling became commonplace in the centuries that followed, competition was largely confined within the campus as an intramural or recreational activity with each school having their own versions of rules for competition (McCrary, 1967)

There is some degree of debate about the date of the first intercollegiate competition. There are publications that have made the claim that the first intercollegiate competition was a dual meet, in 1900, between Yale University and the University of Pennsylvania (Keen et al., 1964). Yet, due to a lack of documentation of such a contest, the generally cited first intercollegiate dual meet is the March 21, 1903 contest between Yale University and Columbia University which finished in a 2-2 draw of the four weight divisions contested (Steinman, 2003; Hammond, 2006).

No matter when the first intercollegiate competition was held, it is widely accepted the origins of intercollegiate competition stem from the men of one school challenging the men from another school to determine the superior wrestling school through a head-to-head team dual meet (Steinman, 2003).This practice of intercollegiate dual meet competition continued to expand throughout the East in the decade following those first matches with team results being well documented.

Independently, almost in unison, and likely organically as in the East, intercollegiate competition spread through the Midwest, led by Cornell College (Iowa) and the University of Wisconsin, Madison (Sapora, 1940).

This popularity of dual meet competitions between schools created a need to adopt standardized rules for competition. Columbia, Pennsylvania, Princeton, and Yale took the initiative to create standards for wrestling in establishing the Eastern Intercollegiate Wrestling Association (EIWA) as the first association to govern intercollegiate wrestling competition. The EIWA's first championship was held in 1905 with each of the four founding schools competing (Keen et al., 1964).

Soon after, in 1909, the Western Intercollegiate Gymnastics Association (known as the Western Conference which would become the Big Ten Conference) added Fencing and Wrestling to its program. With this addition, the Midwest established standardized rules and began sponsoring an annual championship in 1913 for schools in the region under the new name of the Western Intercollegiate Wrestling, Gymnastics and Fencing Association. Soon after, each of the charter schools of the Big Ten Conference officially began intercollegiate competition (Case, 1964).

To this day, the Big Ten Conference recognizes conference members who won the Western Intercollegiate Wrestling, Gymnastics and Fencing Association's annual championship as Big Ten Champions (Big Ten Conference, 2006). On occasion, the Big Ten has elected to recognize the team with the best regular season dual meet record against conference foes as the Big Ten Conference champion. Such as, in 1922, where Indiana has been recognized as the Big Ten Champion after finishing the season with three wins and one loss against conference opponents (Indiana Athletics, 2021). It's worth noting that in the same year, the Western Intercollegiate Championship was won by Iowa State, a school that was not a member of the Big Ten Conference (Iowa State Athletics, 2015).

By 1924, with intercollegiate dual meet competitions held all over the United States, there were five regional collegiate wrestling associations (EIWA, Western, Southern Conference, Northwest Intercollegiate, and Missouri Valley Conference) each with their own rules and holding their own championship (Sapora, 1940).

This regional divide within the sport, resulted in a need for a national rules committee to create uniform standards. The first national rules committee was formed in 1927 under the umbrella of the National Collegiate Athletic Association (Case, 1964).

Following the formation of collegiate wrestling standards by the NCAA, each of the regional associations adopted the NCAA's rules (Nelson, 1974). This uniformity within the sport made it possible for the first national intercollegiate wrestling championship to be held by the NCAA in 1928. Wrestling was the third sport to have a championship sponsored by the NCAA, which had been created in 1906 to establish uniform ethical standards within collegiate sports (Crowley, n.d.). These early NCAA wrestling championships featured no team scoring. The committee had debated including a team component and decided against it, to not distract from the purpose of the event which was individual competition to establish a "Champion of Champions" at each weight division. The NCAA marketed the first event as the "Individual Wrestling Championships" (Hammond, 2006).

Six years later, the NCAA had officially formalized a team scoring system which was overlayed onto the 1934 individual championships as to not require any change in the individual competition format, providing an avenue for determining a national intercollegiate team champion which has remained a part of the championships ever since (National Collegiate Athletic Association, n.d. -j).

The regionalized structure of the sport and lack of a national championship event did not prevent schools from claiming national team titles before the NCAA's creation of a championship. One of, if not the first, schools to claim the distinction of national team champion is Penn State University (Penn State Athletics, n.d. -a). Penn State has long recognized a national intercollegiate wrestling team championship for the 1921 season.

Following their win of the EIWA Championship in March, Penn State barnstormed the Midwest to face the region's top programs. Penn State found success against Indiana (32-14), who had recently won the Western Championship (Indiana Athletics, 2021; Penn State Athletics, n.d. -b) and knocked off the previously undefeated Iowa State team, believed to be the strongest in the Midwest, by a score of 28-18 (Iowa State Athletics, 2015). Based on the results of these two dual meets Penn State claimed their national championship honors.

Despite this championship claim, Amateur Wrestling News does not recognize Penn State as the top collegiate program for the 1921 season. That distinction is bestowed on the Naval Academy (Sayenga, 1979). Navy had completed their third consecutive undefeated season and defeated Penn State 19-6 one week prior to Penn State's win at the EIWA championship (Navy Sports, n.d.). However, as was the case with many schools at the time, despite adopting the EIWA's rules for wrestling, Navy was not a member of any association and thus did not compete at the EIWA championships.

Unlike the Westerns which hosted an open tournament for colleges in the region, the EIWA only permitted their members to compete in their championship. And gaining membership could prove difficult. Penn State was admitted to the EIWA as the seventh member after eight years of applying for membership (Penn State Wrestling Club Centennial Committee, 2008).

The intercollegiate competition structure of team dual competitions throughout the season, followed by regional and/or national individual competitions using team scoring was the direct result of the limitations of the era in which intercollegiate wrestling was founded. This structure has persisted, largely unchanged, to this day.

B. Current Team Championship Issues

    Bonus and Advancement Points

    The current scoring system at the NCAA wrestling championships, which includes bonus points, advancement points, and placement points, may not accurately reflect the performance of individual athletes or their teams. Placement points are earned based on the success of an athlete within the context of the event and provide a weighted value representation of the athlete's relative standing against the championship field. Of the points awarded, placement points are the best representation of the performance of the athlete within the context of the individual national championship. Bonus and advancement points, on the other hand, are awarded to a team based on the success of an athlete against a specific individual opponent. These points, unlike placement points, do not provide any useful information about the overall quality of the athlete or team that won or was defeated, yet they are factored into the event's team standings as if they do. And these points can have a significant impact on the event standings. According to Lee Cothran (2016), "bonus points are where championships are won."

    Bonus points in wrestling competitions are intended to motivate teams and athletes to perform at a higher level, but there is little evidence to suggest that these incentives have this effect. In fact, research suggests that the opposite may be true. According to Knoeber and Thurman (1994), "tournament theory has shown that those who are higher performers will take less risks within a tournament setting due to the high negative cost to them." Within wrestling, a match can end quickly and suddenly by fall, as a result, elite athletes are reluctant to put themselves in positions where they might lose, as doing so could end their chances of winning an individual championship.

    Wrestlers at the championship are primarily focused on achieving their own individual goals, such as becoming a National Champion or All-American. This creates a misalignment of incentives between the individual wrestlers and the teams they represent. The primary motivation for the athlete is to achieve their individual goals and the team championship is secondary or even ancillary.

    The double-elimination format of the NCAA Wrestling tournaments, which requires athletes who lose earlier in the competition to have a longer path with more matches required for placement, also leads to more cautious and reserved competition, as overexerting oneself can risk an athlete's long-term performance in the longer double elimination competition (Deck & Kimbrough, 2015).

    While the use of bonus points may provide more data for comparison and help to distinguish between team performances in the competition, it can also harm most programs within the sport.

    This issue is magnified within the context of matches contested on Thursday within the first two sessions of the NCAA Wrestling Championships. A total of 250 wrestlers (75% of the total field) will exit the NCAA championships with one or fewer matches won (National Collegiate Athletic Association, n.d. -aj). Which means the importance of seeding is heightened for programs with fewer chances to score points for the team race, creating perverse incentives for the regular season. For most programs within the sport, the first day at the championship is their best, and possibly only, chance at scoring any points.

    Each team represented by an individual athlete at the championships will finish with a team score. This score has become a definitive and objective score that signals the program's performance for the season to stakeholders, including administrators. For many programs their season's performance will be judged by these points and the subsequent team standings they produce at the championships.

    For a sport where roughly two-thirds of programs earn fewer than ten points at the championship, each point earned within the first day of competition is significant. And yet, when a team's points are primarily earned from advancement points, as is the case with teams outside of the team race, it is when the team results are the most misleading. This issue is further exacerbated by the imbalance of brackets due to the inclusion of pigtail matches.

    There is a dynamic which is observable at championship after championship in which two athletes in the same weight class end the championship with identical records of one win and two losses. With each athlete's win coming by decision against the very same opponent. And yet, despite both athletes having an otherwise identical result against the same identical athlete, one team is awarded one point while the other team is only awarded half a point.

    No other NCAA sport has a system of calculating team scores which includes advancement points or bonus points like wrestling does. Men's Tennis was the last NCAA sport to use advancement points before eliminating the method of scoring in 1976 (National Collegiate Athletic Association, n.d. -ah).

    Within individual sports, the exclusive use of placement points is common for calculating team standings. This system has the benefit of clustering many non-scoring teams together at the bottom of the standings. This clustering provides security for programs as they are surrounded by a high quantity of peer programs who performed, based on the points awarded, at a similar level with no distinctions made between members of the group.

    For example, 51% of all participating teams failed to score a team point at each of the men's and women's 2021 NCAA Outdoor Track and Field Championships (National Collegiate Athletic Association, n.d. -ai), resulting in most of the programs represented at the championship being clustered together. However, within wrestling, the delineation between programs is much more granular due to advancement and bonus points, allowing for further distinctions between the programs near the bottom of the standings.

    This distinction between programs outside of the top programs adds no value to the sport. At the 2022 NCAA Wrestling Championships, six of the represented teams failed to score any team points and found themselves at the bottom of the team standings (TrackWrestling, n.d. -a). Two of these six programs finished the season with records of 9-4 (Ohio Athletics, n.d. -a) and 12-6 (Buffalo Athletics, n.d. -a) in team dual competitions. Despite delivering quality performances throughout the season, these programs appear to have struggled in the postseason, resulting in their placement as one of the worst programs in the country. For these programs, and many others like them, even though they may have a competitive program with competitive athletes, the team scoring system that is overlaid on individual competitions unnecessarily creates a negative perception of the quality of their program.

    Difficulty in Fan Consumption of Championship Scoring

    The current format for scoring the team championship in wrestling is confusing for fans and spectators (Colucci, 2019). Not only does it differ significantly from the process used throughout the season to evaluate team performance, but the tournament structure makes it difficult for fans to understand their team's standing at any given moment during the championship competition. With many matches taking place simultaneously, it is very difficult for spectators to be able to track the current standings (Cothran, 2016). This is the same issue women's gymnastics faced which prompted reform to their championship structure (Giambalvo, 2019). This phenomenon has been described by Ryan Herrington (2014) as "a free-for-all tournament with too many protagonists for the layman to sift through."

    The NCAA Championships' focus on individual achievement over team scoring is evident in the promotional materials and information provided to educate spectators on the championship event. In fact, should someone want to understand the scoring, they wouldn't even be able to consult the NCAA Wrestling Rules Book since the NCAA Wrestling Championship has had a waiver in place since 2013 to not apply NCAA wrestling rule 4.8.7 for calculating advancement points for byes (Steen, 2017).     How does the scoring work?

    The scoring in the NCAA Division I wrestling tournament has changed over time. For the first five years of the tournament, a specific team scoring method did not exist. Oklahoma State won the first tournament in 1928 without team scoring because the team had the most individual champions, but the Cowboys then won the first tournament to include team scoring, in 1934, under the same coach that led them to the first title in 1928.

    Individual scoring also went through a series of changes during the first years of the tournament, but in 1941, the NCAA wrestling committee created a system where point values equated to different moves for individual athletes. Takedowns and reversals resulted in two points while escapes earned an athlete one point. Those with time advantages could also earn points, but stalling penalties were given to those delaying action. Even more changes were made in 1955, as athletes finishing first, second, third and fourth, scored seven, five, three and one point respectively.

    Today, athletes complete in a seven-minute match and can win in one of three ways. First, an athlete can score more points than their opponent through takedowns (two points), reversals (two points), escapes (one point), riding time (one point) and near falls (two or four points). Two, an athlete can earn a technical fall against their opponent, meaning that they scored more than 15 points than the opposing wrestler at any point during a dual. A technical fall ends the bout regardless of how much time remains. The final way to win a wrestling match is by fall, meaning that one wrestler controls another wrestler and puts both of his shoulder blades on the mat for two seconds. Like a technical fall, a fall, or pin, ends the match. If one wrestler has more points than the other, but then is pinned, the wrestler who is pinned always loses.

    Knowledge Requirement

    Men and women differ in their ways of expressing enthusiasm for sport and in their motivations for attending sporting events (Sutton & Knoester, 2022). These differences are evident within intercollegiate wrestling's spectator demographics where 75% of spectators are male (James & Ross, 2004). Men tend to enjoy sporting events more when they are well-informed about the context of the competition, including the athletes, teams, and brands involved (Rogers et al., 2017). Although men and women attend and watch sports at similar rates, men tend to spend more time learning about the sports they watch. Men who do not feel knowledgeable about a sport are less likely to consider themselves fans, even if they enjoy going to events and watching the sport (Dietz-Uhler et al., 2000).

    The individual-centric format for determining the team champion in wrestling requires a significant investment from spectators attending the event or watching from home. This is because the tournament format used for the wrestling team championship is different from that of other NCAA bracketed individual sports, such as tennis, golf, bowling, and gymnastics, where teams advance through the bracket to determine the team champion. In wrestling, individuals advance through the bracket to determine the team champion, which shifts the focus of the competition from the teams to the individual competitors. To be informed within the individual perspective of wrestling's championship, spectators must be knowledgeable about a multitude of competitors and their respective teams, as well as the dynamics between them. Which is problematic for the sport as attendees of intercollegiate sports have one of two primary points of attachment to competition; they are either attached to the program (school) or the sport (Trail et al., 2003). As noted by Kim et al. (2008), many spectators of collegiate sports watch competitions despite having no connection to the sport or the individual competitors, rather relying solely on their connection to the institutional brand. However, within collegiate wrestling, where championship competition is individual-centric, we see that those who attend events featuring top-ranked intercollegiate wrestlers tend to be loyalists of the sport (Cooper, 2009b).

    To fully enjoy the six consecutive sessions of the NCAA Wrestling championships, spectators must be knowledgeable about the vast field of competitors. This can be challenging for those who are not already loyal fans of the sport. Those who have a primary attachment to a team are less likely to watch the event, leaving the primary spectators to be the sport loyalists who are willing to make a significant investment due to their enjoyment of the sport. The dedication of these die-hard wrestling enthusiasts is exemplified by their willingness to invest their time and money to attend the championships. The sheer joy experienced by those present is unmistakable. This is evidenced by the fact that 48% of attendees traveled more than 500 miles to witness the 2014 championship (Palmer, 2015). These dedicated wrestling loyalists have contributed to the success of the championships.

    However, despite the widespread recognition of wrestling and its popularity as the sixth most popular high school sport for boys (Stanec, 2013), there is still a large gap between the sport's loyalists and potential fans who have not yet been developed or retained. Although many high school state championships sell out (Pilcher, 2013; Mitchell, 2022; Hoffman, 2022; KMTV, 2022), making wrestling the largest revenue generator for many state high school associations (Henderson, 2009), which has caused private arenas to give high school wrestling priority scheduling over NCAA Division I Men's Basketball Conference Championships (Shaw, n.d.), the intercollegiate model tailored to loyalists can result in a significant variance in the time spent as spectators, making wrestling loyalists one of the least dependable spectator bases for engagement (Mahony et al., 2002). Many wrestling loyalists have other priorities, such as family or work, including coaching youth or high school wrestling, which take precedence at different stages in their lives while still providing the wrestling fulfilment they crave as sport loyalists. Although they still identify as wrestling loyalists, they often lack the intimate knowledge of the competitors necessary to fully enjoy the individual-centric NCAA Wrestling Championships as a spectator. Relying solely on this limited pool of supporters creates a vacancy of support for the collegiate programs themselves.

    The issue of insufficient knowledge about competitors has long plagued individual sports. However, this issue is exacerbated within the collegiate model which limits athletes to four years of competition and thus requires a much greater effort to educate the consumer base about the constant changing faces within collegiate wrestling (Kaplan Hecker & Fink LLP, 2021-c). This is much less of an issue within professional enterprises where the athletes have much longer tenures providing a greater return on investment when marketing individual athletes.

    Mixed martial arts and the commercial success of the UFC are often touted as evidence of the individual model's success for combat sports. However, research on UFC programming consumption has revealed that American consumers are most interested in knowing fighters' backgrounds and histories before watching them compete (Kim et al., 2009; Zembura & Żyśko, 2015). Consequently, the UFC has taken significant steps to educate its consumer base about competitors within the promotion.

    "The Ultimate Fighter," a TV program about aspiring competitors, served as a catalyst for consumer support of the UFC as it educated them about the competitors (Tainsky et al., 2012). Programming like "UFC Unleashed" highlights the most popular fighters while educating consumers about other fighters they should know to remain interested in the UFC (Kim et al., 2009).

    The design of competition structures in professional individual sports is meant to prolong attachment to individuals in a way that cannot be replicated in the collegiate context. UFC's pay-per-view events, which feature the biggest names in the sport, have been criticized for their length and pacing (Fowlkes, 2014), but their structure and length is by design. All UFC branded events are extended events that require viewers to tune in early and become familiar with other competitors if they want to ensure they do not miss the headline competitions (Brantner, 2018). The pace of these events allows the community of consumers to turn the events into social events, further developing the community of consumers around the events (Kim, 2014). The level of social interaction at these events, not the competition itself, is the most accurate indicator of the spectator's intention to return to a future event (MacIntosh & Crow, 2011).

    By intentionally limiting the number of competitors in each event, UFC has found the audience becomes more informed about the competitors within the event. If they were to include more competitors, the audience would be overwhelmed by the number of new competitors they are learning about. This approach helps educate consumers about the UFC's newer signed athletes while they are watching the event for the competitors they already know.

    The length of the collegiate season makes it difficult to make the necessary investments to educate consumers. Professional seasons are much longer than collegiate seasons. For example, the Association of Tennis Professionals (ATP) plays an 11-month-long season (ATP, n.d.), while the Professional Golfers Association has no breaks in their calendar (PGA Tour, n.d.), and the UFC competes year-round with a major pay-per-view promotion scheduled each month (Way of Martial Arts, 2021).

    The extended seasons in professional individual sports enables a deeper familiarity with competitors, which is not feasible in the collegiate model where athletes' careers are limited by policy. This leads to a high turnover of athletes in collegiate competitions, while top professional athletes often compete for over a decade, even in combat sports. Consequently, consumers don't need to keep re-educating themselves about the ever-changing field of competitors in professional sports, which is necessary in collegiate wrestling.

    The collegiate model poses a significant barrier to potential consumers in gaining knowledge about individual competitors in the sport. Collegiate competition sees constant turnover of competitors due to the shortened timeline in which they can compete. Thus, the individual-centric model necessitates a far greater investment from collegiate programs than professional individual sports to generate interest in individual competitors.

    Even if campaigns to drive interest in individual competitors within a program were successful, the programs that made these investments to cultivate a following for individuals would be likely to lose these consumers to other entities because of the consumers' attachment being to the individual.

    When these former collegiate competitors move on to other college programs as coaches, become senior-level competitors at international competitions, or become professional athletes in other promotions such as MMA or WWE, the consumers are likely to follow the individuals and not remain consumers of the program. As a result, these programs are not likely to fully realize their investments, and the sport, as well as the programs within it, will continue to rely primarily on a limited number of loyalists as the primary consumer base.

    For these reasons, the success of MMA promotions, or other individual sports, should not be thought of as models to emulate for individual collegiate sports looking to expand and retain their consumer base.

    Season Continuity

    The use of two different team competition structures within intercollegiate wrestling, where teams are judged by both the team dual format and the tournament format, has created a complicated system that is confusing for those less familiar with the different competition structures. The complexity of this system makes it difficult for athletic department administrators, many of which have limited previous knowledge of wrestling, to effectively administer the program and hire the right leadership (Fair, 2017). The unique postseason structure, an outlier within NCAA sports, adds to the complexity of administering the sport, making it more challenging than necessary.

    Team Composition

    According to the NCAA Sports Sponsorship and Participation Rates Report (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2021g), the average Division I wrestling program carried a roster of 32 athletes in the 2021 season. And yet, this structure for determining a team championship (and national standing for programs) guarantees that only a minority of athletes will ever be able to compete during the postseason, even for those programs competing for a national team title.

    Since 2014, there have been fewer than three (2.75) programs a year, on average, represented at each weight class at the championships.


    There is a claim made by some within wrestling that programs with limited resources may benefit from a scoring system that provides the opportunity for teams to finish high in the team standings due to a few athletes reaching All-American or National Champion status. But it's unclear if these programs or schools receive any benefits for a higher team placement outside of the notoriety that comes from the individual accomplishments of their athletes' success which were the direct cause of these team accolades.

    This lack of roster inclusion for programs is a major structural issue within wrestling as any weight class that is not competitive at the national level is largely irrelevant to a program's national standing within wrestling's individual-centric framework.

    The current competition structure poses a problem, as programs with limited resources may choose to withdraw from one or two weight classes where they have performed below the median Division I athlete. This approach likely enhances their national standing and does not put them at a disadvantage during postseason competition. By forfeiting these less competitive weights, the program can redirect the resources previously used for coaching, travel, equipment, and scholarships to improve the performance of the other weight divisions that have a greater impact on the team standings during championship events. This strategy allows for a more focused allocation of resources on a smaller subset of athlete and an increased chance of success in the program's overall performance while greatly reducing the outward appearance of wrestling's viability at the school due to a lack of participation at weight divisions within team dual competitions.


    Program Building

    The current individual-centric championship model in college wrestling hinders program building. Not only does this model mean that there will be few, if any, competitions at the home venue with any postseason implications, but it also makes the individuals within the program, rather than the program itself, the main vehicle for the program's success. With only one individual being able to carry the program through the whole postseason at any weight class, there is a propensity of negative internal dynamics within the program (Kilduff et al., 2016) as individual self-interest leads to a decline in the cohesion of the program (Mawritz, 2018) reducing collaboration and leading to a decline in team performance (Chan, Li, & Pierce, 2014).

    Based on the points values at the last five championships, a National Champion is twenty (20) to fifty (50) times more valuable to the team's standings at the NCAA championships than a national qualifier who wins one match. As a result, some coaches and administrators believe that focusing on attracting and developing a few elite athletes to an All-American level will lead to success for the program. While this may lead to a high finish at the national championship, there is no evidence that it leads to long-term success for the team or builds a program.

    Becoming a superstar depends not just on ability, but also on sustained success and the accumulation of accolades, such as winning a championship (Yang & Shi, 2011). The limited eligibility within NCAA competition results in a shorter opportunity for programs to benefit from superstar athletes, as they will naturally exhaust their eligibility soon after establishing their superstar status (Kaplan Hecker & Fink LLP, 2021-a).

    The presence of a superstar athlete with significantly higher levels of talent than the rest of the athletes can have adverse effects on the performance of other teammates during training and competition (Brown, 2011). This phenomenon may be especially problematic for wrestling programs where practice with teammates is based on individual competition.

    Having a superstar athlete represent a program is more important to supporters of historically strong programs than to supporters of less successful programs (Hoegele et al., 2014a). This is because the motivations for supporting these programs differ. Supporters of historically successful programs tend to value the program's identity as a winner, while supporters of unsuccessful programs are more likely to be drawn to the community of supporters surrounding the program (Fisher & Wakefield, 1998). This makes sense as they would not continue to support an unsuccessful program if their primary motivation was the program's success. It's important to understand the unique nature of the program's support (Samra & Wos, 2015). Support for a program does not decline suddenly following a sudden decline in performance (Craig et al., 2017). Many supporters remain loyal to the program long term regardless of its level of success (Winegard & Deaner, 2010).

    It is not a team's wins and losses that start the building of a program, but rather the reputation and interpersonal connections of the individuals within the program and community of supporters that begin the building process (Fisher & Wakefield, 1998). To build a foundation of support for long-term success, the whole organization must be fostered (Fridley & Stokowski, 2022; Fitzpatrick, 2011).

    Wrestling's focus on one starter per weight-class has sometimes led to a perception within programs of a commitment to only one athlete. Becoming a significant hindrance to fostering the whole program, creating a cultural divide between a few athletes and the remaining majority that are the foundation of the program's long-term success (DeLong & Vijayaraghavan, 2003). This issue is one that hinders program development and can be further amplified when toxic individuals take on a superstar role (Housman & Minor, 2015) or coaches prioritize some individuals leading to a reduction in the level of support and instruction of training provided to others (Amorose & Horn, 2001). These issues can negatively shape the administration's perception of the program causing a divestment in wrestling (Fair, 2017).

    While all intercollegiate sports experience roster attrition when athletes are in their later years (Miller & Kerr, 2003), the attrition within wrestling is likely exacerbated by the individual-centric nature of the sport that reduces the quality of the athletic experience for many athletes (Rivera, 2004).

    Intra-squad competitions among evenly-matched student-athletes equally likely to compete in the postseason should promote consistent effort throughout the season. However, it is precicely when the competition is the most balanced that luck has the greatest impact on outcomes. And when luck has an outsized influence, student-athletes will perceive negative events as deterministic. Consequently, the already challenging competition for a postseason opportunity becomes even more daunting. This perceived influence of luck on competition results may further deter student-athletes, even the most team oriented and supportive of their teammates, causing them to reduce their motivation to stay engaged during negative events (Ryvkin & Drugov, 2000).

    When only one student-athlete is allowed to compete in a weight division for all four1 postseason championships, it can result in a lack of team identity and feelings of exclusion (whether real or perceived). This individual-centric nature of the sport may diminish the investment of student-athletes in the program. Furthermore, with the proliferation of Regional Training Centers in college programs, the significance of student-athletes as training partners has decreased despite their importance in the program's success (USA Wrestling, n.d.).

    This is significant for men's wrestling which due to the high percentage of first-generation athletes (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2016) and their attrition having a negative impact on the program's Academic Progress Rate (NCAA Research, 2022b).

    Lack of Rivalries

    Rivalries have been an integral part of intercollegiate sports since their inception. Intercollegiate wrestling can trace its founding to men from different schools challenging each other to determine which institution had the superior athletes on the mat (Steinman, 2003). This focus on rivalries between institutions persisted for many decades, as evident by Franklin & Marshall College prioritizing their long-standing rivalries over the opportunity for individual national championship opportunities in 1969 and 1970 (Gilbert, 1970). Their regional rivalries within the EIWA were viewed as more important than any individual awards or honors the NCAA's Collegiate Championship could provide.

    The significance of these rivalries was reaffirmed 44 years later when David L. Lehman, an alumnus of Franklin & Marshall College during the era when the program chose to skip the NCAA tournament to maintain their EIWA rivalries, made the largest ever gift to the school's athletic department, ensuring the endowment of the wrestling program for future generations (Paying His Debts, 2014). Instead of speaking to aspirations of individual accolades or national honors, Lehman spoke of his experience on a team that finished undefeated in freshmen dual meets and the rivalries the program had in dual meet team competitions, naming the specific regional schools that were important rivals (Franklin & Marshall College, 2014). Such rivalries serve as the standard for measuring athletic programs, leaving a lasting impact on both individuals and institutions alike. Sports Information Directors work diligently to preserve the records of team dual meets throughout an institution's history for this reason.

    While rivalries are not always mutually shared by both sides, they are influenced by a variety of factors such as geography, competitiveness, shared values, historical success, and competition for personnel, including recruits, students, coaches, and fans (Kilduff, 2014; Quintanar et al., 2015; Tyler & Cobbs, 2017). Rivalries are often complex and evolve over time (Benkwitz & Molnar, 2012).

    A key aspect of a rivalry is the emotional investment in the results of contests involving a rival, whereby supporters want their rival to fail, even when their preferred team is not involved in the competition (Havard, 2014). This investment and the associated emotions are measurable on fMRI brain scans with individuals experiencing real pain and pleasure based on the success and failures of their rivals (Cikara, Botvinick, & Fiske, 2011). The response to rivals extends beyond the two parties and can affect how individuals view third parties based on their affiliation with or reaction to the rival (Berendt & Uhrich, 2016). Interestingly, fans who share a rival can develop a sense of kinship and mutual respect as a result.

    In the past, intra-conference rivalries within collegiate wrestling were fostered by the competition for a limited number of qualification slots available to each conference and the politicking that could take place in their distribution, a dynamic that has changed with the shift towards a qualification system based on the current season's results and an individual's national standing (NCAA, 2009). While the new system is better overall, it has reduced the cultivation of intra-conference rivalries, which were once a hallmark of the sport (McLaughlin, 2009).

    The consolidation of conferences into larger regional entities has also had an impact on rivalries within the sport as the frequency of head-to-head competitions between conference teams has been reduced. Consequently, the culture between programs has become more collegial as they no longer compete for a finite number of bids based on the conference's historic results.

    These dynamics can make it challenging to cultivate and develop rivalries outside of binary contests between two parties, particularly within the individual-centric team scoring tournament structure employed in college wrestling's postseason events.

    Consider the following scenario: You are an avid supporter of a program competing in the NCAA Wrestling Championships, vying for the coveted NCAA Team Championship title. Over the years, you have had a fierce rivalry with another program, constantly battling for the top spot. However, since none of your athletes are currently competing, you find yourself watching matches featuring other teams. One such match is a consolation match with an athlete from your rival program facing off against an opponent from a team with which you have little familiarity or rivalry. The question arises: whom should you cheer for in this match?

    According to the literature on rivalries, fans want their rival team's athlete to lose, and lose significantly (Wann & Branscombe, 1990; Campbell, Aiken, & Kent, 2004). However, in the postseason competition format used by collegiate wrestling, the situation is not always so black and white. In this scenario, your rival's opponent is from a team that is also in the running for the team championship, along with your preferred program.

    This presents a dilemma. Do you cheer for your rival, hoping that their success will benefit your own program's chances of winning the team championship? Or do you cheer for your rival to lose, potentially risking your own program's chances of winning the team race?

    This dilemma is a common occurrence in the dynamic competition structure of the Division I Wrestling postseason. The conflicting dynamics of the scoring structure diminishes rivalries within the sport, muting team identification for fans. As a result, fans with high team identity are likely to become mere spectators of the competition, without a vested interest in the outcome of much of the competition as their only safe position is to cheer for their team's athletes and against everyone else.

    On the other hand, many other attendees particularly those that are wrestling loyalists, view the competitors as individuals, relatively free from their team affiliations, and support a competitor in a particular matchup based on their interest in the individual(s). For these fans, the team competition is not significant, and they are able to enjoy a broader range of competitions as fans of the sport. Though this approach is only possible for spectators without a vested interest in the team race who possess a deep knowledge of the field of competitors.

    The group of attendees who are most likely to thoroughly enjoy the NCAA Wrestling Championships are those who fall into the latter category. These individuals attend the event as true sports enthusiasts, deriving pleasure from each match of the competition. In contrast, those who are solely loyal to a particular team must endure lengthy periods of waiting for their team's turn to compete.

    Which is likely why attendees of dual team competitions, which have demonstrated a stronger regular season attendance than individual tournament competitions, have stated they are drawn to attend due to the team match-ups (Cooper, 2010).

C. New Championship Format

The format for determining the team championship was created nearly 100 years ago, due in large part to the limitations of the time. However, it is now possible to hold a team championship within the dual meet team competition structure which has been the primary method of competition within Division-I wrestling for nearly 100 years.

Interestingly, the NCAA rules book (Wrestling Rule 3.5.a) defines Team Competition as: "When one team competes against another team and utilizes the dual meet team score rules." And yet, the NCAA team Champion is not decided by the team competition method defined in the rules book.

The current competition format of Division I wrestling, while effective as an individual championship, is not ideal as a team championship in the modern sports era. The structure of the competitive season and the championship creates a convoluted sport that leaves much to be desired. To address this issue, the sport would benefit from returning to the NCAA tournament's origins as an individual championship (Hammond, 2006) and adopting a simpler structure that is more suited for team competition in the modern era. This will help to make the sport more easily understood and administered, as well as improving the student-athlete experience while being more appealing to spectators and potential fans.

The team-centric model will foster a sense of program identity and lead to a higher level of buy-in from athletes within the program. This creates a stronger foundation for long-term program success by providing a sustainable model for program building.

A postseason structure that provides more opportunities for student-athletes on the roster to participate and contribute to the team's success leads to better engagement and helps to build the program. Fans with high team identification support the team regardless of the outcomes of the competition (Yim & Byon, 2018). By changing the vehicle for team success to focus on the whole program rather than a select group of individuals, the focus shifts to fostering team identification. This change in perspective is crucial for cultivating a system of ongoing support from athletes within the program. The propensity for negative intra-team rivalries can be mitigated by an emphasis on the team identity as the vehicle for success when there is a path for more team members to have an opportunity to directly contribute to a team's postseason success (Kim & Glomb, 2014).

Elite athletes as alumni are significantly less likely to donate to their alma mater or former team as they believe the success they brought to the school was a major gift to the school (O'Neil & Schenke, 2007). These athletes understand that they could have represented many different institutions and the one they chose to represent benefited from their contributions. This feeling is further amplified for athletes when they are significant outliers within their program or when a program has elevated the athlete through marketing efforts. Furthermore, these marketing efforts may not be effective and in fact hinder long term support for a program (Hyman & Sierra, 2010). The fans you're likely to attract to the program based on the characteristics of the program are different than the devotees of the sport who would be attracted by the campaign. And since these are the people the who would likely already be aware of the athlete, "you're preaching to the choir" (Robinson et al., 2005). A structure which places an emphasis on elite individuals is one that is likely to stifle long term support for member programs compared to a structure where the emphasis is on the team results and the athlete's engagement within the team structure.


1. Automatic Qualification

    About

    Automatic Qualification is the process where an eligible NCAA Division-I Wresting Conference is provided with automatic entry for one of their member schools to the NCAA Wrestling Championship.

    Eligibility

    To be eligible for automatic qualification, a conference must:

    • Have at least six (6) Division I wrestling schools as members (18.5.1.a). A conference may be either a Core Multisport Conference (18.5.1.a), or a Single-Sport Conference (31.3.4.3.2).
      • Core Multisport Conferences with fewer than six members may, at the discretion of the Competition Oversight Committee, be granted Automatic Qualification waivers based on factors such as, but not limited to, the following (31.3.4.5.c):
        • Historic success of the conference membership in the sport and at the championship event.
        • Preservation of historic conference affiliation.
    • Hold conference team competitions and determine a conference champion, based on the results of the conference competition, prior to teams being selected for the NCAA Wrestling Championship (31.3.4.1.a).
      • Conferences electing to subdivide into divisions of five or more teams are required to conduct a single round-robin competition within each division and develop a process for determining the conference champion, which must be approved by the Wrestling Championship Committee prior to the start of the season (31.3.4.1.d, adopted).
        • Note: Though a postseason conference championship is permitted to determine the Conference Champion, one is not required (17.28.5.3.a).

    Dates and Schedules

    Conference Champions must be determined by Sunday, February 22, 2025. The release of NCAA Wrestling Championship Brackets will be Monday, February 23, 2025 on an ESPN Network.

    Benefits of Automatic Qualification
      Impact to Conferences and Teams

      The majority of NCAA Division I wrestling programs compete in conferences that depend on single-sport associate members to meet the minimum number of participating institutions. This puts the sport in a unique position where conference sponsorship and membership is very flexible. There are only two conferences, the Atlantic Coast Conference (Smith, 2022) and the Big Ten Conference (Big Ten Conference, n.d. - a), currently sponsoring wrestling as a conference sport without any associate members. Except for the Ivy League, where six member schools compete in the Eastern Intercollegiate Wrestling Association (EIWA), no other conference has the required six full-member schools sponsoring wrestling (Ivy League, n.d. -b).

      For the other wrestling conferences, which make up 76.25% of Division I wrestling programs, the conferences are built around single-sport associate membership. The current conference membership is influenced by a variety of factors, including history, geography, and finances. However, the most significant factor in recent wrestling alignments has likely been the NCAA Championship qualification process. This process has led to conference consolidation as programs seek a greater number of qualifying tournament pre-allocations for their athletes (Mid-American Conference, 2019).

      Any changes in the NCAA Championship qualification process will create new incentives, benefits, and drawbacks for conference membership. This will likely result in many programs reconsidering the value of their current conference membership. Wrestling programs have a unique ability to respond quickly to changes in incentives because their conference affiliation only needs to consider the impact on wrestling and not the impact on the athletic department for programs outside of the ACC and Big Ten. Additionally, these conference relationships have always been understood to be transient, which limits the contractual obligations that would hinder a shift in wrestling conference membership.

      By implementing a system of automatic bids for conference championship-winning wrestling teams, programs will seek to act in their own self-interest. This will likely result in a "right-sizing" of conferences, where most programs hold membership in a conference that they believe is the best fit for their program. The result will probably be more conferences with fewer members, leading to increased parity as the membership shares a similar commitment to resources for their programs.

      Conference Accolades

      With more conferences comprised of fewer members each, there will be more opportunities for schools to earn conference championships. Being able to hang a banner is an important accolade within athletic departments (Glier, 2012).

      Awarding an auto-bids to the NCAA Championship for conference championship winning teams would bring wrestling in-line with most NCAA sports within athletic departments. This would further expedite educating potential fans about wrestling's team championship process. The conference regular season would hold particular significance, not only within the context of their conference but also in the program's ability to participate in the postseason. This ensures that the competitions already hosted at home in front of the local fans will hold particular significance for the program in their postseason ambitions.

      Wrestling is not the only sport that has had this issue with a disconnect with qualification for team championships and the conference championship. Cross Country has been working on a reform to make the sport more conference centric to eliminate some of the confusion around their qualification process and bring it in-line with the more universal NCAA processes (Gault, 2020).

      Expanded conference accolades is likely to produce a greater investment into programs. Not only is there an incentive for institutions to allocate resources into programs that can find success within the conference framework (Greenwell et al., 2007) but conference team championships at the time of participation leads to an increase in lifetime giving from former members of those teams (Meer & Rosen, 2009). Outside of institutional and governmental support, donations are the second largest source of revenue within Division-I (National Collegiate Athletic Association, n.d. -ak). Donations become the number one source for programs with limited media revenue, which would include a large percentage of wrestling's membership. As a result, any changes which have the added benefit of cultivating an increase in donors is beneficial to wrestling long-term.

      College students are more likely to identify with winning programs (End et al., 2002). And as such, the ability to engage the student body with on-campus competitions against conference rivals with postseason implications is significant. This provides a much better opportunity for the campus community to be engaged with the program and raise the standing within the campus community.

      Along with the better alignment of conference competition, the reduced number of contests required to complete a conference slate of team competitions provides more flexibility in scheduling for programs. This allows for individual programs to schedule in a manner that best serves not only their current team but the development of their athletes for the future.

    Conference Parity

    The current structure of conference tournaments with pre-allocations for individual competitions will never increase parity within conferences.

    Individual competitions are not affected or motivated by competitive balance. In these events, athletes compete with their peers and can advance to face more elite athletes or move to the consolation bracket to compete with athletes who have suffered losses, ensuring that competitive balance is achieved within each individual competition.

    The nature of individual competition and systems based on it leads to the inevitable and desired consolidation of programs into larger conferences. This creates significant mismatches in competitive balance and the characteristic makeup of programs that join together when competing within the team dual competition framework. Only two conferences, the Big Ten and ACC, consist of peer academic institutions. The rest of the conferences are more accurately described as regional qualifiers.

    When team competition is the standard, programs have an incentive to work within their conference frameworks to create policies and secure membership that preserves their programs and allow them to succeed.

    The shift towards team competition will likely produce the deconstruction of many conferences. Teams will seek to join peer institutions and/or wrestling programs. There may also be the adoption of conference bylaws that standardize the conference's investment level to create uniform competition, like to what has been done in FCS football with the non-scholarship Pioneer Football Conference (Gormley et al, 2022) and the limited scholarship Northeastern Conference (Herder, 2022).

    Conferences have also taken enforcement actions against member programs for violating these bylaws, such as when the Patriot League banned Fordham from earning a conference football title or receiving the conference's automatic bid to the NCAA Championship for four years (2010-2014) because of providing NCAA permissible athletic financial aid in violation of a since-rescinded Patriot League bylaw prohibiting such aid in football (Patriot League, 2013).

    In Division I wrestling, it is common for programs to have limited funding. Many programs compete with less funding than is needed to fill each scholarship with an out-of-state or international athlete. Additionally, the impact of training and supplemental coaching provided through Regional Training Centers, which are set up to supplement Division I programs, varies greatly by program, and effectively increases the resources available to NCAA programs under the Olympic exemptions, remaining largely outside the NCAA's governance (USA Wrestling, 2020).

    The implementation of competitive balance policies in FCS football conferences, however, does not necessarily mean a lack of success for these programs. In fact, these policies may not change anything for programs such as the Ivy League, which already prohibits member schools from offering merit aid to admitted students (Diemeyer, 2021).

    Team auto-qualification provides a path for teams to compete, and the individual qualification process offers an opportunity for elite athletes in any conference to compete regardless of their program's financial commitment.

    Similar to how limited resource programs in other Division I sports have been able to compete with and attract top athletes, wrestling programs would also be able to do the same, even if they are in a conference with fewer resources. Being in a less resource-rich conference would not prevent a wrestling program from providing competitive opportunities for high-performing athletes or from having a competitive team overall. In fact, success within the conference could provide a path for the team to compete against other successful programs. While some programs may find more success in team competition, others may excel in individual competition. This diversity of paths to success allows athletic departments to focus on the specific strengths of their programs.

2. At-Large Qualification

    About

    At-large Qualification is a process in which deserving teams who have not earned automatic qualification to the NCAA Wrestling Championship may be awarded bids to the NCAA Wrestling Championship based on the team's season performance in team competitions. At-large qualification is also the path for qualification for any independent programs, should they exist. There is no requirement for conference membership to earn qualification to the NCAA Wrestling Championship.

    Eligibility

    All NCAA Division-I Wrestling programs may be eligible for consideration for at-large qualification provided they meet each of the following standards:

    • Current full Division-I member in good standing for the sport of wrestling (17.28.5.3.a),
    • Minimum of at least 50% of contests are to be against NCAA Division-I opponents (20.10.7.2),
    • Compete in a minimum of 10 team (dual) competitions against Division-I programs, and
    • Meet winning percentage requirements:
      • Have a winning record (greater than .500) in all team competitions (Volleyball, Ice Hockey, adopted).
      • Have a record of at least .500 against Division I opponents. (Men's Golf, adopted).

    Notes:

    • Coaches should be aware of the application of Bylaw 12.8.3.1 (seasons of competition). Unless a specific exception is applicable, participation by a student-athlete against outside competition, however limited, may constitute a season of competition.
    • Only team competitions against varsity intercollegiate teams of four-year, degree-granting institutions that conduct a majority of their competition against varsity intercollegiate teams of four-year, degree-granting institutions will count in a team's record. Competition against others, including, but not limited to two-year colleges and club teams will be excluded from the team's record (31.3.3.1; National Collegiate Athletic Association, n.d. -r).

    Process

    Once the official automatic qualifier for each conference is determined, the NCAA Wrestling Committee is responsible for selection of the remainder of the championship field.

    The NCAA Wrestling Selection Committee will select the at-large teams from a pool of teams meeting the above defined criteria. Outstanding teams from automatic-qualifying conferences and independent teams (if applicable) for the at-large selections (Baseball, adopted).

    The committee must consider objectively and without prejudice the competitive records of all other eligible teams. To the best of its ability, the committee shall select the most highly qualified teams to complete the championship field in accordance with the regional structure of the first two rounds (31.3.5).

    Additionally, the scheduling of additional matches not listed on the institution's published schedule as an aid for selection shall not be considered. If an institution encounters extenuating circumstances (e.g., natural disaster), the committee will consider a written waiver request (Soccer, adopted).

    Rating Percentage Index - Teams

    For team competitions, the formula to be utilized for the RPI will be altered slightly to accommodate the possibility of ties. The weight given to winning percentage, opponent's winning percentage, and opponent's opponent's winning percentage will remain unchanged from the formula used for the athlete RPI calculation.

    The formula used will weigh ties as half a win and half a loss so a team's winning percentage would be calculated as: (W + 1/2T)/((W + 1/2T) + (L + 1/2T))).

    Based on this formula, a record of 8 wins, 8 losses, and 4 ties, would have a .500 winning percentage.

    Selection Considerations

    The metrics to be considered by the committee when selecting at-large athletes are:

    • Won-lost record (31.3.3.a)
    • Strength of Schedule (31.3.3.b)
    • Eligibility and availability of student-athletes for NCAA Wrestling Championship (31.3.3.c)
    • Ranking Percentage Index Rank (Soccer, Volleyball, Ice Hockey, adopted)
    • Head-to-Head Competition (Soccer, Volleyball, Ice Hockey, adopted)
    • Quality Wins (Ice Hockey, adopted)

    Dates and Schedules

    Only team competitions held on, or before, Sunday, February 22, 2025 will be considered. The release of NCAA Wrestling Championship Brackets will be Monday, February 23, 2025 on an ESPN Network.

    Benefits of At-large Qualification

    Conference membership is not required for team championship bids or individual athlete bids in this system. Therefore, conferences can continue to operate with intraconference competition without awarding an auto-bid to the NCAA Wrestling Championship if they so choose. The Atlantic Coast Conference has taken this approach for Men's Lacrosse. Despite being one of the premier conferences in NCAA Men's Lacrosse, the conference only has five participating members and therefore does not qualify for an auto-bid to the NCAA Championships. However, this has not stopped the conference from preserving their conference competition without associate membership. Despite only having access to an at-large bid, the lack of an auto-bid has not hindered the conference's ability to compete at the highest level. In the 2021 NCAA Men's Lacrosse Championship, all five ACC programs received at-large selections, and three advanced to the National Semi-Finals (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2021e).

    The process for at-large selection requires programs to have a winning record, including non-Division I programs that can be bundled with Division I competition on the same date. This incentivizes competition between programs of different levels and between Division I and non-Division I programs. The dual meet winning percentage requirement for at-large eligibility is a key component that helps incentivize competition between programs that may not otherwise compete, and it further helps to balance interests between programs to provide a better marketplace for scheduling agreements. This system is likely to result in financial guarantees being provided to less competitive programs, increasing stability within the division (Belzer, n.d.). Financial guarantees are the third largest funding source for the bottom quartile of Division-I member schools and have helped to elevate the quality of the student-athlete experience (National Collegiate Athletic Association, n.d. -ak).

    This increased inter-divisional competition benefits the sport by providing more competition opportunities for athletes and potentially increasing travel efficiency for competitions. This increase in the number of home team dual competitions desired by student-athletes and coaches (Cooper & Weight, 2011) can serve the home fans by providing opportunities to see their team perform at a high level and stimulate pride and interest in the program (Buraimo & Simmons, 2008). This increased demand for engagement provides an opportunity for the program supporters to transition their support from what Popp et al. (2016) refers to as altruistic support to transactional support, where the supporters feel as though they are getting something in return. Success, even against lower tier opponents, leads to increase engagement with the program including increased attendance and sales of team merchandise (Dalakas et al., 2004), which is why so many college football and basketball programs will schedule lower level opponents at times (Wittry, n.d.).

    It should be noted the 1982 NCAA Wrestling participation across all divisions (7,914 athletes), when intra-divisional team competition was much more common, is close to the number of participants (7,673) for the 2020 NCAA season (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2021g).

    The at-large team qualification process enables wrestling programs to qualify for the NCAA Wrestling Championships, even if they don't win their conference championship. This opens up opportunities for more programs to achieve success and establish themselves as top contenders nationally by transforming the season into a two-stage championship structure. The regular season serves as the first stage of championship competition, increasing motivation and performance of programs and competitors throughout the season (Altmann, Falk, & Wibral, 2012).

    The postseason is considered the benchmark for success in collegiate sports, and the at-large qualification provides a way for programs to achieve national success without needing to win their conference championship. This widens the pool of programs that can potentially qualify for the NCAA Wrestling Championships and compete at the highest level of college wrestling which will yield higher donation rates from fans of various levels of giving (Park et al., 2016)

    By not needing to secure the conference's auto-bid to qualify for the NCAA Wrestling Championships, programs have a greater chance of establishing themselves as one of the top programs in the nation, regardless of the strength of their conference. This allows for a more equitable opportunity for success, regardless of a program's location or conference affiliation.

    This path to qualification for mid-tier programs within the most elite conference will increase the rivalries within the conference, particularly for those challenging for an at-large bid, driving fan interest where there is currently a shortage of support (Cooper, 2009a). With the path to at-large qualification being within reach for programs who have traditionally lacked conference or NCAA level success, the significance of conference and non-conference team competitions will increase, especially those against other programs that are also within reach of an at-large bid. This will lead to the further development of rivalries for programs (Tyler & Cobbs, 2017).

    The issue of teams overperforming their schedule, finishing the season undefeated, and not being provided with an opportunity to compete against top-performing teams within a team dual postseason to determine their program's actual strength within the national context will no longer be a concern for wrestling (Northern Iowa Athletics, n.d. -b). This limitation has held back fan investment in the sport, but it will no longer be an obstacle for programs that exceed season expectations.


D. Championship Administration

The following section will detail the way the NCAA Wrestling Championship is to be conducted.

1. Building the Team Championship Bracket

    The Competition Oversight Committee will adopt a field of 24 teams to compete in the NCAA Wrestling Championship (31.3.1).

    The NCAA Wrestling Championship will implement the nonpredetermined championship structure (31.1.3.2.2) for the first three rounds of competition in accordance with the NCAA's standard for on-campus preference (31.1.3.2.3). Adopting this process for site selection will provide schools with championship opportunities on their home campus. These contests will engage their fan base and campus community while providing efficient management of a championship quality event and financial responsibility for the Association. The NCAA Wrestling Championships will continue to operate under the provisions of the Association's revenue sport model.

    The top eight teams will be seeded and separated in the bracket, the remaining 16 teams will be placed geographically without regard for rankings or seed. In making bracket assignments, the closest geographical consideration will be used as well as the ability to separate conference opponents in the first two rounds of the championship, when possible (Lacrosse, adopted).

    Seeding will be conducted using the same criteria applied to at-large selection.

    Steps to building the bracket:

    1. The top-eight teams nationally, as determined by the seeding/selection criteria will be seeded in order.
    2. The top two seeds will each be assigned a No. 1 seed and placed in opposite brackets so that they each would meet in the finals if they were to advance.
    3. The national No. 3 and No. 4 seeds will be assigned the No. 2 seeds in the bracket with consideration given for teams from different conferences to meet in the semi-finals.
    4. The national No. 5 and No. 6 seeds will be assigned the No. 3 seeds in the bracket with consideration given for teams from different conferences to meet in the quarter-finals.
    5. The national No. 7 and No. 8 seeds will be assigned the No. 4 seeds in the bracket with consideration given for teams from different conferences to meet in the quarter-finals.
    6. Remaining teams are placed into the bracket based on geography of their first-round opponent and would be second-round opponent.

    Benefits, Building the Bracket

    The importance of the NCAA Wrestling Championship bracket cannot be overstated, as it not only determines the number of participating teams, but also how they will be distributed within the bracket. The field of 24 teams is significant, as it is a similar allocation ratio for the team championship in Division I Men's Tennis. The structure of the bracket of 24 teams ensures that most teams (16 out of 24) will host an NCAA championship team dual competition at home, providing more home competitions for their athletes and fans without adding a countable date to their schedule. This is a highly coveted reward for programs and aligns with the desired objective of student-athletes and coaches (Cooper & Weight, 2011).

    Granting byes to the top-8 teams ensures the first and second round matchups held at home venues are more competitive, creating a better experience for athletes and fans alike (Ferguson & Lakhani, 2021). Furthermore, the structure of the bracket is financially responsible, as it minimizes travel for programs and allows for geographic assignment of opponents without seeding the first round of competition. This not only reduces the administrative burden of the championship and travel costs, but it also increases the frequency in which opponents within a region are likely to meet, cultivating rivalries between teams (Tyler & Cobbs, 2017).

    With half of all first-round, second-round, and quarter-final programs not needing to travel for competition, the expense of transporting athletes, as well as their time away from campus, is minimized, creating a more efficient and cost-effective championship.

    This structure for a team-centric postseason will bring significant recognition to the programs that qualify, as team qualification for team-based competition has been universally accepted within NCAA institutions and by their stakeholders as a significant benchmark for program performance.

    These tournament-based benchmarks are the standards by which most institutions not only judge team performances but honor and recognize these performances by hanging banners in their arenas. These banners serve as a sense of pride for program supporters while aiding as a sign of the program's success for recruits (Glier, 2012). While it is possible to hang banners for placement in national events, and a few programs have done so on occasion, it is significantly less popular as hanging a banner that says "19th place" doesn't have the same sense of accomplishment as hanging a banner to signify that your program made it to a championship competition for which few other programs advanced.

    Sampling of NCAA institution banners.

    Due to the significance of teams qualifying to post season competition being viewed as a significant accomplishment, team postseason qualification draws greater financial support from donors than winning records alone (Baade & Sundberg, 1996).

    Though donor support is not solely contingent upon athletic success, it is influenced by it. Donors want to see athletic success as an objective way to know that their contributions are making a difference, and as such, donations are also likely to increase when donors believe that their donations could yield a material impact on the program (Heixson, 2012). Programs that have been on the cusp of athletic success, which can now be measured by team postseason participation, are more likely to see an increase in donations with the goal of giving the program the needed push to reach the milestone that is within reach.

2. Site Selection

    Early Rounds

    Any institution interested in hosting preliminary-round competition of the NCAA Wrestling Championship must submit a proposed budget and respond to sport-specific questions about the facility and availability. A separate proposed budget must be submitted for each round that an institution is interested in hosting (FCS Football & Men's Soccer, adopted).

    All rounds of the championship, except the pre-determined site portion (semi-finals and finals), will be conducted on the campus of one of the participating institutions (Men's Soccer, adopted).

    Note: For second-round sites, a team in the top-8 will automatically be selected as a site host, unless the institution did not submit a bid to host or meet the minimum specifications (FCS Football & Men's Soccer, adopted; 31.1.3.2.5).

    Championship Site

    The Championship site is the pre-determined location where the Semi-Finals and Finals of the NCAA Division I Wrestling Championship will be held over two consecutive days. The venue will be announced prior to the start of the season.

    Site Selection will be conducted based on competitive bids, with the following criteria used to consider site selection:

    • Quality and availability of the facility and other necessary accommodations; (31.1.3.2.1.a)
    • Revenue potential (e.g., a financial guarantee or guideline that ensures fiscal responsibility); (31.1.3.2.1.b)
    • Attendance history and potential; (31.1.3.2.1.c)
    • Geographical location; and (31.1.3.2.1.d)
      • The site for the NCAA Wrestling Championship should not be held in any location that is less than 600 miles from the NCAA Wrestling Championships. The two championship events should maintain geographic diversity to attract different groups of fans and maximize fan engagement each season.
      • The NCAA Wrestling Championships and the NCAA Wrestling Championship should not be in competition with each other to attract fans within the same region of the country.
    • Championships operating costs. (31.1.3.2.1.e)

    Seeded teams to meet the minimum financial guarantee in their bid to host will automatically be the home team. If a team did not meet the minimum financial guarantee in their bid to host, the bid will be compared with the competing bid(s) with a selection made by the championship committee to selection of the host team.     Benefits, Site Selection

    Men's wrestling is one of only five NCAA championships to generate revenue for the association, hence the choice of location is crucial for the wrestling championship property to remain an asset for the association (National Collegiate Athletic Association, n.d. -l).

    Higher-seeded teams are rewarded for their performance during the regular season by allowing them to bring championship competitions to their home communities and fans.

    The focus on qualification for the team postseason increases the incentive for programs to prioritize team competitions during the season which will raise the quality of the service and experience for attendees of these events throughout the season. Team competitions do a better job of developing team identity and building relationships between the program and their supporters (Tsiotsou, 2007) which progresses the goal of institutions to cultivate a community through the programs they sponsor. This provides spectators and fans with a higher quality product, which is a factor in donor and fan support (Bass, Achen, & Gordon, 2015).

    Team attachment is a stronger predictor of long-term sport attendance than interest in the sport itself (Mahony et al., 2002). Those who support a team plan to attend team competitions, establishing a more regular routine of attendance even when they are less knowledgeable about the sport or not even fans of the sport more generally. With most season competitions being team dual meets, a more inclusive system that does not require a large investment from spectators to understand the sport or the national field of competitors is likely to yield more spectator engagement in the sport of wrestling overall (Dietz-Uhler et al., 2000).

    This system provides qualified programs with the opportunity to market championship events at their home venue, while utilizing the power and prestige of the NCAA brand, all without needing to sacrifice any regular season or postseason competitions from their schedule to accommodate the new dates. The financial guarantees have intentionally been kept low. These lower financial guarantees allow for ticket prices to be more accessible to prospective attendees which will aid attendance (Ferguson & Lakhani, 2021). Additionally, the lower financial guarantees will allow for prospective programs, venues, and host organizations to submit bids to host without taking on significant risk while the model is being adopted.

    Through this effort, programs will be able to market significant events with championship implications to the local and regional wrestling communities after the high school and youth seasons have passed, providing athletic departments with the ability to capture information on attendees to these events for future marketing and fan engagement campaigns. This takes the success and excitement of NCAA championships events and turns it into a lead generation system for potential future fans of the program (Osti, Disegna, & Brida, 2012).

    Financial guarantees are used as an incentive to encourage programs to foster a community around their program and reward the ones that have effectively done so by building a following of supporters and spectators. Selecting a neutral site for the semi-finals and finals allows for advanced planning and marketing of the event long before the participating teams are known.

    The substantial economic impact of past NCAA Division I Wrestling Championships (Terrrell, 2013; Kelley, 2013; Dolan, 2021; Lee, 2000) and other wrestling events (Journal Record, 2017; Chilson, 2017; Ford, 2012) will attract interest from many communities and venues. This creates an opportunity for a truly neutral championship experience to bring Division-I level wrestling to new and diverse communities around the country.

3. Championship Venue

    Configuration

    A single competition mat, raised on a platform in adherence with the NCAA Wrestling Championships Session Six standards, will be used at the venue with only one team competition taking place at a time throughout the entirety of the two days of competition.

    The venue should be configured in a manner that makes for an intimate venue. When the venue and configuration allow (ie: Hockey Arenas), there should be floor seating added to increase capacity and bring spectators closer to the competition mat.

    Considerations

    While financial considerations may make on-campus facilities a feasible option for the NCAA Wrestling Championship, the event should also consider soliciting bids from private and municipal venues and cities that may not meet the requirements for hosting the NCAA Wrestling Championships. These smaller venues and cities may have less competition for booking events and hosting the championship could make the event a major draw for the community, while also increasing the geographic diversity of wrestling. It is recommended that the venue have a minimum capacity of 9,000 people when configured for the championship. By lowering the spectator capacity minimums for bidding on the (individual) NCAA Wrestling Championship, the number of potential bids will increase, resulting in greater geographic representation and the potential for greater engagement of fans from around the country.

5. Travel Party

    Administration

    Prior to the start of the NCAA Wrestling Championship, participating teams must submit their championship lineup forms.

    A maximum of 22 athletes may be declared on the form. Competition within the championship shall be limited to only the athletes listed on this form. To be listed on the form, athletes must be physically able and eligible to compete. Student-athletes injured to the point of not being able to play (e.g., broken bones, etc.) or student-athletes who have been declared as redshirts or student-athletes who are not eligible for NCAA, conference or institutional reasons, cannot be listed on the lineup form and cannot travel to the championship events (Tennis, adopted).

    Size

    The first round, second round, and quarter-final contests, traveling parties will be limited to 13 student-athletes and five (5) non-athletes. For the championship site (semi-finals and finals) the number of student-athletes in the traveling party is raised to 20.

    Each round's travel party may include a different composition of student-athletes if the athletes were listed on the team's lineup form.

    The travel party size is in keeping with the established travel policies for NCAA championships (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2022k).

E. Impact to Team Standing

For there to be universal acceptance of a new system, we need to ensure that programs from all conference types can compete within the framework. One way to do this is by awarding auto-bids to each conference, which adheres to NCAA principles and provides a path for all programs to compete in the championship. It's also important to consider the standing of autonomous conferences (sometimes referred to as the "Power Five") compared to non-autonomous conferences (Babb, 2014). The goal is to ensure that any policy change does not unduly impact any group of programs.

When we analyze the team dual performance (RPIs) and NCAA placements of these two groups, we do not see a significant deviation. Autonomous conferences may slightly benefit more in an individual-centric format, while non-autonomous conferences may have a higher standing based on their dual performance as calculated within the RPI. However, the difference is not significant and may even disappear with a larger sample size. There is a correlation between a team's dual performance and their individual performance, which suggests that the standings between these two groups would not be significantly impacted under a new system. Furthermore, the relative national ranking for programs within the individual framework and the team competition framework did not differ significantly based on the number of national qualifiers for the program.
