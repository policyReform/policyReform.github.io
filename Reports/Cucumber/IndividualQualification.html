IV. NCAA Wrestling Championships: Weight Class Championships

The event where individual athletes compete to be crowned NCAA Division-I Wrestling National Champions for a weight class is to be called the NCAA Wrestling Championships to signify that there are plural champions to be crowned by virtue of the results during the event (31.02.2.2). The following information will detail the qualification methods available for athletes to qualify for the NCAA Wrestling Championships. The administration of the event will remain unchanged.

A. Current Qualification System Issues

The current system of allocating qualification bids to conferences based on the number of national qualifier-worthy athletes participating in the conference qualifier is a significant improvement over the previous system based on the historic success of the conference (Johnson, 2008). However, the current system still has significant flaws. Wrestling's unique qualification structure has caused significant questions about the sport's integrity (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2022o).

Despite the change in qualification process, national qualification-worthy athletes are still not guaranteed qualification to the NCAA (Individual) Wrestling Championships based on their regular season performance (McLaughlin, 2009). This is a result of the lack of at-large qualification slots built into the system. Since the new system was adopted in 2009, an average of only three slots have been allocated for at-large bids within each weight classification each year. Only a few athletes are assured they will earn qualification based on their season's body of work. Even a top-five ranked athlete by the NCAA Championship's own selection criteria has no assurance that they will be awarded an at-large bid if they do not perform well at their qualification tournament. Despite a record of success throughout the season, there are only a very limited number of athletes that can be awarded at-large bids (Spey, 2017).

For most athletes, the path to qualification depends solely on their performance at their qualifying tournament, requiring athletes to peak at the right time, regardless of regular season performance (Sesker, 2009). While they are conference championships in name, in practice, these qualifying tournaments more closely resemble the NCAA's regional qualification model where placement determines qualification to the National Championships. However, under this system, there is added complexity and uncertainty regarding the benchmark that an athlete must achieve for qualification. The number of pre-allocated bids is dynamically calculated throughout the season which creates a moving target that persists even through the first round of competition at the qualifier (depending on if each pre-allocated athlete at the qualifier takes the mat to compete).

In wrestling, athletes do not compete against the field as they would in a cross country race with 300 athletes running at once. Nor do athletes compete against a field to compare their performance against a clock or a score like athletes in track, diving, golf, or gymnastics. Qualification tournaments in wrestling create a unique path to qualification for each athlete based on the unique matchups they are presented with. This system can create drastically different levels of difficulty within the same qualifying tournament depending on where an athlete is placed within the bracket.

Coaches know that an individual matchup can play an outsized role in their athlete's ability to qualify for the NCAA Championships. And due to the parity within the sport as well as the method in which team standings are calculated at the NCAA Championships, this can have a significant impact on the team's postseason success.

At the last three NCAA Championships, those since the NCAA adopted a policy of seeding all athletes, 46% of athletes seeded 25th or worse have won at least one match at the NCAA Championships. Simply getting your athletes into the field, even without a favorable seed, provides a program with a coin toss chance of scoring points in the team race.

This incentivizes many coaches to work to minimize the quality of the opposition in an athlete's path through their qualifying tournament. It is this system that creates incentives that lead to questions of integrity within the sport.

Overall, the current qualification system for wrestling has significant flaws that hinder the sport and raise questions about its integrity. There is a need for reform to create a system that more fairly allocates bids to ensure qualified athletes are guaranteed a place in the NCAA Championship based on their season's body of work.

B. New Qualification Process

A new qualification process is created that better fits into the new holistic approach for the season while still leveraging many of the current performance metrics used within the current system.

The following section will detail the qualification for athletes to the NCAA Wrestling Championships within the restructured framework.

1. Direct Qualification

    About

    Direct Qualification is a method by which a wrestler earns a bid to the NCAA Wrestling Championships by virtue of their regular season performance and in representing their team during the season.

    The NCAA Wrestling Committee will allocate a maximum of 20 direct qualifiers per weight class.

    Eligibility

    To be eligible for consideration to be a Direct Qualifier, a wrestler must have met the Team Representation standard for the season.

    • Team Representation
      • An athlete must have competed in a minimum of 45% of the total team competitions at a scored weight during the season at the time of consideration for qualification.
        • A team competition is defined by Wrestling Rule 3.5.a. (Example: Dual Meet)
      • A scored weight is when the results for the weight were factored into the team score. Note: Extra matches/Exhibition Matches do not count as scored matches.
      • There is no weight class requirement to meet the Team Representation standard. A wrestler may qualify at a different weight than they represented the team.
      • Team competitions do not need to be against NCAA Division-I members to meet the Team Representation standard but must be countable contests per NCAA bylaws.
    Process

    All wrestlers who meet the Team Representation standard and are ranked in either the Coaches Ranking (CR) or the Ranking Percentage Index (RPI) Ranking will be considered for Direct Qualification. Note: To be included in either ranking an athlete must be designated by weight class on the NCAA Division I Starters page within TrackWrestling.

    There is a predetermined sliding scale of three thresholds that move in unison, by round, to determine the cut line for the Direct Qualifiers in each weight class.

    A wrestler must meet two of the three standards in a round to become a Direct Qualifier.

    If a round would produce over twenty (20) direct qualifiers, then that round is not completed and none of the wrestlers in that round will become Direct Qualifiers. This will produce fewer than twenty Direct Qualifiers for the weight.

    Any of the top-25 athletes in the sliding scale by round, will be moved to the at-large consideration automatically.

    Should a round produce more than twenty wrestlers, then all those wrestlers will move to at-large consideration.

    Sliding scale metrics:

    • Wrestler's Winning Percentage (Win%)
      • Winning Percentage is defined as Division I wins divided by total Division I matches contested at the designated weight class on the NCAA Division I starters page in TrackWrestling.
      • Must have a minimum of eight (8) Division I Matches at the designated weight class on the NCAA Division-I Starters page to have a calculated winning percentage.
    • Coaches Rank (CR)
      • The Coaches rank is determined by rankers at each weight class representing each conference earning an automatic qualification spot the NCAA Wrestling Championship. Each ranker will rank the top-33 athletes at a weight.
      • A point value is assigned to each ranking spot. Wrestlers are ranked based off the total points.
      • The high and low vote is removed from the ranking for each wrestler
      • To be considered in the coaches ranking, a wrestler must have a total of eight (8) Division I Matches at the designated weight and one match must have occurred within 30-days of the latest ranking period.
    • Rating Percentage Index Rank (RPI)
      • RPI is calculated by the following formula: 25% athlete's winning percentage, 50% opponents winning percentage, and 25% opponent's opponent winning percentage
          • Note: For the purposes of calculating winning percentage for the RPI, all forfeits are to be omitted from the athlete's season record. Only matches that have been contested are to be included.
      • Must have 15-Division I Matches at the designated weight class on the NCAA Division I Starters page to have a calculated RPI.

    The committee will not go below the following minimums when establishing Direct Qualifiers:

    • Division I weight class winning percentage: .700
    • Coaches Ranking: Top-30
    • RPI Rank: Top-30

    Direct Qualifier selection will be based on all matches through Sunday, February 22, 2025 for the 2025 Championship.

    Any matches after February 22, 2025 will not be used for determination of Direct Qualifiers, but will be used in the calculations for at-large selections and seeding at the NCAA Wrestling Championships.

    Note: An athlete does not need 15 matches to earn direct qualification. If the athlete meets the thresholds for team representation, winning percentage, and coaches rank they may be allocated a direct qualification.

    Dates and Schedules

    The release of Direct Qualifiers will be Wednesday, February 25, 2025.

    Replacements and Withdrawals

    An athlete that has earned a Direct Qualification bid may be replaced, at the school's discretion, provided the replacement athlete meets all the following criteria at the time the replacement is requested:

    • Has met the Team Representation standard defined in the direct qualification process as of the date of the replacement request.
    • Replacement athlete has met a minimum of two out of the three Replacement Standard Minimums from the metrics in the final round of direct qualification.
      • Note: In order to have a Coaches Rank, the replacement athlete will need to be updated in the Track Wrestling Designated Starter page at the weight prior to coaches rankings being submitted. If this change has not been made and a replacement request is submitted, an athlete will only be evaluated using the Ranking Percentage Index Rank and Winning Percentage. In such a case, the athlete must meet both standards.
    • Replacement request is submitted prior to the day of seeding for the NCAA Wrestling Championships.

    If the replacement athlete has not met the Replacement Standard Minimums, but meets the other criteria, then the direct qualifier will be allocated to the at-large pool and the replacement athlete will be eligible for supplemental or at-large qualification if they would have been eligible for either in the absence of the teammate being declared the starter.

    If the replacement athlete request for an athlete eligible for supplemental qualification is submitted after the start of the supplemental qualifier the team is assigned to then the athlete will not be eligible for consideration for a bid and the bid will be allocated to the at-large pool.

    Benefits, Direct Qualification

    This system provides direct rewards to athletes based on their season performance and contributions to their team, fostering alignment between individual and team interests and creating a more harmonious structure for wrestling. To earn recognition as a starter on the TrackWrestling Starters Page, athletes are required to participate in a minimum number of team competitions. This requirement addresses the principal-agent problem often found in individual sports, where team competitions play a pivotal role (McClure & Spector, 1997). This change balances the team interest by ensuring athletes represent their team in competition and contribute to the team's success to earn individual qualification.

    The competition threshold is intentionally set low to accommodate program flexibility in using various lineups during the season. Athletes are only required to participate in a minority of team competitions, which ensures that they can miss time due to injury or to allow other athletes a chance to compete without affecting the team representation provision.

    High-performing athletes who consistently meet the standards for qualification will be directly included in the individual championship field based on their season's performance. This is a widely adopted method of qualification in NCAA individual sports.

    Under this system, athletes are no longer required to defend their bid to the championship against other athletes who may not have performed consistently throughout the season. This benefit is not only extended to the identified starter at the time of selection, but also to any subsequent replacement athlete if the original qualifier is unable to compete at the championships and another athlete within the program meets the standards for qualification.

    Introducing a direct qualification system will relieve programs of a significant financial burden. This change means that programs will no longer be required to fund athletes' travel to qualifiers when these athletes have already met the qualifications necessary for entry into the NCAA Championships.

    While this report acknowledges the shortcomings of the metrics used in wrestling's current pre-allocation process, as identified in the study published by Bigsby & Ohlmann (2016) in the Journal of Sports Analytics, the established metrics, and criteria for individual qualification will continue to be used in the direct qualifier process to ensure a smooth transition to the new process. The top athletes under the current pre-allocation system will remain the top athletes in the direct qualifier process under the new system, as the same consideration process is being utilized.

2. Supplemental Qualification (Regional Events)

    About

    Supplemental Qualification is a process where a wrestler may earn a bid to the NCAA Wrestling Championships by virtue of their placement in a two-day National Qualifying Event.

    Teams are assigned to a region prior to the start of each season based on geographic location. The regions will be equally divided with any imbalance in the number of teams in a region being decided based on geography so that the region covering the most geographic area will have the fewest teams.

    There will be one regional event held for each of the following regions:

    • North
    • South
    • West

    Regional events will be held at predetermined locations that are secured based on competitive bids to host. As is the case with the current qualifying system, individual programs are responsible for the cost of their athletes and staff to travel and compete at regional events.

    Dates and Schedule

    2025 Regional events may be held on either of the two possible dates.

    • Friday March 6, 2025 to Saturday March 7, 2025
    • Saturday March 7, 2025 to Sunday March 8, 2025

    If a school has a written policy preventing competition on Sundays the team will be assigned to a region competing on the Friday to Saturday schedule. Note: A minimum of one regional must compete on the Friday to Saturday schedule each year.

    The schedule for each day of competition at a regional event is to be established so that competition for the day will end a minimum of two hours prior to the scheduled start time of any NCAA Wrestling Championship competition on the same date.

    Eligibility

    To be eligible to compete in the supplemental qualification process a wrestler must meet all of the following criteria:

    • Is competing in the designated weight class on the NCAA Division-I starters page in TrackWrestling.
    • Has met the Team Representation standard defined in the Direct Qualification process as of the date of the competition.
    • Has not earned Direct Qualification.
    • Not a member of a team represented in the quarter-finals of the NCAA Wrestling Championship.

    Seeding

    An updated set of Coaches Rank, RPI, and Winning Percentage will be provided for each athlete.

    A tiered system will be used to group athletes for seeding purposes at regional events. Each athlete will be placed into one of the three following tiers:

    • Tier I
      • Any athlete earning at-large consideration from the Direct Qualification process.
    • Tier II
      • Any athlete with a .700 or better Winning Percentage
      • Any athlete represented on the Coaches Rankings
      • Any athlete represented on the Ranking Percentage Index Ranking
    • Tier III
      • All remaining athletes

    Seeds will not be declared and seed numbers will not be listed on the brackets. The process of seeding is the act of separating athletes within the bracket based on tiers.

    Athletes in Tier I will be randomly drawn and placed onto the top seed lines. Next Tier II athletes will be randomly drawn into the bracket on the remaining seed lines. Tier three athletes will then fill out the remaining seed lines of the bracket.

    This process will ensure that athletes are separated based on standards established within the qualification process.

    Results

    The results from each of the three regional events will determine qualification to the NCAA Wrestling Championships as well as eligibility for at-large consideration.

    • Regional finishes qualifying for the NCAA Wrestling Championship.
      • Regional Champion
      • Regional Runner-Up
      • Regional Third Place
    • Regional finishes earning at-large consideration (3 per regional, per weight; 9 total, per weight)
      • Regional Fourth Place
      • Regional Fifth Place
      • Regional Sixth Place

    Benefits, Supplemental Qualification

    The current process for selecting pre-allocations for the NCAA Championships is susceptible to controversy, especially when choosing athletes who are "on the bubble." As the selection process moves further away from the top athletes, this issue becomes increasingly prevalent. However, the supplemental qualifier process offers a solution to this problem by addressing both the unequal qualification opportunities for athletes from different conferences and the challenge of selecting "bubble" athletes. This process allows non-qualifying athletes from the regular season to compete against each other for a chance to earn a bid.

    With the supplemental qualifier, imbalanced qualifiers are no longer an issue, and the practice of stealing pre-qualified athlete bids is eliminated. Athletes who have earned a bid through their successful season keep their bids.

    The supplemental qualifier offers a opportunity for athletes from programs no longer competing for the NCAA (Team) Championship to secure qualification to the individual championship.

    The supplemental qualifiers are the only events in which an athlete can secure qualification to the NCAA Championships based on a single event performance. All other qualification methods will consult the full season's body of work. This ensures that the primary focus of qualification remains based on season performance while still offering a last-chance pathway to determined athletes from the programs not competing for an NCAA (team) Championship.

    This process is in line with the regional and direct qualification processes used by many NCAA individual sports. The allocation of bids is spread out regionally through three regional supplemental qualifiers, reducing the sport's concentration in certain regions while maintaining competitiveness. This aligns with the goals of the 2009 qualification reforms as outlined by the Division I Wrestling Committee Chair and Big Ten Conference Deputy Commissioner, Brad Traviolia (Johnson, 2008).

    Reducing the number of qualifying events will make it easier for fans to keep track of the results from a more concise set of qualifiers taking place during the weekend. This provides increased exposure to athletes from programs that may not have received much attention during the regular season, promoting their visibility leading up to the NCAA Wrestling Championships.

    In addition, with fewer Division I wrestling competitions taking place on the qualifier weekend, officiating at qualification events will be more consistent with one another, aligning better with the standards set for the NCAA Championships. This enhances the integrity of the entire competition.


3. At-large Qualification

    About

    At-large Qualification is a process in which deserving athletes within each weight class who have not yet been awarded a bid to the NCAA Wrestling Championships may be awarded bids to the NCAA Wrestling Championships by the NCAA Wrestling Selection Committee.

    Dates and Schedule

    At-large selections will be made by the committee and announced on:

    • Monday, March 9, 2025 (Along with the National Championship)

    Eligibility

    To be eligible for consideration an athlete must meet each of the following criteria:

    • Is competing in the designated weight class on the NCAA Division-I starters page in TrackWrestling.
    • Has met the Team Representation standard defined in the Direct Qualification process as of the date of selection.
    • Competed in one of the following:
      • NCAA Wrestling Championship (any round)
      • Supplemental Qualifier

        • Note: For the purposes of Wrestling Rule 9.1.8, all of the above events should be considered National Qualifying Events.

    Additionally, the athlete must meet one (1) of the following criteria to be considered for at-large selection:

    • Designated as an at-large contender during the Direct Qualification process.
    • Finished top-six at a Supplemental Regional Qualifier
    • Member of a program represented at the NCAA Wrestling Championship Quarter-finals.
    • Attempted to compete but was prevented from competing in their assigned Supplemental Regional Qualifier due to a failed medical/skin check (Wrestling Rule 9.1.8).

    Process

    The NCAA Wrestling Committee will select the at-large athletes from the pool of athletes meeting the above defined criteria.

    Prior to seeding, an updated set of Coaches Rank, RPI, and Winning Percentage will be provided for each athlete.

    The metrics to be considered by the committee when selecting at-large athletes:

    • Winning Percentage against the Qualifiers
    • Winning Percentage
    • Head-to-Head Competition
    • Quality Wins
    • Results Against Common Opponents
    • Coaches Rank
    • Ranking Percentage Index Rank
Benefits, At-Large Qualification

The at-large selection process has been improved to no longer require elite athletes who had their bid stolen from them to secure an at-large bid.

The new at-large process considers only athletes who narrowly missed qualification in the direct qualifier process or through placement in a supplemental qualifier, as well as those athletes on teams who were unable to compete in a supplemental qualifier because their team was competing in the NCAA Wrestling Championship. This narrows the field of athletes under consideration.

C. Championship Administration

The NCAA Individual Championship will continue to be administered in its current format. All 33 athletes per weight class will continue to be seeded. The only alteration will be the elimination of team scoring and team awards at the event.

The selection process for conference pre-allocations is already a proxy for direct selection of athletes. This change simply formalizes the process and directly ties the pre-allocated bids to the athletes who earned them. Currently athletes are required to compete in the conference tournament in order for the conference to retain the bid. This means that the bid is for the athlete currently.

This change eliminates the problematic process of stealing bids and having to defend them. There are three main issues with this process:

  1. Only athletes in that specific conference are able to compete for the bid, which unfairly removes opportunities for some athletes based on their conference affiliation while giving greater opportunities to others based on their conference affiliation.
  2. Athletes in a conference with a lot of allocations must compete in an excessive number of matches just to determine allocations that have no conference placement value for the team (e.g. determining 12th place in a conference tournament). This puts an unreasonable burden on the athletes. The system does not ensure that the top athletes in the country qualify for the NCAA tournament. Qualification is based on performance within a conference rather than a national or regional field of competitors.
  3. The system does not ensure that the top athletes in the country qualify for the NCAA tournament. Qualification is based on performance within a conference rather than a national or regional field of competitors.

The new direct qualification process rewards athletes who perform at a high level throughout the season while representing their team in team competitions. It ensures that the top athletes will earn their way into the NCAA Championships. Additionally, it provides an avenue for other athletes to earn their own bids to the NCAA individual championship, even if they were not selected as direct qualifiers.

This process also helps to eliminate the issue of injured athletes defaulting out at qualifying events in order to preserve their path through an at-large bid. By earning a direct qualification, there is no need for these athletes to enter an event when they are unable to compete, which improves the integrity of the sport.

In order to be eligible for the postseason, including the opportunity to win an individual national championship or earn All-American honors, athletes must have been a starter for their team in at least 45% of team competitions. This requirement is designed to ensure that athletes who participate in the postseason are truly starters for their program and have contributed to their team's success. The threshold for earning the team representation requirement is not weight class specific, which means that a team could potentially have up to 22 athletes who meet the requirement at the end of the season.

The direct qualification process is designed to reward the top-performing athletes during the season. These athletes have demonstrated their abilities and deserve a spot in the NCAA Wrestling Championships. In the current system, there is often universal agreement on which athletes are the most deserving of inclusion in the championship. These athletes, if they were to fail to qualify through a conference allocation, would likely be given an at-large bid. The direct qualification process allows these top performers to bypass qualification events, providing an added incentive for athletes to perform during the season.

Furthermore, the direct qualification process provides the opportunity in the schedule to have a dual track postseason process for teams and individuals which improves athlete well-being. Programs competing for an NCAA Team Championship, will not be burdened with a packed schedule of competition and significant increase in matches.

The top performing programs within team competitions historically are also those that have the greatest number of athletes earning their conferences pre-allocations today. As a result, the number of athletes from teams competing in the NCAA Wrestling (Team) Championship and still seeking a bid to the NCAA Wrestling Championships is likely to be relatively low.

This system of individual qualification provides equal opportunities for athletes from all programs, regardless of their conference affiliation. NCAA Rule 17.28.5.3 Annual Exemptions, Number of Dates of Competition will continue to allow conferences to hold conference championships without counting a date of competition, if they choose, which could actually increase opportunities for athletes by allowing multiple entries per weight class from each school. This would make college wrestling more inclusive and increase equity among athletes. The current system of qualification, with its regional qualifiers and focus on consolidation, has minimized opportunities for individuals and teams to compete in conference championships. The new system aims to address this issue and create a more fair and equitable system for all wrestlers.

The top performing programs historically within team competitions are also those that have the greatest number of athletes earning their conferences pre-allocations today. As a result, the number of athletes from teams competing in the NCAA Wrestling (Team) Championship and still seeking a bid to the NCAA Wrestling Championships is likely to be relatively low.

However, there may still be athletes within these programs that did not earn direct qualification but are still within the top-24 athletes based on their full season of competition (including their postseason results). As a result, these athletes are going to be considered for at-large qualification to the NCAA Wrestling Championships.

The season competition is the primary qualification process to the NCAA Wrestling Championships. The majority of athletes will qualify through direct qualification or at-large selection which increases the significance of the results from the season rather than simply focusing on one qualification competition.

The supplemental qualifier is designed to provide a path for athletes from programs not competing for a NCAA championship to earn a bid to the NCAA Wrestling Championships. This is achieved by consolidating athletes who have not earned qualification into regional qualifying tournaments. This allows these athletes to compete directly with each other for qualification. This process ensures that athletes from every region of the country have the opportunity to earn representation for their program and themselves based on their performance against other athletes in their geographic region who have not yet been selected to the championship.

The at-large consideration process is separate from the supplemental qualifier process and is used to ensure that the top-25 athletes for the season are included in the final field of 33 athletes in each weight class. While the supplemental qualifier process may result in some athletes from under-represented programs earning qualification to the NCAA Wrestling Championships despite not having a top-33 quality season, this is not seen as a flaw in the process. Instead, it is a way to provide opportunities for athletes from all programs to earn a place at the NCAA Wrestling Championships and to ensure that the final field is as competitive as possible.

The match is a countable contest where a result will be recorded for an athlete which will factor into seeding at the NCAA Wrestling Championships.
