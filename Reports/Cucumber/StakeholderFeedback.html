II. Stakeholder Feedback

Reviewing the current competition structures utilized by individual sports is useful for identifying commonalities and outliers within the structures but this information does not provide valuable context about these structures about their support and acceptance within the sport. It is important to get feedback from stakeholders on these structures.

In an effort to gain insights into the perspectives of the coaches of Division I (or National Collegiate, if the sport does not compete in divisions) programs, feedback was solicited through surveys and interviews.

A. Interviews

Interviews were conducted with coaches from a cross-section of individual sports to gain a further understanding of what coaches of individual sports, other than wrestling, felt were strengths and weaknesses in their systems.

These interviews were augmented with interviews from athletic directors at schools ranging in degree of sport sponsorship and level of resources allocated to their athletic department to gain different perspectives.

All interviews were conducted on the condition of anonymity and as such will not be cited. A list of interviews may be found on the reference page.

    1. Interview Trends

    As is the case within wrestling, the interviews reflected many different perspectives within each sport with many coaches within each sport sharing conflicting visions for the sport.

    Below are the most consistent perspectives shared through these interviews.

      a. Season Focus

      The most common theme among all sports was a desire to compete within a team-centric framework. This was a surprising finding, as it was consistent across sports and across programs with varying levels of financial resources within each sport. On the rare occasions when an interview yielded support for an individual-centric framework, it was often from a program competing for NCAA titles. The reasoning behind this preference was that an individual-centric model would better prepare their athletes for professional careers. However, this was not a widespread trend among the top programs in individual sports, and there were even some low-resource programs that held this perspective. There was no apparent difference in structure preference based on the level of resources available to the program, although no formal test was conducted to verify this finding.

      b. Championship Structure

      The next most common theme among the interviews was a desire to have an individual championship that is separate from the team championship. Many respondents expressed a preference for having the team and individual championships as two distinct events, with a strong desire for the individual championship to follow the team championship as a stand-alone event. There was a consensus that programs should be able to focus on the team championship first, and then allow their top athletes the opportunity to compete for individual accolades once the team championship has been completed. Many coaches felt strongly that this structure would be most beneficial for their program, as they were concerned that too much focus on individual athletes could come at the expense of the team. These coaches wanted to maintain a team focus, but also wanted to add a small number of individual opportunities to the season. Coaches who wanted to move towards more of an individual focus were typically from sports with few, if any, individual opportunities, and even then, they still wanted to keep the team focus as a priority. This ordering of championship events, with the team championship coming first followed by the individual championships, is supported by collegiate wrestling coaches according to previous research (Cooper & Weight, 2011).

B. Coaches Survey

Survey of individual sport coaches at programs competing at the highest division within NCAA competition.

    1. Survey Framework

    The sports chosen for the survey were those identified in the previous analysis as sharing similar qualities with wrestling or for which the sport has made recent changes to the championship structure.

    With the goal to see if sports with similar competition qualities share similar perspectives to wrestling or if there is a deviation in perspectives. Additionally, sports that have recently altered their competition structure are included to gain feedback on perspective following changes in competition structure as these sports have the unique perspective of competing under different frameworks.

    As such, coaches of the following sports were surveyed:

    • Bowling
    • Fencing
    • Golf
    • Gymnastics
    • Tennis
    • Wrestling

    Participants were provided the following definitions to create a uniform understanding of the questions asked:

      Individual Centric Model - A team having one or more athletes that can stand out in a national field of athletes from a collection of multiple teams. (Examples: Advancing deep in an individual tournament, finish in stroke play, best score nationally, etc.)
      Team Centric Model - The team that wins directly in head-to-head matchups with another team using a collection of their athletes from their roster. (Example: A team Dual Match Play format.)

    The following eight (8) sentences were provided within each survey and respondents were asked to complete the sentence from a list of possible responses:

    1. For determining the definitive best team in the country...
    2. For getting the most engagement from the current fan base...
    3. For getting the most engagement from alumni and donors...
    4. For getting the most school administration support...
    5. For expanding the fan base...
    6. For providing opportunities to the athletes on your roster...
    7. For TV Viewership...
    8. For Collegiate Competition...

    The possible answers to each question asked were the following:

          The Individual Centric Model is Significantly Better
          The Individual Centric Model is Slightly Better
          Neutral: Both are equal
          The Team Centric Model is Significantly Better
          The Team Centric Model is Slightly Better

    3. Survey Findings

    Wrestling is an outlier among the surveyed sports due to the higher level of support for the individual-centric model compared to the other sports. In contrast, bowling, fencing, and tennis, which do not have individual intercollegiate competitions for their athletes during the regular season, still had coaches who supported the team-centric model. This means that these sports only allow athletes to compete as part of a team in dual competitions. Fencing also has the added issue of being coed, even though some schools only sponsor competition for women. Despite these limitations, none of these sports, including wrestling, had a majority of coaches who said that an individual-centric model would be better for providing opportunities to their athletes. Women's bowling, the most team-centric sport, had unanimous (100%) support from respondents for the team-centric structure.

    Sports that use a team-centric championship model have support for their model, while sports that use an individual-centric championship model do not have support for their model. Fencing and wrestling, which are the most individual-centric in terms of how their championships are conducted, do not have majority support for their current model. However, both sports still conduct their regular season in the alternative team-centric model, which may explain why they lack support for their current championship model.

    Respondent coaches believe the individual-centric model produces a better representation of the definitive best team, but also believe it's better to use the team-centric model throughout all intercollegiate competition, including the postseason. Many coaches prefer intercollegiate competition to be conducted in a framework in which they acknowledge may result in the best team not winning the championship. This sentiment was reflected in interviews with coaches and administrators following the adoption of the team championship in collegiate golf. While many coaches still feel that the best team does not always win the NCAA Championship, they also believe that the current system is the best one for collegiate golf and support the team competition model. A similar response was also observed in interviews with coaches in bowling. Many coaches prefer longer formats that reward consistency to identify the best performing teams and individuals, while the team dual format is more likely to result in unexpected upsets. Coaches understand that the best team over the course of the season may not necessarily be the team that wins on a particular day within the preferred competition format.
