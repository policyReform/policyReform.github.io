Answers

  1. Why change the qualification system to select athletes directly to the NCAA Wrestling Championships? (October 30, 2022)

    2. The selection process for conference pre-allocations is already a proxy for direct selection of athletes. This change simply formalizes the process and directly ties the pre-allocated bids to the athletes who earned them. Currently athletes are required to compete in the conference tournament in order for the conference to retain the bid. This means that the bid is for the athlete currently.

    This change eliminates the problematic process of stealing bids and having to defend them. There are three main issues with this process:

    1. Only athletes in that specific conference are able to compete for the bid, which unfairly removes opportunities for some athletes based on their conference affiliation while giving greater opportunities to others based on their conference affiliation.
    2. Athletes in a conference with a lot of allocations must compete in an excessive number of matches just to determine allocations that have no conference placement value for the team (e.g. determining 12th place in a conference tournament). This puts an unreasonable burden on the athletes. The system does not ensure that the top athletes in the country qualify for the NCAA tournament. Qualification is based on performance within a conference rather than a national or regional field of competitors.
    3. The system does not ensure that the top athletes in the country qualify for the NCAA tournament. Qualification is based on performance within a conference rather than a national or regional field of competitors.

    The new direct qualification process rewards athletes who perform at a high level throughout the season while representing their team in team competitions. It ensures that the top athletes will earn their way into the NCAA Championships. Additionally, it provides an avenue for other athletes to earn their own bids to the NCAA individual championship, even if they were not selected as direct qualifiers.

    This process also helps to eliminate the issue of injured athletes defaulting out at qualifying events in order to preserve their path through an at-large bid. By earning a direct qualification, there is no need for these athletes to enter an event when they are unable to compete, which improves the integrity of the sport.

  2. Why is there a team representation requirement? (November 10, 2022)

    3. In order to be eligible for the postseason, including the opportunity to win an individual national championship or earn All-American honors, athletes must have been a starter for their team in at least 45% of team competitions. This requirement is designed to ensure that athletes who participate in the postseason are truly starters for their program and have contributed to their team's success. The threshold for earning the team representation requirement is not weight class specific, which means that a team could potentially have up to 22 athletes who meet the requirement at the end of the season.

  4. What is the point of Direct Qualifiers? Why shouldn't those athletes have to earn their qualification like others? (November 22, 2022)

    5. The direct qualification process is designed to reward the top-performing athletes during the season. These athletes have demonstrated their abilities and deserve a spot in the NCAA Wrestling Championships. In the current system, there is often universal agreement on which athletes are the most deserving of inclusion in the championship. These athletes, if they were to fail to qualify through a conference allocation, would likely be given an at-large bid. The direct qualification process allows these top performers to bypass qualification events, providing an added incentive for athletes to perform during the season.

    Furthermore, the direct qualification process provides the opportunity in the schedule to have a dual track postseason process for teams and individuals which improves athlete well-being. Programs competing for an NCAA Team Championship, will not be burdened with a packed schedule of competition and significant increase in matches.

    The top performing programs within team competitions historically are also those that have the greatest number of athletes earning their conferences pre-allocations today. As a result, the number of athletes from teams competing in the NCAA Wrestling (Team) Championship and still seeking a bid to the NCAA Wrestling Championships is likely to be relatively low.

  5. Why move to a system of regional qualifiers? Doesn't this diminish the Conference Championships? (December 12, 2022)

    6. This system of individual qualification provides equal opportunities for athletes from all programs, regardless of their conference affiliation. NCAA Rule 17.28.5.3 Annual Exemptions, Number of Dates of Competition will continue to allow conferences to hold conference championships without counting a date of competition, if they choose, which could actually increase opportunities for athletes by allowing multiple entries per weight class from each school. This would make college wrestling more inclusive and increase equity among athletes. The current system of qualification, with its regional qualifiers and focus on consolidation, has minimized opportunities for individuals and teams to compete in conference championships. The new system aims to address this issue and create a more fair and equitable system for all wrestlers.

  8. What is the point of having coaches pick the order of matches in a dual when all matches are going to be wrestled anyways? (January 14, 2023)

    9. While it is true that in regular season dual meets all weight classes will be contested, the selection of order for weight classes may impact on the athletes that a coach decides to use within those weight classes.

    As all weights are contested, the specific order is not important. The current structure's only benefit is to provide flexibility for programs wanting to bump up athletes throughout a competition.

    By changing the progressive increase in weight classes that has been traditionally been used and by having a limited number or alternate wrestlers (rovers) programs can selectively and strategically attempt to push for specific matchups or to hide athletes that they may only want to be used when the team result is on the line late in a contest.

    This system provides flexibility for programs while giving a strategic competition dynamic to the team competitions that is not currently in place today.

  9. Why are weaker programs given an advantage to get their athletes qualified through the regional qualifier but the top programs competing at the National Championship don't have the chance to push through athletes that have a good performance at a qualifier? (January 18, 2023)

    10. The season competition is the primary qualification process to the NCAA Wrestling Championships. The majority of athletes will qualify through direct qualification or at-large selection which increases the significance of the results from the season rather than simply focusing on one qualification competition.

    The supplemental qualifier is designed to provide a path for athletes from programs not competing for a NCAA championship to earn a bid to the NCAA Wrestling Championships. This is achieved by consolidating athletes who have not earned qualification into regional qualifying tournaments. This allows these athletes to compete directly with each other for qualification. This process ensures that athletes from every region of the country have the opportunity to earn representation for their program and themselves based on their performance against other athletes in their geographic region who have not yet been selected to the championship.

    The at-large consideration process is separate from the supplemental qualifier process and is used to ensure that the top-25 athletes for the season are included in the final field of 33 athletes in each weight class. While the supplemental qualifier process may result in some athletes from under-represented programs earning qualification to the NCAA Wrestling Championships despite not having a top-33 quality season, this is not seen as a flaw in the process. Instead, it is a way to provide opportunities for athletes from all programs to earn a place at the NCAA Wrestling Championships and to ensure that the final field is as competitive as possible.

  12. The team tie-breaker doesn't make much sense why have mini matches? (June 1, 2023)

    13. A proper process is needed to handle tie-breaking in team competitions, particularly to determine team advancement, as the current scorer's table process of tabulating in-match statistics is inadequate. Considerable effort has been invested in designing the team tie-breaker, utilizing various modeling methods to explore different potential solutions. The aim is to create an exciting format that engages athletes, teams, and fans, enabling a meaningful comparison between the two teams.

    The proposed format introduces additional competition akin to an overtime period, where athletes engage in extra matches to determine the team's overall result. The selection of weight classes for this competition is based on matches with the narrowest scoring margins. By doing so, weight classes that were most likely to have a different outcome in a rematch are contested, providing an opportunity for additional competition among eligible athletes in those weight classes. This method uses the most tightly contested weight classes to determine the team results.

    Conducting competition for a complete (two minute long) period allows athletes to compete both on the mat and on their feet, offering opportunities to earn points towards their team's victory without being confined to a single specific aspect of wrestling during the overtime period. Furthermore, combining the results into a super-match format empowers coaches to respond to the current outcomes and decide which athletes they wish to field in subsequent periods. This enables the selection of athletes based on their specific skill sets they possess.

    The super-match structure, in which three different athletes may compete within one match to determine the team result, changes the structure from an individual component match structure to a team structure. This unique attribute further shifts the competition structure to be more team-centric in the tie-breaker for team advancement.

    19. In order to streamline the already comprehensive report and prioritize crucial content, only the final proposal and its corresponding considerations have been included within the Cucumber Report. A significant portion of the additional research and considerations which lead to the contents of the report have been omitted.

    Prior to producing the report, there was an extensive analysis of the proposals, hypotheses, and recommendations circulating within the wrestling community, including those put forth by the esteemed Blue Ribbon Task Force. Multiple options were thoroughly reviewed and evaluated, taking into consideration relevant research and modeling that could provide insights into these proposals.

    This analysis identified deficiencies in the hypothesis that shifting the NCAA Wrestling Championships to April, or later, would enhance consumer demand and interest in the NCAA Wrestling Championships and/or regular season. While it is true April hosts fewer NCAA Championships compared to March, simply moving the championships to April (or later) does not guarantee an increase in consumption of NCAA Wrestling.

    In order to maintain the strong interest in the NCAA Individual Wrestling Championships within the expanded structure of a team and individual championship, it is crucial to ensure that the quality of the venue and the competition structure remains enjoyable for what this report identifies as sport loyalists who have been the core regular attendees of the Championship event for years.

    If the championships were moved to April (or even May), it would likely result in a decrease in the demand for bids to host the championship event, directly impacting the event's profitability.

    The NCAA Wrestling Championship requires high-quality arenas capable of accommodating 15,500 spectators per session, equipped with LED video boards, ribbon boards, media production studios, professionally equipped video control rooms, green rooms, meeting rooms, as well as full-time staffing for all in-house production staff, located within a city with enough hotel rooms to accommodate the influx of attendees (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2019a). Most of the venues capable of meeting these standards are professional sports arenas with full-time NBA and/or NHL tenants.

    The duration of the NCAA Individual Wrestling Championships requires the host venue to be allocated for nearly a full week (Monday to Sunday). Thus if the NCAA Wrestling Championships are moved to April, it will be nearly impossible for venues with professional tenants to bid on hosting the Championships as the event's dates are in direct competition with the NBA and NHL playoffs (National Basketball Association, 2023; National Hockey League, 2023). These venues must reserve these playoff dates for their full-time tenants, ensuring facility availability should their primary tenant(s) make the playoffs.

    This reduction in the number of prospective venues will significantly reduce the number of competitive bids received by the Association. Therefore, moving the Championships to April or May would likely result in cities like Des Moines (2013 host), Albany (2002), or Omaha (2010) winning the rights to host the Championships, after cities like Philadelphia (2011), New York (2016), Cleveland (2018), Detroit (2022), and Saint Louis (2021) decline to bid on the Championships.

    Not only would the net impact likely lead to a reduction in the number of possible attendees due to smaller venues in less desirable locales, but it would also increase expenses for flights and hotels for the association as well as fans adding to a decrease in overall local championship amenities.

    The impact of such a change is particularly concerning due to the NCAA Wrestling Committee recently reviewing increasing the minimum financial guarantee necessary for hosting the NCAA Individual Championships from $2.5 Million to $3 Million (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2023a). These higher financial guarantees may prove too burdensome for the remaining prospective host municipalities, resulting in a reduction in the financial guarantees for the championships overall.

    A reduction in financial guarantees would directly impact the Association's investment in the NCAA Wrestling Championship(s) and subsequently lead to a decline in the quality of the event for all stakeholders.

    While there has been considerable discussion about the NCAA Wrestling Championships competing with the NCAA Men's Basketball Championships for viewers, it's important to recognize that the consumption of intercollegiate sports is driven primarily as an entertainment and leisure activity. As a result, competition is not limited to NCAA Championships or or even sports in general. Competition may come from any alternative entertainment or leisure activity individuals find more preferable, in the moment, than consuming NCAA Championship Wrestling.

    This means that while moving the Wrestling Championships would avoid the competition with NCAA Men's Basketball Championships, new competition would arise from alternative sports programming such as the NBA playoffs, NHL playoffs, and MLB regular season. Additionally, the most significant competition is likely to come from outdoor activities, spring high school or youth sports, or simply nicer weather and longer days.

    In March, the prime television-watching season comes to an end, and viewership declines significantly during the spring and summer months (Krantz-Kent, 2018). Research by Barnett et al. (1991) has shown that modeling based on three non-programing based variables, which includes seasonality, can model television viewership with 99% accuracy. This builds on previous research which was able to model highly accurate viewership predictions for primetime network television based on viewing habits and non-viewing activities without needing to factor in programing or demographics of the viewership (Gensch & Shaman, 1980). If the NCAA Wrestling Championships were moved from March to April, it would shift the event from a period when many people are watching televised sports to a time when fewer people intend to watch any television.

    This change is due primarily to the transition in seasons, which leads to more sunshine (longer days) and increased average temperatures. As temperatures rise and days become longer, following the cold and dark winter, television viewership declines at a predictable and measurable rate (Eisinga, Franses, & Vergeer, 2011). The improved weather provides new opportunities for engaging in alternative entertainment activities that feel more novel following the winter months and are valued more highly than watching television at this time of the year.

    Moreover, people are not only less likely to watch television in April and May but also less likely to be aware of televised events. This is partly due to reduced consumption of television, which provides information on future programming, and would likely be exasperated within wrestling's consumers due to the lack of continuity of the various wrestling competition seasons.

    Aligning the intercollegiate wrestling season with the youth and high school seasons is crucial for fostering a culture of wrestling consumption. During this time of year, the wrestling community spends more time together, thinking about the sport, and engaging in natural discussions about the sport, including the programming and results of the NCAA Championships.

    Shifting the intercollegiate seasons and championships to a different time of year, when the majority of high school and wrestling stakeholders have moved on to other activities, would diminish the sport's connection with its target consumers (high school and youth wrestlers, coaches, and their parents). The sport specialization rate within wrestling (~30%) is lower than the typical high school sport specialization rate (Howard, 2016). Even at the high school level, where athletes are most likely to specialize compared to the youth level, there is a high probability that the target consumers will have moved on to other activities and will be spending time outside of the wrestling community.

    As an example, the Ohio High School Athletic Association's (OHSAA) state wrestling championships, which conclude in mid-March, mark the last high school wrestling championship of the year (Ohio High School Athletic Association, 2023). By moving the NCAA Wrestling Championships to April, every single high school State Championship will have been completed for at least a month, with nearly 50% of high school State Championships concluding over two months prior (Abbott, 2022).

    Considering that the high school wrestling postseason often runs for a month and the majority of high school (and youth) athletes do not compete in state championships, most of the targeted prospective youth and high school wrestling consumers will have ended their wrestling season three months prior. Moving the Championships away from the high school competition season would reduce the influence that key wrestling stakeholders, such as wrestling coaches and state qualifier athletes, have on other prospective wrestling consumers.

    In April, the wrestling community would be less concentrated as the majority of those involved in high school and youth wrestling take time off from the sport and move on to other communities. One of the most common communities they transition to is spring sports, with Little League baseball being a prominent example, running from March to May (Coatess, 2023). Consequently, the social influence within the wrestling community, which can serve as a strong motivation for casual sports consumption, would be greatly diminished as the influence of the Little League baseball communities became stronger for these individuals.

    The high school athletes who move on to spring sports, such as baseball, would already be a month into their baseball games when the NCAA Championships are held. Wrestling wouldn't only be competing for attention but actually be in direct competition with actual scheduled athletic events for the prospective consumers.

    It's essential to note that the improved weather does not only result in a change to the athletic communities people may be within but also result in a change to their social behaviors (Essa et al., 1990). The transition in weather and longer days leads to a change in behaviors, resulting in reduced interest in watching television or attending indoor events (Eisinga, Franses, & Vergeer, 2011l Vachon, 1983).

    The likelihood of individuals being more engaged with the sport due to the college season no longer facing competition from the youth and high school season is not great. This is already evident within USA Wrestling's senior level competitions, which feature the biggest names in the sport. While these events may not face the competition often cited as a barrier for the consumption of the NCAA Wrestling Championships, consumption remains weak, with wrestling loyalists comprising the base.

    Consumers are going to be less likely to choose wrestling as an entertainment activity as the event will be taking place indoors at a time when consumers are eager to be able to enjoy outdoor activities.

    It is worth noting that the Big Ten Network's (BTN) support for shifting the wrestling programming should not be surprising. BTN has limited programing options available in the spring due, in large part, to the majority of the conference's spring inventory of athletic competitions being baseball and softball games (Big Ten, n.a.). These contests, which start six weeks prior to Major League Baseball's opening day (Major League Baseball, 2023), require the Big Ten's teams to travel to warmer climates for away games throughout February and March in order to be able to play games without snow on the ground. This results in the conference not being able to capture the media rights for these contests as not being the host (sponsoring) school.

    For instance, Michigan didn't play their first game in the north until their 15th game of the season, following trips to Arizona, California, and Texas (Michigan Athletics, 2023). Even after that single home game, they traveled back south for another four games in Georgia. Leaving only one out of the first twenty games of the season being available for the Big Ten Network to broadcast.

    As a result, the network is left with minimal programing options during the spring (Big Ten, n.a. -a). Shifting the wrestling schedule would help load balance the BTN's programing, providing more original content and reducing their reliance on reruns of previous broadcasts (Big Ten Network, 2023). However, this shift does not necessarily guarantee an improvement in the overall reach of wrestling for the Big Ten or other conferences.

    Though it should be noted, the recent addition of warm climate schools UCLA and USC to the Big Ten Conference will increase the available Big Ten controlled spring sport programing through expanded Women's Gymnastics, Baseball, and Softball, and Beach Volleyball programming for the early spring season (Uggetti, 2022). This programing would simply be new competition for wrestling programming on the BTN if wrestling were to shift the season to April or May.

    It is important to note that the location of the wrestling championships and postseason on the calendar, when properly aligned, can greatly benefit from the heightened interest in sports television consumption fueled by March Madness.

    Not every viewership slot in March is filled with NCAA Men's Basketball Championship programing. The NCAA Wrestling Championships being the same month of the Men's Basketball Championship can be a significant asset to wrestling with an expanded Championship structure that features the team championships during the week and between the March Madness basketball tournament. It is possible to align wrestling's championship schedule to benefit from the increased sports viewership that consumers plan for due to March Madness.

    Harold Hotelling's (1929) pioneering research on spatial competition and location theory has demonstrated the benefits of clustering competing firms, whereby competitors positioning themselves next to one another improves their ability to capture select customers.

    This phenomenon can be observed in car dealerships, mattress stores, gas stations, restaurants and many other industries, where firms in direct competition with each-other will cluster together (Joffe-Walt & Davidson, 2009).

    These same benefits apply to media consumption, where being positioned on select distribution networks and/or being positioned alongside high-demand programming can yield better performance for a media property (Malang et al., 2022; Ryu et al., 2019). The increasing success of the NCAA Women's Basketball Championships serves as a prime example (Weiner, 2023). The Women's basketball championships have benefited from being positioned alongside the Men's Championship in March, leading to greater consumption of the women's championship than would be experienced if the event was scheduled on its own in April.

    It's true that wrestling's currently scheduled individual championship date is not optimal. (See calendar below.) Currently, the championship sits in direct competition with the NCAA Men's Basketball Championship's busiest days of competition. Moving away from this saturated date on the calendar is the correct decision, based on the views of this author as well as those on the Blue Ribbon Task Force. However, there is disagreement regarding the magnitude of the move.

    In addition to the Wrestling Championships being better positioned as one of the limited number of live sporting events on each of the dates of the event, the Team Wrestling Championships will also be held on a date when there are no NCAA Men's Basketball games due to the postseason structure of men's basketball.     These changes position wrestling well to benefit from the increase in televised sports consumption produced by the cultural impact of March Madness.

    As an example of the benefits of spatial proximity in driving sports programing viewership, we can look at the postseason college football schedule. As there are fewer games on each day, the post season continues to attract fans who have grown accustomed to watching Saturday sports through the fall. This scheduling proximity benefitted the 2022 Celebration Bowl, which drew 2.42 million viewers for the matchup between North Carolina Central University and Jackson State University (Showbuzzdaily, n.d.-a). These are programs which rarely attract rated viewership to their games.

    This game would not have drawn such strong viewership if it were positioned within a regular season week with many other games going on at the same time. For comparison, in week nine of the college football season, the 7th most watched game of the week was Notre Dame vs Syracuse with 2.34 million viewers (SportsMediaWatch, n.d. -a). The matchup with North Carolina Central and Jackson State outdrew this matchup by being scheduled at a time when consumption is high but programing is low. The same structure which the Cucumber Report is proposing for wrestling.

    In fact, moving games to April, where there is less competition, did not benefit the 2020 FCS Division I Football Championships. Following the Covid-19 impacted fall 2020 football season, with the support of the television networks looking to bolster spring programing, the FCS football championship was rescheduled to the spring of 2021 when there was significantly less sports programming to compete for viewership. However, the spring Championship was only viewed by 905,000 people (Herder, 2021). A significant decline from the 2.7 million viewers from the previous year's FCS Championship, despite the previous year's championship facing competition from the NFL playoffs and college basketball.

    While the significance of the game did not change, nor did the popularity of the sport, what changed was the seasonal viewership of television and the seasonal preference of consumers. Fall is largely viewed by many consumers as football season. They associate watching football with the season and the holidays serving as the time for championships. This all changed with the shifting of the FCS Division I football Championship to the spring season, and the consumers did not respond well. While shifting the championship to the spring provided more football at a time of year that football fans don't typically get to watch football. Consumers did not respond. And the same is likely true for wrestling's identity within the winter season.

    For this reason, wrestling shifting to the spring is likely to experience a decline in engagement with its regular season. While there is competition with high school and youth competitors' own seasons, which can limit their ability to attend collegiate competitions, as previously mentioned, it is precisely because they are in-season and focused on the sport at that moment within the calendar year that there is interest in attending collegiate competitions in the first place. Creating a high-quality championship for wrestling is important, but as Seth Duckworth (2023) has noted, the financial success of the NCAA Wrestling Championship doesn't benefit individual programs. It is important that the structures utilized within the sport don't simply continue to focus on the Championships but work to be a holistic structure that benefits programs of disparate levels of investment and performance.

    Wrestling needs to be able to measure the impact of any changes made. Simply making changes without performing a qualitative and quantitative assessment of the changes prevents the fine-tuning of the structure.

    By not making a significant shift to the season, the impact on consumption and engagement for the new structure with a separate team and individual championship can be measured. This is important for any significant change.

    While the Blue Ribbon Task Force and this author have independently come to the same position regarding a team dual championship, the proposed shift in the season, proposed by the Blue Ribbon Task Force, introduces unnecessary risk. It is vital that there be an evaluation and consideration period for any implemented structure. The adoption of the proposal herein should be done for a four-year period, during which there will be a review of the structure to decide if the implementation shall persist or if the stakeholders wish to revert to the previous structure.

    When making a change in the season structure at the same time as the competition structure, you introduce many new variables that can impact the metrics which make it impossible to assess performance. Does viewership increase or decline due to the new format or due to the season shift? Does the season shift impact student-athletes positively or negatively, or should that be attributed to the change in the competition structure?

    The above-stated considerations were among those that led to the Cucumber Report producing an alternative conclusion upon reviewing the recommendations of the Blue Ribbon Task Force.

  20. In terms of tie-breaker rules, in the event that the final match of the dual has the slimmest margin of victory (1 point) and would therefore be the first tie-breaker match necessary in the next section. Would this not interfere with the rest time necessary to compete this match and by wording in the report, does this then ensure that the weights that were most closely contested would then not have the same athletes competing? Further, would this mean that "rovers" would need to compete at the given weight? In the event that one or both teams are not able to field an athlete due to rest time restrictions, what would the result be? (August 29, 2023)

    21. The participation of an athlete in a team tie-breaker doesn't count as a separate match as the result from the team tie-breaker will not reported on the individual athlete's Season Record Form. Therefore, the stipulated rest time between matches doesn't apply to the team tie-breaker situation. The team tie-breaker is essentially a continuation of the ongoing dual meet, permitting athletes to compete.

    Engaging in a team tie-breaker allows athletes to extend their wrestling capacity without the requirement of immediate rest, much like how an individual match's over-time extends their ability to compete without a mandatory rest period.

    However, if an athlete intends to participate in a second team tie-breaker, the rest time requirement comes into effect. For example, let's say the concluding match of the dual meet involves the 125 weight division and also serves as the first tie-breaker. If a 125-weight athlete competes in the dual meet's 125 tie-breaker, the athlete would need to observe the mandated rest period from the time of the tie-breaker ended before being eligible for another tie-breaker.

    This means that if the second tie-breaker was the 133 division, the 125 athlete could not bump up and compete in it because the athlete would not have the rest needed. But if the 133 division was tie-breaker eight, then the 125 athlete may be able bump up to the 133 tie-breaker, should enough rest time have passed.

    Rovers are not necessary for a program to compete in a dual or a tie-breaker. The Rovers simply provide additional options to a team.

  21. Why provide visiting teams with an Extra Pound Allowance on dates where there is only a single team competition? (September 8, 2023)

    22. The question of why visiting teams should be granted an extra pound allowance is a reasonable one. It's important to note that there are already existing provisions within the NCAA wrestling rules that allow for weight allowances in certain circumstances (Examples: Rule 9, Section 3, Article 2; Rule 9, Section 4, Article 5). The proposed change to provide a weight allowance for visiting teams is not a radical departure from established practice. In fact, the concept of weight allowances is not entirely new, as individual athletes are already eligible for allowances even if they did not compete on the previous day (Rule 9, Section 3, Article 2). And this rule would only apply to the athletes who have already weighed-in for the weight division previously and are permitted to make the weight without the allowance in their weight loss plan to ensure that the health standards within the sport are maintained and this change is not used as a mechanism to increase weight loss practices.

    NCAA wrestling encompasses a diverse range of programs, each with its unique interests and concerns. This proposed change aims to address the specific challenges faced by programs that lack geographical proximity to other Division I institutions, whether within or outside their conference. Additionally, it considers the constraints imposed by class schedules and the financial resources available for travel.

    While some programs may not view travel as a significant barrier, especially those situated in regions with many Division I programs or with substantial resources to facilitate air travel and extended lodging stays, there are numerous programs that hesitate to participate in team dual competitions away from home. Their hesitation often stems from concerns about the added stress their athletes will experience while managing weight during travel, particularly across different time zones, which can result in jet lag and hinder performance.

    This proposed change aims to alleviate some of the added stress associated with travel. It acknowledges that many programs would like to engage in more team competitions in various locations but are deterred by the long bus journeys, lodging costs, and potential adverse effects on their athletes' performance, both on and off the mat.

    By implementing this small change, we can foster a more inclusive and competitive wrestling environment. Teams that travel to disparate locations will no longer face as significant an obstacle when it comes to weight management, making it easier for them to participate in team competitions and further enrich the sport of wrestling as well as the experiences of the student-athletes within their programs.

    There is now an incentive for programs looking to offer a financial guarantee to bring in an opponent to offer that guarantee to a program from outside of their region as they will also be offering a weight allowance to their opponent.

    Moreover, implementing an extra pound allowance for visiting teams may also stimulate the creation of long-term series between institutions. With the alternating weight allowance benefiting each team in different years, it creates a natural incentive for teams to engage in a series of matches that span multiple seasons. This dynamic encourages teams to build competitive rivalries and seek opportunities to return the advantage in future years. These long-term series not only enhance the excitement and tradition of college wrestling but also contribute to a more balanced and competitive landscape, where the playing field is leveled over time. In this way, the weight allowance policy fosters not only fairness but also the growth and sustainability of collegiate wrestling.

    It is important to note that this weight allowance does not apply to neutral site competitions or to dates of competition where there are multiple team or individual competitions being held.

  22. Why Do Coaches Select the Match Order? (September 8, 2023)

    23. The changes outlined and proposed within this report represent a significant shift in the team dynamic within the sport. Consequently, team competitions take on greater significance throughout the season. With this change, the team dynamic and the coaches' ability to maximize the competition for their student-athletes become paramount, introducing a strategic dimension to the matches.

    Individual matches within the team competition now carry an additional weight in the team's post-season qualification, leading athletes to feel a greater sense of responsibility for their program and the need to respond to the conditions of the team competition, as well as their individual opponent.

    As a result, coaches may wish to position certain athletes earlier or later in a match based on their ability to handle this added level of stress or their capacity to elevate their performance when needed, considering the team score.

    Coaches possess an in-depth understanding of their team's strengths and weaknesses, as well as those of their opponents. This knowledge allows them to tailor the match order to exploit favorable matchups or mitigate disadvantages effectively.

    Moreover, giving coaches the authority to determine the match order opens up opportunities for additional athletes to earn their place in the lineup. Coaches might want to introduce backup student-athletes into the team competition to gain experience and recognize their progress in the practice room. However, they might be unable to do so if the weight class is scheduled early in the competition, with uncertainty about the team result later in the contest.

    This flexibility allows coaches to utilize rovers, altering their lineups on-the-fly to better serve their team's needs. This could involve moving a star athlete up a weight class in pursuit of a different net outcome to make up team points or resting athletes when the team result is no longer in doubt.

    This dynamic introduces a new dimension to coaching, enabling coaches to exercise creativity in decision-making to influence the ebb and flow of team competitions in their favor, while opposing programs strive to do the same.

    The outcome of this strategic coaching battle is likely to produce a sequence of matches that is more captivating for fans watching the competition than a fixed progression based solely on weight classification.

  24. Why is the team championship first? The Individual Tournament needs to be held first with the team championship being second. Otherwise you risk harming the individual tournament which is what everyone values the most. (February 17, 2024)

    25. The historical precedence of the individual NCAA wrestling championship as the culminating event of the season is well understood and deeply ingrained in the wrestling community. Student-athletes have long aspired to attain individual titles as the pinnacle of their collegiate careers, as the NCAA's individual wrestling championships predate the NCAA holding a team championship (Hammond, 2006; National Collegiate Athletic Association, n.d. -j).

    Addressing the concern about the sequence of championship events, it's essential to understand the rationale behind prioritizing the team championship first. One key rationale supporting the prioritization of the team championship being contested first lies in its role as the program's focal point. This shift prompts athletes to concentrate on team objectives until individual competition becomes their sole focus. This balance is crucial for reshaping how collegiate wrestling competitions work, potentially easing the burden on coaches. With these changes, coaches may find it less challenging to navigate between team interests and individual aspirations, allowing them to better prioritize both the team's goals and the individual goals of each student-athlete on their roster throughout the season.

    Emphasizing the team championship initially fosters camaraderie and team spirit among athletes, coaches, and fans (Bernache, Laurin, & Bodet, 2012). It enables wrestlers to compete cohesively, bolstering pride and unity within their respective teams, thereby maximizing institutional benefits for the institutions investing in the sponsorship of the sport of wrestling (Branscombe & Wann, 1991).

    Team dual meet competitions tend to draw more spectators and generate greater excitement than individual tournaments, as evident by the events throughout the regular season. By spotlighting the team championship first, NCAA wrestling can capitalize on this heightened interest to draw in more wrestling enthusiasts to the sport through the team championship, garnering increased attention for the sport as a whole in a format that is easily understood by those not familiar with the field of collegiate athletes. This will allow spectators of the team championship to be introduced to the elite athletes within the team competition, which will increase their interest in continuing to follow those athletes within the subsequent individual championships (Hoegele, Schmidt & Torgler, 2014b).

    Collegiate athletes have long prioritized their personal interests, necessitating a structural balance between team and personal goals within the sport of wrestling. It's imperative for athletes to prioritize the interests of the institutions sponsoring their opportunities, which helps to safeguard the continued investment into the sport of wrestling at the sponsoring institutions (Fair, 2017).

    Due to the sport's history and culture, it's anticipated that wrestling loyalists will continue to prioritize the individual championship when choosing to attend only one championship event. Hence, maintaining the individual championship as the season's pinnacle event is essential, as it amplifies the championship narrative, with occasional rematches becoming more personal as the individual accolades hinge on the results, adding to its allure.

    Holding the team championships after the individual championships would likely result in some student-athletes (starters and non-starters) electing to opt-out of competing in a team championship after they have exhausted their individual championship opportunities. This phenomenon is already observed within other NCAA sports (Boone, 2019; Peter, 2022; Myerberg, 2023). This would likely be exacerbated within an Olympic year with athletes opting out of the team competition to train for the Olympics, potentially diminishing the significance of the collegiate championships.

    When the team championship is held first, the interest of the athlete complements and supports the interest of the sponsoring institution, which has made the investment into the sport and funded the opportunities for the athletes within their program. All of the individual component matches not only serve the interest of the institution in their quest for institutional accolades but also serve the individual athletes in positioning themselves for opportunities to earn individual accolades. The championship event which recognizes the accomplishments of the institution funding the student-athlete opportunities is prioritized first-increasing the benefits to the institution.

    The structure proposed within the Cucumber Report balances not only the interests of an institution and its student-athletes but also the student-athletes and institutions throughout the sport's membership with various levels of performance. This is because the length of the postseason is not dramatically different for programs or individuals based on the performance of team.

    The dual-track postseason proposed, whereby teams not competing within the team championship may still compete for qualification of their athletes for the individual championship, ensures there are no extended breaks for programs within the season, nor is the season extended for a select number of programs based on their team's success.

    This is significant, as it not only aligns with the preference of many coaches and student-athletes to avoid extending the season but also enhances the chances of approval within the NCAA's committee structure. The collegiate framework and NCAA policies dictate that any proposed team championship structures following the individual championships are unlikely to gain committee approval.

    This is primarily due to the length of the season and provisions for individual competition outlined in the NCAA manual (17.28.1). The NCAA competition season is well-defined and limited in length by program declared schedules (17.28.5). Athletes may participate in postseason training and competition only upon qualifying for a championship event, without the dates counting against the team's season length (17.1.9). Currently, only individual athletes qualifying for the NCAA championships continue postseason training, with necessary training partners also permitted to continue. All other team members are released from training and transition into the offseason, by NCAA policy (17.1.9.c).

    Introducing a team championship at a later date would extend the season for entire rosters, contingent on the possibility of qualification for the team championships.

    This extension could create false hope for many programs and athletes, potentially leading to unwarranted training for a championship invitation that may never materialize. Additionally, it could result in significant disparities in team training, with some teams enduring a prolonged season, including training through both the individual and team championships while other teams would have a significantly shorter season.

    Even individual athletes in the starting lineup for a team that qualified for the team championships may not be guaranteed participation in the team competition after extended training. Since team outcomes are determined once the team result is decided, not all weight classes may be required to determine the team outcome. Scheduling the team competition second would likely lead to instances where athletes who did not qualify for the individual championship are required to train for an additional month, or more (depending on the proposal), for a match that never occurs.

    Given the sport's expressed desire for reduced training, such a proposal would face resistance from coaches and athletes during the vetting process and never gain traction. From a policy perspective, the extensive additional timeline for full rosters to remain engaged in training would likely fail administrative committee review within the NCAA's vetting process as well.

    There will inevitably be athletes who train during the postseason. The current policy, restricting postseason training to those athletes and teams included in postseason events, is deemed the most appropriate and will remain unchanged within the framework proposed within the Cucumber Report.

    Prioritizing the team championship first in the sequence of events allows teams qualifying for the championship to maintain consistent training timelines alongside programs focusing on supplemental qualifiers for their individual athletes. This standardizes season length and competition frameworks for all programs, regardless of team performance throughout the season.

    Only the athletes qualifying for the individual championship and opting to pursue individual honors will have an extended training cycle, distinguishing them from other team members. This is the current process and adheres to the NCAA standards and policy.

    While many different structures and proposals were explored, the structure proposed within the Cucumber Report best serves the various interests necessary for implementing change while also ensuring the long-term success of the championship(s) within the proposed structure.
