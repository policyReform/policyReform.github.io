VII. Schedule

A. Season Structure

    1. Start of the Season

    The start of the season will remain unchanged for Division I wrestling.

    By not moving the start date of the season, student-athletes will not have their first competitions and weight-certification pushed deeper into the semester where it could create conflict with the end of semester academic demands.

    2. Postseason

    The postseason competition schedule will maintain the same spread between the final date of regular season competition and the start date of the NCAA (Individual) Wrestling Championships. The break between competition prior to the individual championship will only be reduced by one day for the teams competing for a National Championship compared to the break most teams had prior to the 2022 championship.

    This use of the competition schedule within the established timelines allows for the adoption of the new competition structure without needing to wait for a new championship bidding cycle. As a result, we can view the schedule within the context of the 2025 wrestling season where the championship has been awarded to the Wells Fargo Center in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania (Wells Fargo Center, 2020).

    The postseason accommodates the individual and team components utilized throughout the wrestling season while keeping the primary focus on team competition until the team championship has been determined. It is at that point that the focus shifts to individual championships balancing the team and individual interests of the season competition structure (McClure & Spector, 1997).

    3. Season Length

    Although the season will be extended by one week, the training and competition duration for athletes will not be expanded. The length of the season will remain unchanged, adhering to NCAA Rule 17.27.1, which limits the season to 144 days, which can be divided into two segments.

    Additionally, wrestling will adopt the NCAA Rule 17.1.7.10 Three-Day Period, which will extend the season without requiring additional competition while also ensuring that student-athletes are provided with an opportunity to provide time with family for the holidays.

      17.1.7.10 Three-Day Period -- Basketball. "In basketball, an institution shall provide student-athletes with three consecutive days off during the institution's official vacation period after the first term of the academic year (e.g., winter break). Required athletically related activities are prohibited on such days."

    By maintaining the same number of competition and training days, programs will have more flexibility to create a competition schedule that best suits their unique needs. This approach better serves the membership as each school may tailor their individual season of competition based on their unique interests and demands such as having different academic calendars, travel, and scheduling concerns, or balancing divergent interests in championship or non-championship segments.

    This added flexibility will allow for the management of the competition season in a way where programs may choose to provide athletes with extended breaks during exams, holidays, or any other time within the season by defining the period as a segment break. This will enhance the athletes' wellbeing and ensure that they have ample time to prepare for academic success (Cooper & Weight, 2011).

B. Shifting the Championship

The sport of wrestling faces a challenge in terms of consumption of the championship due to its proximity to the NCAA Division I Men's Basketball Championship and high school state wrestling championships (Cooper & Weight, 2011). The men's basketball tournament, known as March Madness, has become a popular event, even among those who do not otherwise follow college basketball. High school wrestling championships also draw in potential lifelong fans of wrestling. As a result, wrestling stakeholders, via the Blue Ribbon Task Force for wrestling's future, have proposed moving the sport to the spring to avoid competing with these events (Hamilton, 2017).

However, after conducting an extensive analysis which included the modeling of various options, an alternative solution has been identified that better serves wrestling's mutliple objectives. This plan involves restructuring the schedule in two phases: Phase II is the preferred plan, with Phase I only to be adopted if the restructuring is implemented in a year in which the championship site host has already been determined, resulting in a fixed date for the individual championship. The difference between the two phases is only one week. Phase II calls for pushing back the NCAA (individual) Championships one week. While this change is relatively small the impact is significant for wrestling as it allows the sport to take advantage of a media vacuum created by other sports avoiding competition during the men's basketball championship, thereby maximizing reach, and creating value for media partners.

The shift of wrestling to compete one week later in the year moves the wrestling postseason from the beginning of the men's and women's college basketball post season to the middle of their post season. This shift may seem insignificant but the structures of those events and the nature of tournament competition which reduces the field of competitors throughout the postseason means that the competition with wrestling is greatly diminished.

    1. Team Championship

    Simply holding a wrestling championship is not enough to generate interest in the event. To maximize its potential, it must be strategically planned to minimize obstacles for potential spectators.

    While college basketball attracts the greatest media coverage and athletic competition consumption, not all college basketball games have the same level of competition for wrestling. The primary competition for wrestling viewership comes from what is colloquially known as the "T6" or top-six men's college basketball conferences (ACC, Big East, Big Ten, Big 12, Pac-12, and SEC), as these programs generate the highest TV ratings and program revenue.

    Outside of this group, wrestling faces less competition from other men's conferences and women's college basketball in terms of attracting spectators and TV viewers. Therefore, the college basketball season has been analyzed to identify windows where wrestling can gain exposure during a lack of top-six competitions.

    There are two primary windows that develop for wrestling:

      Championship Week (Window: Sunday, March 6, 2022-Monday, March 7, 2022)

      Championship week, as named by ESPN, refers to the week when the top-six men's college basketball conference championship tournaments are held (Hiestand, 2003). It is considered the start of March Madness by many fans (Rutherford, 2020) and usually starts on Tuesday or Wednesday, lasting until Saturday or Sunday.

      To prepare for the conference tournament, teams avoid scheduling games on Sunday and Monday to allow for rest and travel. If there is a Sunday game, it is typically an early mid-day game, creating a window from mid-day Sunday to Tuesday with limited men's college basketball competition.

      Some mid-major conferences have moved their tournament games earlier in the season to gain national exposure, with the opportunity to play their championship game on Monday or Tuesday night in primetime on national TV when the top-six conferences are not playing.

      This window, with limited programming available, allowed the Big 12 Conference Wrestling tournament finals to be broadcast nationally on ESPN Sunday night (Habbas, 2022). The semi-finals and finals of the NCAA Wrestling (Team) Championship are scheduled to be held in phase I during this window. In phase II, the second-round duals hosted by top-seeds in their home arenas are scheduled to take place, competing with mid-major basketball, where wrestling television programming is more competitive.

      Selection Sunday (Window: Sunday, March 13, 2022-Monday, March 14, 2022)

      Selection Sunday is the day when the season ending tournaments are announced (Beller, 2018).

      The evening of Selection Sunday, along with the Monday following is the only time within the whole men's college basketball season guaranteed to not have any games. This is a result of NCAA Rule 17.4.4 which prevents basketball teams from playing any regular season games after their conference tournament started. And with each conference now using a conference tournament to determine their conference's automatic bid to the NCAA Championship (Tracy, 2016), this leaves only season ending tournaments as options for games to be played at these times. But since the selection of the NCAA Men's Division I Basketball tournament is announced that evening, teams are awaiting to learn of their possible selection into the NCAA Championship. This has a cascading effect as the inclusion (and exclusion) of teams in this event dictates the teams that play in the other post-season competitions such as the National Invitational Tournament, The Basketball Classic (Judd, 2022), or the College Basketball Invitational (Rill, 2022). Which leads to the Monday after Selection Sunday being an off day for all of men's college basketball with teams preparing to travel for their end of season tournaments.

      It is within this window that wrestling has the opportunity within phase II to place the NCAA Wrestling (Team) Semi-Finals and Finals with little, if any, competition from other collegiate programming.

    Consumer behavior habits have a greater impact on television consumption than supply and demand of programming (Vergeer et al., 2012), meaning that people are likely to watch what is available to them when they sit down to watch TV on the channels (or streaming services) they regularly look to for programing. This makes windows with limited programming a valuable opportunity for wrestling and its media partners, as they can provide consumers with something to watch even if it is not what they originally intended to watch.

    By focusing on weeknight competitions, the wrestling championship brings team competitions to consumers during prime television consumption when they have limited time for alternative leisure activities which increases the likelihood of consuming the wrestling championship (Krantz-Kent, 2018).

    While there is no guarantee that championship media partners will pick up all, or any, of the programing options for distribution on linear television, the odds are greatly increased due to the lack of competition.

    The viability of the championship is not dependent on linear television distribution. Any media partner holding the distribution rights for the championship will make the events available to consumers even if only through streaming services.

    2. Individual Championship

    The rescheduling of the individual wrestling championship to one week later (phase II) will have a significant impact on the sport's ability to draw media attention and audiences. This is because the event will follow the team-advancement championship, which will attract new fans, and be scheduled at a time with less competition from other NCAA championships. Although there will still be some basketball championships during this time, the reduced competition will make it easier for news and media companies to cover wrestling. Additionally, many people will be looking for more sports programming after the start of March Madness, and wrestling will be one of the limited options available during the second week which has a reduction in sports programming.

    March Madness and its media partners have adopted a staggered schedule for championship games, allowing games to start roughly every 30 minutes (Sugiura, 2011). This enables fans to watch the endings of each game and ensures no more than two games are on at a time. This is beneficial for the wrestling championship as there may be instances when March Madness games are not competitive, and the ending may not be desirable for fans to watch. This results in consumers seeking alternative programming. With even fewer basketball games during phase II programming, curious fans, including those with prior wrestling experience, can watch wrestling, increasing the sport's exposure and value.

    3. Competition with High School Championships

    It's crucial to have the college wrestling season follow the high school championships to attract interest from the high school and youth wrestling communities. These communities have strong interpersonal connections that greatly influence their members (Bond et al., 2012), especially adolescents and their peers (Shin, 2022). These real social networks create optimal conditions for promoting wrestling as a leisure and social spectator activity when the communities are strongest, after having spent a full wrestling season together.

    It's widely known that observing skilled athletes compete can improve lower-skilled athletes' performance (Bruton & Wright, 2022). This has been a motivator for parents and coaches to expose their kids to college wrestling. But this shouldn't be the only reason for watching wrestling. The sport should be seen as a fun spectator activity that brings together people who love the sport, regardless of their competitive goals or past achievements, for children to develop an interest in it beyond personal competition (Cope, Bailey, & Pearce, 2013).

    The primary motivation for children's participation in youth and high school sports is the enjoyment derived from the participation, which in the early stages of participation is often based on the social aspects, such as meeting new people (MacPhail et al., 2003), making friends (Light & Lemonie, 2010), or gaining social acceptance (Smith, Balaguer, & Duda, 2006). However, the source of a participant's enjoyment from the sport and subsequent motivation for participation often evolves over time (Deci & Ryan, 2000) with achievement-based motives becoming more prevalent after extended periods of participation for some individuals (Nicholls, 1984). These changes in the source of enjoyment and subsequent motivations for participation among individuals can be the determination in the likelihood an individual continues to participate in the sport as more emphasis is placed on competition and results.

    The tournament competition model common in wrestling at the youth and high school level has an inherent emphasis on competition and performance by design. This competition structure where only one athlete wins magnifies athlete failure (Sagar, Lavallee, & Spray, 2007) which combined with long days of weekend competition, leads to a high attrition rate among participants motivated by the social aspects of the sport (Quested & Duda, 2011).

    The loss of participants when competition intensifies and becomes results-oriented is not exclusive to wrestling. Nonetheless, for many sports, individuals who stop participating still benefit from the social aspects of the sport and maintain relationships within the community as fans (Lock & Funk, 2016). This shift from participant to consumer, usually during adolescence, is driven by peer influence and a desire to stay connected to the sport and community they once enjoyed participating (McCarthy & Jones, 2007).

    Even though the individuals that remain active as participants are less motivated by the social aspects of the sport over time, it does not mean that the social aspects are insignificant. Without the social aspect of the sport, even participants do not develop a long-term attachment to the sport outside of their personal competitive ambitions. This leads to not only a lack of consumption of wrestling by former participants of the sport but a complete detachment from the sport in many instances.

    This is college wrestling's consumption problem.

    Wrestling is the sixth most popular high school sport for boys (Stanec, 2013) and girls wrestling is experiencing significant growth in sponsorship and participation (Bennett, 2022; Eaton, 2022), providing a large base of potential college wrestling consumers who already understand and enjoy the sport. However, there is a relatively weak culture of consumption of college wrestling due in large part to competition structures which has placed a significant emphasis on individual competition and a scheduling structure which leads to significant competition with alternative programing including youth and high school competitions.

    Cultivating a culture of socialization around competition is only possible if the competition structure makes the sport accessible to non-experts who can pick up an understanding of the competition at any point in the season, just as March Madness has done.

    The changes to the championship format proposed for college wrestling here do not require individuals to have an extensive knowledge about any athletes or teams. While it would be ideal to have consumers engaged throughout college wrestling's regular season, consumers are able to start following the sport at the postseason and fully understand the competition.

    The team-centric format is ideal for ease of consumption as spectators will naturally pick a side in a two-team competition drawing on outside notions of rivalries (Wenger & Brown, 2014). With the dates of the team championship competitions avoiding the most popular wrestling competition day of Saturday and more heavily focused on weeknights, youth and high school parents, athletes, coaches, and former athletes alike will be able to watch wrestling within their natural television viewing times in which they seek an escape from the day (Billings & Ruihley, 2013).

    This schedule is optimized for television and streaming distribution, which greatly reduces consumer friction. It allows individuals to become naturally educated about the teams and athletes through limited exposure to college wrestling during the team-advancement format. This will naturally lead to increased real engagement within these communities, as sports consumers are highly likely to seek out others who share their interest in the sport while watching the sport (Hino, 2015) and to continue discussing the competition long after it has concluded (Gantz et al., 2016).

    The team championship serves as a gateway to the individual championship, drawing in potential fans who have developed an affinity for the participating individuals and teams. Through the school brands and a simple team competition format, the team championship generates interest in the individual championship, making it appealing to those who have previously not watched it. After the team competition, viewers will have a desire to see more and will be curious about potential match-ups that didn't occur during the team competition.

C. Discussion: Single Semester Proposal

The Blue Ribbon Task Force has made a recommendation for Division I Wrestling to become a single semester spring sport (Holmes, 2017). According to Troy Nickerson, a member of the task force, a motivation for moving wrestling was to improve athlete academic performance. The hypothesis being that student-athlete academic performance within wrestling has been reduced due to the high number of first-generation students, the stress of starting college training, and going through the weight certification process while trying to prepare for fall semester finals (Hamilton, 2017).

This policy proposal, which has shown support from athletes and coaches (Cooper & Weight, 2011) may stem from anecdotal evidence based on communications with student-athletes. Student-athletes perceive their academic situation as being more difficult than non-athletes due to their athletic commitments, despite student-athlete academic performance being consistent with non-athletes who enter college with similar levels of academic preparedness, even at highly selective colleges (Aries et al., 2004; Hildenbrand, 2005). While college athletics can be a stressor for student-athletes, it's also a buffer to stress which helps athletes in their academic pursuits (Kimball & Freysinger, 2003). The academic performance of student-athletes is largely a function of their academic motivations (Gayles, 2004).

The literature on the topic of academic performance for student-athletes has largely supported the position that academic performance is not hindered by athletic participation (Frost, 2001; Hada, 2006; Robst & Keil, 2000; Valleser, 2014). In a study conducted of major programs, which included wrestling, a negative effect for the revenue sports of football and basketball was identified with researchers stating: "there is a negative season effect in revenue sports but not in non-revenue sports... Unlike athletes in the revenue sports, participants in nonrevenue sports show no grade differential between on- and off-season" (McCormick & Maloney, 1993).

A further analysis, specific to Division I wrestler academic performance, is possible by reviewing research utilizing the NCAA's dataset of sport specific athlete semester grades conducted by Scott et al. (2008). While this research shows a slight difference in grade point averages for Division I wrestlers between the fall semester and spring semester the results were not statistically significant. This study reaffirmed the previous research findings that high profile revenue sports, identified within this study as men's basketball, baseball, and football, have a negative effect on student-athlete academic performance compared to Olympic men's sports. According to Scott et al. (2008), "the in-season vs. out-of-season GPA differences are enhanced for the males in high profile sports in comparison with the effects seen among all other student-athletes in Division I."

The GPAs of all athletes trend upward over time and as a result, freshmen having a lower GPA or fall grades being slightly lower than spring grades is consistent with this trend (Scott et al., 2008). Additionally, first-generation students as a group are significantly more likely to not be prepared for college when compared to their peers whose parents attended college (Choy, 2001). This is significant for the sport of men's wrestling which has the second highest percentage (23%) of first-generation athletes within all NCAA sports (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2016). As a result, we would expect to see a difference between the fall to the spring semester GPAs as well as a significant difference in the number of credits earned between the two semesters.

However, the sport specific analysis by Scott et al. (2008) finds Division I wrestlers earning the same number of academic credits in the fall semester as the spring semester.

It is frequently observed that NCAA student-athletes will earn fewer credits during a semester where they are expected to experience greater demands or are still in the process of acclimating themselves to the rigorous demands of college life (McKerrow & Daly, 1990). Optimizing the course load of student-athletes across the academic year is a common practice within NCAA athletic departments that allows students to be well positioned for academic success.

If wrestling coaches, administrators, and academic advisors believed the composition of wrestling's seasonal acclimation period led to a reduction in academic performance within the fall semester then we should see a rebalancing of the academic courseload for wrestlers to mitigate the effects. However, the data doesn't demonstrate the practice of academic load balancing within Division I wrestling when we review the data (Scott et al., 2008).

This lack of academic load balancing is likely an indication that a change in the season will not result in a change to academic performance for wrestlers within programs.

The lack of academic load balancing may not be due to NCAA wrestling but rather due to the nature of competition and training for Division I wrestlers when they are outside of the NCAA wrestling season. As noted by Andy Vogel (2017), the proliferation of clubs and Regional Training Centers imbedded within Division I programs (USA Wrestling, n.d.) has led to Division I wrestlers training and competing year round. Collegiate wrestlers continue to compete with their coaches and teammates following the NCAA season through the spring semester simply under a different team name (Miller, 2017; VAWA, 2017; FloWrestling, 2021). While the NCAA season may not continue through the spring semester, the wrestling season for NCAA athletes and coaches continues through the spring semester. This likely explains why we don't see Division I wrestlers earning more credits in the spring than they do in the fall.

This also explains why Division I wrestlers want to see a mandatory three days a week off from training outside the NCAA season, a position endorsed by the athletes from very few other Division I sports (NCAA Research, 2016).

Though the NCAA wrestling season may have ended, the wrestling season for NCAA Division I wrestlers continues. And as such, any policy change made for NCAA wrestling is not going to have a significant impact due to this outside competition by wrestlers.

The phenomenon of unattached competition is more likely to have an impact on student-athlete academic progress, no matter what the semester distribution. This unique aspect of the sport has a rich history and is intricately intertwined with Division I wrestling.

To better understand this issue, it's important to understand the history and progression of collegiate competition in general.

The origins of college sports can be traced back to athletic clubs established on college campuses. These clubs gained popularity and led to competitions with organizations outside of the campus community (Lewis, 1970) including other college clubs as well as organizations like the AAU or local YMCAs, particularly when these were the only available competition options (Martinez, 2017).

In 1921, the NCAA held its first-ever national championship when the University of Chicago hosted the NCAA Men's Track & Field Championship (The First Championship, 2020). By this time, many colleges had been sponsoring track and field programs, with the AAU championship serving as the de facto national championship for college athletes (Tricard, 1996).

The height of competition between college programs and non-collegiate programs was during World War II when many colleges put athletic competition on hold (Klien, 2021). As a result, many of the top collegiate football programs filled their schedules with competitions against military service football teams comprised of service men training for the war. This allowed the Iowa Pre-Flight Seahawks, a military team, to finish second in the 1943 AP College Football poll behind first place Notre Dame and ahead of third place Michigan (Welper, 2020).

The post-war period saw a significant increase in college enrollment, propelling the growth of college sports (Snyder, 1993). This expansion coincided with the NCAA asserting more control over collegiate competition, ending their association with the AAU, implementing the "sanity code", and adopting a professionalized staff and headquarters. This allowed for a significant decline in the competition between collegiate programs and non-collegiate programs, particularly within team competitions (Crowley, n.d.).

To this day, NCAA programs remain permitted to compete against non-collegiate teams. It is for this reason new and emerging sports are possible. When the University of Minnesota-Morris launched the first ever women's collegiate wrestling program the only way to compete was by entering Canadian and USA Wrestling events (Abbott, 2020). This flexibility with competition and scheduling was necessary for subsequent program growth within women's wrestling (Wilder, 2017).

In many sports, especially individual sports, the rules for collegiate competitions are the same as those for other organizations, leading to no distinction between the competitions. Nine NCAA sports even use another organization's rulebook instead of producing their own, such as NCAA rifle following USA Shooting's rules and NCAA skiing adhering to the International Ski Federation's rules (What is a playing rule?, n.d.). As a result, there is minimal difference between intercollegiate and open competitions. Athletes in these sports produce the same times or scores irrespective of the competition being an open event or an intercollegiate event. By competing within open competitions, collegiate programs can enter more local events minimizing the expense of operating a program while still providing athletic opportunities to athletes.

Though this blending of collegiate and non-collegiate competitors raised regulatory concerns for the NCAA, including questions about the eligibility of student-athletes. The NCAA prohibits athletes, during the season, from competing on teams outside of their institution, except when participating for an Olympic or National Team (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2021d). However, athletes engaging in personal activities has been viewed differently than engaging in team competition. The wide range of levels of individual competition in open events, from local charity fun-runs to events featuring Olympians, made it challenging to create consistent standards for member institutions and student-athletes. To minimize the regulatory burden of reviewing each possible event a student-athlete may want to enter and because individual athlete participation in open events was not considered an impact on collegiate competition, the NCAA took a hands-off approach and implemented the unattached individual athlete provision (codified in rule 17.33.1.8)

    17.33.1.8 Competition as Individual/Not Representing Institution. - It is permissible for a student-athlete to participate in outside competition as an individual during the academic year in the student-athlete's sport, as long as the student-athlete represents only the student-athlete in the competition and does not engage in such competition as a member of or receive expenses from an outside team.

In collegiate wrestling, the use of unattached athletes is more prevalent than in any other sport, despite a high attrition rate of athletes not completing their athletic eligibility (Levendusky, 2021). This practice has a significant impact on student-athletes. Not only do student-athletes have to bear the expenses themselves, but they are also not allowed to receive coaching from their college coaches, and open tournament structures require a considerable time commitment (Helfrich, 2017).

Financial concerns are a significant stressor for student-athletes, ranking third according to an NCAA survey (NCAA Research, 2022). This burden is felt even more acutely by first-generation students, who report nearly double the stress levels regarding their ability to pay for their education compared to their peers (Soria et al., 2020). Furthermore, a study on collegiate financial wellness found that first-generation students are less equipped to manage their finances (Study on Collegiate Financial Wellness, 2020).

The financial burden of participating in wrestling can be substantial due to the limited scholarship support for non-headcount sports (Kruhm, 2021). Despite this, there is a widespread expectation that athletes will compete unattached, which one head coach estimated would cost an athlete an extra $900 per season (Helfrich, 2017).

Unattached competition in collegiate wrestling not only places a financial burden on athletes but also extends to their time commitment. Due to the structure of unattached competitions, athletes only compete in tournament events, which can lead to prolonged competition days, further exacerbated by the need for carpooling and waiting for their group's final athlete to finish before leaving. Even though most competitions on intercollegiate schedules are single dual meets, wrestling has the second-highest average time commitment per competition day among all NCAA sports. This time burden is particularly onerous for unattached athletes, who are subjected to an even greater time commitment than the average collegiate wrestler.

Athletic activity can negatively impact academic performance as athletes may spend more time on their sport than they realize. According to Paskus (2008), athletes may experience a decrease in the time they dedicate to their academics when they increase their athletic activity. In addition, athletes may underestimate the amount of time they allocate to their sport during the season. For example, a study conducted by Ayers et al. (2012) found that while athletes reported spending an average of 21.5 hours a week on their sport in-season, their actual time spent on the sport was 31.14 hours per week.

Unattached competition also presents challenges for athletes, beyond just the financial expenses. Coaches are not allowed to provide coaching to athletes competing unattached, and the competition schedule is often drastically different from the program's posted schedule. This lack of support can put unattached athletes at a disadvantage in their training cycles, as their training may not be optimized for their competition. This lack of focus from the coaching staff can contribute to the high attrition rate and exacerbate the pressures of adjusting to college life (Amorose & Horn, 2001).

Moreover, the unattached athlete provision in collegiate wrestling is no longer relevant in the current framework. The original idea behind this provision was that open competitors, even if they were collegiate athletes competing individually, would not have any impact on the collegiate season and therefore did not require regulation from the association. This notion has since become obsolete.

Despite the perception that unattached competition is solely developmental and has little impact on an individual's or team's collegiate career, since 2006 an unattached match may count retroactively if a redshirt is pulled (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2006). Thus, each unattached competition could have the same significance as an official collegiate competition. Consequently, athletes and coaches are aware of this, making unattached competition, in effect, no longer a developmental exhibition but an actual collegiate competition, further burdening unattached athletes.

Unattached athletes compete directly with collegiate athletes and can influence the outcome of matches and national tournament qualifications. The Division I qualification system considers unattached competitors as intercollegiate competition, and the results against these competitors are included in the calculation of RPIs. This factor affects not only the athletes who enter open competitions but also their opponents who do not compete in any open competitions throughout the season. The inconsistent results from unattached competitors, caused by the lack of support, raise concerns about the reliability of win percentages and RPIs for qualification purposes (National Collegiate Athletic Association, n.d. -am).

The challenges faced by unattached athletes are well-known within the sport, with coaches frequently discussing the issues surrounding their participation. However, despite these challenges, coaches appear unwilling to encourage the burning of a season of eligibility to gain the full benefits of collegiate athletic participation (Hamilton, 2015). It has become standard practice to expect student-athletes to endure these burdens to benefit the program should the student-athlete remain a member of the program in five-years time.

Despite open (unattached) competition being viewed as competition outside the NCAA's purview, NCAA wrestling has increasingly adopted an authoritative position over rostered athletes competing as individuals, even when unattached. The sport has dictated provisions around weight classes, restricted the timing of competitions (17.33.1.8.1), and even brought unattached competition under the NCAA's purview for the purpose of enforcing administrative violations upon wrestlers (Wrestling Rule 8.4.). This has increased the administrative burden required to operate a wrestling program.

Unlike other NCAA individual sports which follow the rules and recommendations of a national sport body, wrestling has its own rulesbook and rules committee, which changes the paradigm of open competition for wrestling. The distinction between open and intercollegiate events may seem subtle, but it is crucial. Within wrestling, only events specifically designed for intercollegiate competition use NCAA rules. If an event fails to adhere to these standards, it becomes an exhibition for collegiate athletes (Barbee, 2020).

The primary purpose of open competitions in wrestling is to permit rostered collegiate athletes to compete in college competitions while maintaining their eligibility. Permitting non-rostered open competitors in these events is largely only done to remain in compliance.

The National Association of Intercollegiate Athletics (NAIA) identified wrestling's widespread use of the provision was perverting the rule's intent and after some attempts to regulate the provision concluded the administrative burden was too great. They responded by removing the unattached athlete provision from their rules book in 2021 (NAIA's Interpretation Library, n.d.).

It's likely that the Blue Ribbon Task force understands that any academic issues within the sport are the result of the unattached competition provision. This would explain the task force only recommending the Division I season be moved despite all divisions going through the same academic acclimation period and weight certification process. The only significant differences between the divisions is that Division I wrestlers are significantly more likely to redshirt and compete unattached their first season on campus (Hamilton, 2017).

This analysis identified deficiencies in the hypothesis that shifting the NCAA Wrestling Championships to April, or later, would enhance consumer demand and interest in the NCAA Wrestling Championships and/or regular season. While it is true April hosts fewer NCAA Championships compared to March, simply moving the championships to April (or later) does not guarantee an increase in consumption of NCAA Wrestling.

In order to maintain the strong interest in the NCAA Individual Wrestling Championships within the expanded structure of a team and individual championship, it is crucial to ensure that the quality of the venue and the competition structure remains enjoyable for what this report identifies as sport loyalists who have been the core regular attendees of the Championship event for years.

If the championships were moved to April (or even May), it would likely result in a decrease in the demand for bids to host the championship event, directly impacting the event's profitability.

The NCAA Wrestling Championship requires high-quality arenas capable of accommodating 15,500 spectators per session, equipped with LED video boards, ribbon boards, media production studios, professionally equipped video control rooms, green rooms, meeting rooms, as well as full-time staffing for all in-house production staff, located within a city with enough hotel rooms to accomodate the influx of attendees (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2019a). Most of the venues capable of meeting these standards are professional sports arenas with full-time NBA and/or NHL tenants.

The duration of the NCAA Individual Wrestling Championships requires the host venue to be allocated for nearly a full week (Monday to Sunday). Thus if the NCAA Wrestling Championships are moved to April, it will be nearly impossible for venues with professional tenants to bid on hosting the Championships as the event's dates are in direct competition with the NBA and NHL playoffs (National Basketball Association, 2023; National Hockey League, 2023). These venues must reserve these playoff dates for their full-time tenants, ensuring facility availability should their primary tenant(s) make the playoffs.

This reduction in the number of prospective venues will significantly reduce the number of competitive bids recieved by the Association. Therefore, moving the Championships to April or May would likely result in cities like Des Moines (2013 host), Albany (2002), or Omaha (2010) winning the rights to host the Championships, after cities like Philadelphia (2011), New York (2016), Cleveland (2018), Detroit (2022), and Saint Louis (2021) decline to bid on the Championships.

Not only would the net impact likely lead to a reduction in the number of possible attendees due to smaller venues in less desirable locales, but it would also increase expenses for flights and hotels for the association as well as fans adding to a decrease in overall local championship amenities.

The impact of such a change is particularly concerning due to the NCAA Wrestling Committee recently reviewing increasing the minimum financial guarantee necessary for hosting the NCAA Individual Championships from $2.5 Million to $3 Million (National Collegiate Athletic Association, 2023a). These higher financial guarantees may prove too burdensome for the remaining prospective host municipalities, resulting in a reduction in the financial guarantees for the championships overall.

A reduction in financial guarantees would directly impact the Association's investment in the NCAA Wrestling Championship(s) and subsequently lead to a decline in the quality of the event for all stakeholders.

While there has been considerable discussion about the NCAA Wrestling Championships competing with the NCAA Men's Basketball Championships for viewers, it's important to recognize that the consumption of intercollegiate sports is driven primarily as an entertainment and leisure activity. As a result, competition is not limited to NCAA Championships or or even sports in general. Competition may come from any alternative entertainment or leisure activity individuals find more preferable, in the moment, than consuming NCAA Championship Wrestling.

This means that while moving the Wrestling Championships would avoid the competition with NCAA Men's Basketball Championships, new competition would arise from alternative sports programming such as the NBA playoffs, NHL playoffs, and MLB regular season. Additionally, the most significant competition is likely to come from outdoor activities, spring high school or youth sports, or simply nicer weather and longer days.

In March, the prime television-watching season comes to an end, and viewership declines significantly during the spring and summer months (Krantz-Kent, 2018). Research by Barnett et al. (1991) has shown that modeling based on three non-programing based variables, which includes seasonality, can model television viewership with 99% accuracy. If the NCAA Wrestling Championships were moved from March to April, it would shift the event from a period when many people are used to watching televised sports to a time when very few people intend to watch TV.

This change is due primarily to the transition in seasons, which leads to more sunshine (longer days) and increased average temperatures. As temperatures rise and days become longer, following the cold and dark winter, television viewership declines at a predictable and measurable rate (Eisinga, Franses, & Vergeer, 2011). The improved weather provides new opportunities for engaging in alternative entertainment activities that feel more novel following the winter months and are valued more highly than watching television at this time of the year.

Moreover, people are not only less likely to watch television in April and May but also less likely to be aware of televised events. This is partly due to reduced consumption of television, which provides information on future programming, and would likely be exasperated within wrestling's consumers due to the lack of continuity of the various wrestling competition seasons.

Aligning the intercollegiate wrestling season with the youth and high school seasons is crucial for fostering a culture of wrestling consumption. During this time of year, the wrestling community spends more time together, thinking about the sport, and engaging in natural discussions about the sport, including the programming and results of the NCAA Championships.

Shifting the intercollegiate seasons and championships to a different time of year, when the majority of high school and wrestling stakeholders have moved on to other activities, would diminish the sport's connection with its target consumers (high school and youth wrestlers, coaches, and their parents). The sport specialization rate within wrestling (~30%) is lower than the typical high school sport specialization rate (Howard, 2016). Even at the high school level, where athletes are most likely to specialize compared to the youth level, there is a high probability that the target consumers will have moved on to other activities and will be spending time outside of the wrestling community.

As an example, the Ohio High School Athletic Association's (OHSAA) state wrestling championships, which conclude in mid-March, mark the last high school wrestling championship of the year (Ohio High School Athletic Association, 2023). By moving the NCAA Wrestling Championships to April, every single high school State Championship will have been completed for at least a month, with nearly 50% of high school State Championships concluding over two months prior (Abbott, 2022).

Considering that the high school wrestling postseason often runs for a month and the majority of high school (and youth) athletes do not compete in state championships, most of the targeted prospective youth and high school wrestling consumers will have ended their wrestling season three months prior. Moving the Championships away from the high school competition season would reduce the influence that key wrestling stakeholders, such as wrestling coaches and state qualifier athletes, have on other prospective wrestling consumers.

In April, the wrestling community would be less concentrated as the majority of those involved in high school and youth wrestling take time off from the sport and move on to other communities. One of the most common communities they transition to is spring sports, with Little League baseball being a prominent example, running from March to May (Coatess, 2023). Consequently, the social influence within the wrestling community, which can serve as a strong motivation for casual sports consumption, would be greatly diminished as the influence of the Little League baseball communities becoming stronger for these individuals.

The high school athletes who move on to spring sports, such as baseball, would already be a month into their baseball games when the NCAA Championships are held. Wrestling wouldn't only be competing for attention but actually be in direct competition with actual scheduled athletic events for the prospective consumers.

It's essential to note that the improved weather does not only result in a change to the athletic communities people may be within but also result in a change to their social behaviors (Essa et al., 1990). The transition in weather and longer days leads to a change in behaviors, resulting in reduced interest in watching television or attending indoor events (Eisinga, Franses, & Vergeer, 2011l Vachon, 1983).

The likelihood of individuals being more engaged with the sport due to the college season no longer facing competition from the youth and high school season is not great. This is already evident within USA Wrestling's senior level competitions, which feature the biggest names in the sport. While these events may not face the competition often cited as a barrier for the consumption of the NCAA Wrestling Championships, consumption remains weak, with wrestling loyalists comprising the base.

Consumers are going to be less likely to choose wrestling as an entertainment activity as the event will be taking place indoors at a time when consumers are eager to be able to enjoy outdoor activities.

It is worth noting that the Big Ten Network's (BTN) support for shifting the wrestling programming should not be surprising. BTN has limited programing options available in the spring due, in large part, to the majority of the conference's spring inventory of athletic competitions being baseball and softball games (Big Ten, n.a.). These contest, which start six weeks prior to Major League Baseball's opening day (Major League Baseball, 2023), requires the Big Ten's teams to travel to warmer climates for away games throughout February and March in order to be able to play games as there is often snow on the ground still. This results in the conference not being able to capture the media rights for these contests as not being the host (sponsoring) school.

For instance, Michigan didn't play their first game in the north until their 15th game of the season, following trips to Arizona, California, and Texas (Michigan Athletics, 2023). Even after that single home game, they traveled back south for another four games in Georgia. Leaving only one out of the first twenty games of the season being available for the Big Ten Network to broadcast.

As a result, the network is left with minimal programing options during the spring (Big Ten, n.a. -a). Shifting the wrestling schedule would help load balance the BTN's programing, providing more original content and reducing their reliance on reruns of previous broadcasts (Big Ten Network, 2023). However, this shift does not necessarily guarantee an improvement in the overall reach of wrestling for the Big Ten or other conferences.

Though it should be noted, the recent addition of warm climate schools UCLA and USC to the Big Ten Conference will increase the available Big Ten controlled spring sport programing through expanded Women's Gymnastics, Baseball, and Softball, and Beach Volleyball programming for the early spring season (Uggetti, 2022). This programing would simply be new competition for wrestling programming on the BTN if wrestling were to shift the season to April or May.

It is important to note that the location of the wrestling championships and postseason on the calendar, when properly aligned, can greatly benefit from the heightened interest in sports television consumption fueled by March Madness.

Not every viewership slot in March is filled with NCAA Men's Basketball Championship programing. The NCAA Wrestling Championships being the same month of the Men's Basketball Championship can be a significant asset to wrestling with an expanded Championship structure that features the team championships during the week and between the March Madness basketball tournament. It is possible to align wrestling's championship schedule to benefit from the increased sports viewership that consumers plan for due to March Madness.

Harold Hotelling's (1929) pioneering research on spatial competition and location theory has demonstrated the benefits of clustering competing firms, whereby competitors positioning themselves next to one another improves their ability to capture select customers.

This phenomenon can be observed in car dealerships, mattress stores, gas stations, restaurants and many other industries, where firms in direct competition with each-other will cluster together (Joffe-Walt & Davidson, 2009).

These same benefits apply to media consumption, where being positioned on select distribution networks and/or being positioned alongside high-demand programming can yield better performance for a media property (Ryu et al., 2019). The increasing success of the NCAA Women's Basketball Championships serves as a prime example (Weiner, 2023). The Women's basketball championships have benefited from being positioned alongside the Men's Championship in March, leading to greater consumption of the women's championship than would be experienced if the event was scheduled on its own in April.

It's true that wrestling's currently scheduled individual championship date is not optimal. (See calendar below.) Currently, the championship sits in direct competition with the NCAA Men's Basketball Championship's busiest days of competition. Moving away from this saturated date on the calendar is the correct decision, based on the views of this author as well as those on the Blue Ribbon Task Force. However, there is disagreement regarding the magnitude of the move.

In addition to the Wrestling Championships being better positioned as one of the limited number of live sporting events on each of the dates of the event, the Team Wrestling Championships will also be held on a date when there are no NCAA Men's Basketball games due to the postseason structure of men's basketball. The team championship finals will be held the day following the announcement of the Men's Basketball Championship field which means teams will be traveling to their postseason events. It is one of the few days of the year in which no men's college basketball games are played. This will be wrestling's championship date.

These changes positions wrestling well to benefit from the increase in televised sports consumption produced by the cultural impact of March Madness.

As an example of the benefits of spatial proximity in driving sports programing viewership, we can look to the postseason college football schedule. As there are fewer games on each day, the post season continues to attract fans who have grown accustomed to watching Saturday sports through the fall. This scheduling proximity benefitted the 2022 Celebration Bowl, which drew 2.42 million viewers for the matchup between North Carolina Central University and Jackson State University (Showbuzzdaily, n.d.-a). These are programs which rarely attract rated viewership to their games.

This game would not have drawn such strong viewership if it were positioned within a regular season week with many other games going on at the same time. For comparison, in week nine of the college football season, the 7th most watched game of the week was Notre Dame vs Syracuse with 2.34 million viewers (SportsMediaWatch, n.d. -a). The matchup with North Carolina Central and Jackson State outdrew this matchup by being scheduled at a time when consumption is high but programing is low. The same structure which the Cucumber Report is proposing for wrestling.

In fact, moving games to April, where there is less competition, did not benefit the 2020 FCS Division I Football Championships. Following the Covid-19 impacted fall 2020 football season, with the support of the television networks looking to bolster spring programing, the FCS football championship was rescheduled to the spring of 2021 when there was significantly less sports programming to compete for viewersship. However, the spring Championship was only viewed by 905,000 people (Herder, 2021). A significant decline from the 2.7 million viewers from the previous year's FCS Championship, despite the previous year's championship facing competition from the NFL playoffs and college basketball.

While the significance of the game did not change, nor did the popularity of the sport, what changed was the seasonal viewership of television and the seasonal preference of consumers. Fall is largely viewed by many consumers as football season. They associate watching football with the season and the holidays serving as the time for championships. This all changed with the shifting of the FCS Division I football Championship to the spring season, and the consumers did not respond well. While shifting the championship to the spring provided more football at a time of year that football fans don't typically get to watch football. Consumers did not respond. And the same is likely true for wrestling's identity within the winter season.

For this reason, wrestling shifting to the spring is likely to experience a decline in engagement with its regular season. While there is competition with high school and youth competitors' own seasons, which can limit their ability to attend collegiate competitions, as previously mentioned, it is precisely because they are in-season and focused on the sport at that moment within the calendar year that there is interest in attending collegiate competitions in the first place. Creating a high-quality championship for wrestling is important, but as Seth Duckworth (2023) has noted, the financial success of the NCAA Wrestling Championship doesn't benefit individual programs. It is important that the structures utilized within the sport don't simply continue to focus on the Championships but work to be a holistic structure that benefits programs of disparate levels of investment and performance.

Wrestling needs to be able to measure the impact of any changes made. Simply making changes without performing a qualitative and quantitative assessment of the changes prevents the fine-tuning of the structure.

By not making a significant shift to the season, the impact on consumption and engagement for the new structure with a separate team and individual championship can be measured. This is important for any significant change.

While the Blue Ribbon Task Force and this author have independently come to the same position regarding a team dual championship, the proposed shift in the season, proposed by the Blue Ribbon Task Force, introduces unnecessary risk. It is vital that there be an evaluation and consideration period for any implemented structure. The adoption of the proposal herein should be done for a four-year period, during which there will be a review of the structure to decide if the implementation shall persist or if the stakeholders wish to revert to the previous structure.

When making a change in the season structure at the same time as the competition structure, you introduce many new variables that can impact the metrics which make it impossible to assess performance. Does viewership increase or decline due to the new format or due to the season shift? Does the season shift impact student-athletes positively or negatively, or should that be attributed to the change in the competition structure?

The above-stated considerations were among those that led to the Cucumber Report producing an alternative conclusion upon reviewing the recommendations of the Blue Ribbon Task Force.
